Overview — open the airways, treat the inflammation

A severe asthma exacerbation is simultaneous bronchospasm, airway inflammation, and mucus plugging that obstruct expiration and cause air-trapping.1,3 Treatment therefore attacks two fronts at once: rapid bronchodilation (β-agonists, ipratropium, magnesium, and in extremis epinephrine) and early anti-inflammatory steroid that takes hours to work but shortens the exacerbation and prevents relapse. The overarching goal in transport is to treat aggressively enough to avoid intubation, which is uniquely dangerous in asthma.1,3

The first-line stack and why steroids go early

Albuterol (escalating to continuous nebulization in severe disease) is the backbone bronchodilator, and ipratropium adds anticholinergic bronchodilation with additive benefit in severe exacerbations.1,3 Corticosteroids are given early even though their effect is delayed several hours, because starting the clock sooner reduces admissions and relapse — the worst time to "wait and see" on steroids is the severe attack.3 Magnesium sulfate is a smooth-muscle relaxant that provides additional bronchodilation in severe/refractory asthma and may reduce intubation, which is why it appears in the severe tier.1,3

Epinephrine and terbutaline for the crashing patient

When inhaled therapy can't reach obstructed distal airways — the patient in extremis with poor air movement — systemic (IM) epinephrine delivers bronchodilation that doesn't depend on tidal flow, and is the same reflex as treating anaphylactic bronchospasm.1 Terbutaline, a parenteral β-2 agonist given as a load plus infusion, is a further option for refractory bronchospasm; because it causes tachycardia and metabolic effects, titration of an established drip is deferred to the sending/receiving physician.1 These systemic catecholamines are the bridge that can avert intubation in the sickest patients.

Why intubating an asthmatic is so dangerous

Intubation and positive-pressure ventilation are a last resort in asthma because the obstructed lungs cannot exhale fully, so each breath stacks on the last — dynamic hyperinflation / auto-PEEP — driving up intrathoracic pressure until venous return collapses, causing hypotension, barotrauma (pneumothorax), and even arrest.2,3 The protocol's ventilator strategy is built entirely around giving the lungs time to exhale: low respiratory rate, low tidal volume, prolonged expiratory time, and tolerance of a high CO₂ (permissive hypercapnia — oxygenation, not ventilation, is the priority, and SpO₂ > 90% is acceptable).2,3 The lifesaving troubleshooting maneuver for sudden post-intubation hypotension is to disconnect the circuit and push on the chest to release trapped air, then rule out tension pneumothorax. Ketamine is the induction agent of choice for its bronchodilating properties.2

Recognizing impending failure & scoring children

The signs that an asthmatic is about to die are paradoxically quiet: a "silent chest" (no air movement to make a wheeze), a falling level of consciousness, exhaustion, and a CO₂ that is rising or "normalizing" in a previously hyperventilating patient (a sign of fatigue, not improvement).1,2 These markers — not the loudness of wheezing — drive the decision to escalate to systemic catecholamines and prepare for a difficult intubation. In children, where assessment is harder, the PRAM score standardizes severity using retractions, scalene use, air entry, wheeze, and oxygen saturation, guiding the same escalation logic.4
