MVC with Extrication

Entrapment · crush · release-decompensation
TS-TR-011 · Sec 9 Trauma
Scene & coordination
  • Scene safety: traffic, fuel/fire, downed lines, unstable vehicle, undeployed airbags, EV/hybrid high-voltage battery. PPE; stabilize the vehicle before entry.
  • Work with rescue — share the clinical picture so extrication speed matches patient stability (rapid vs controlled).
  • High-energy MOI red flags: intrusion, ejection, death in same vehicle, prolonged entrapment, rollover, ped/motorcycle.
Care during entrapment (what you can reach)
  • Control catastrophic hemorrhage first — tourniquet / wound packing on accessible bleeding (record time).
  • Manual C-spine / SMR; airway + high-flow O₂; assist ventilation PRN.
  • IV/IO access early (before release if entrapped); keep warm (prevent the lethal triad).
  • Pelvic binder for suspected pelvic fracture; splint obvious long-bone fractures.
  • Analgesia (TS-AS-002 — IV acetaminophen first, then ketamine/opioid); ketamine useful for the entrapped, painful patient.
Crush / prolonged entrapment (> ~1 h)
  • Limb/torso compressed > ~1 h → anticipate crush syndrome (TS-TR-005): start IV fluids BEFORE release (NS 0.9%); monitor ECG for hyperkalemia.
  • Anticipate sudden decompensation at the moment of release (reperfusion K⁺ surge, loss of tamponade) — have blood, airway, calcium, and defibrillator ready.
Resuscitation
  • Blood products preferred (TS-HD-002); permissive hypotension SBP ~90 (100–110 if TBI).
  • TXA < 3 h — ≥ 12 yr 2 g IV over 10 min; < 12 yr 15 mg/kg (max 1 g).
  • Reassess after extrication — injuries/bleeding declare once the patient is freed and fully exposed.
Transport
  • Highest-level trauma center; pre-notify (long entrapment / high MOI). Renal/critical-care capability if crush suspected.
  • From the Keys, rotor-wing removes 3+ h of ground time for a high-MOI patient.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • MOI (speed/intrusion/ejection/rollover), entrapment time, body region compressed, time of release.
  • Hemorrhage control (tourniquet time), SMR, access, fluids/blood/TXA, pelvic binder, analgesia.
  • ECG/hyperkalemia watch; release-decompensation events; trauma-center destination + pre-notification.
Overview — two clocks running at once

A motor-vehicle crash with entrapment is a standard high-energy trauma layered on a prolonged, hazardous scene. Two clocks run simultaneously: the patient's physiologic clock (hemorrhage, hypothermia, crush) and the extrication clock controlled by the rescue team. The flight crew's job is to deliver full trauma care in place while it cannot move the patient, coordinate the speed of extrication to the patient's stability, and anticipate the dangerous moment of release.1,2 Everything builds on the core trauma assessment and hemorrhage-control ladder (TS-TR-001) and damage-control resuscitation (TS-HD-002).

Scene hazards & crew safety

An entrapment scene is dangerous to responders: moving traffic, fuel and fire risk, unstable vehicles, sharp metal, undeployed airbags that can fire during extrication, and — increasingly — high-voltage EV/hybrid battery systems that pose electrocution and thermal-runaway (fire) hazards.1 The crew wears appropriate PPE, ensures the vehicle is stabilized and powered down before entering, and stays clear of airbag and cutting zones. A wounded provider helps no one — clinical care begins only once the working space is made safe by the rescue team.

Matching extrication speed to physiology

Modern extrication doctrine balances spinal protection against time: an unstable, bleeding, or deteriorating patient warrants a rapid (emergency) extrication accepting some spinal-movement risk, whereas a stable patient permits controlled, minimal-movement extrication.2 Recent consensus also favors self-extrication and minimal handling where the patient is stable and able, since prolonged, elaborate disentanglement causes its own movement and delay.2 The decisive input is the clinical picture the crew communicates to rescue — "this patient is crashing, we need them out now" versus "we have time, keep the spine still." That shared situational awareness is the heart of MVC-with-extrication care.

Crush syndrome & the release moment

When a limb or the torso is compressed for roughly an hour or more, the crew must anticipate crush syndrome (TS-TR-005): on release, a bolus of potassium, myoglobin, and acid washes into the circulation, risking hyperkalemic arrest and myoglobinuric kidney injury.3 The key, counter-intuitive action is to start aggressive IV fluids (normal saline) BEFORE the compressing force is removed, dilute the potassium surge and protect the kidneys, with the ECG monitored for hyperkalemia and calcium/defibrillator ready. More broadly, the moment of extrication is a recognized decompensation point for any entrapped trauma patient — release can remove a tamponading force and unmask hemorrhage, and the change in position can drop blood pressure — so the crew has blood, airway equipment, and resuscitation drugs staged and ready before the patient comes free.3

Hypothermia, hemorrhage & disposition

Entrapped patients are often exposed to the elements for a long time and are being resuscitated — both drive hypothermia, one corner of the lethal triad that worsens the coagulopathy of the very hemorrhage being treated, so active warming starts during entrapment.1,3 Hemorrhage control proceeds with whatever is reachable (tourniquet, packing, pelvic binder), access is obtained early, and damage-control resuscitation (blood, permissive hypotension, TXA within 3 hours) is delivered.1,3 High-energy mechanism and long extrication are themselves trauma-team-activation criteria, so the patient goes to the highest-level trauma center (with renal/critical-care capability if crush is suspected); from the Keys, rotor-wing transport collapses the multi-hour ground option and is frequently decisive for these high-acuity patients.1
