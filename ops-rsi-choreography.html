Two-Person RSI Choreography

RN + Medic · P1 airway / P2 resus
TS-OPS-003 · Sec 4 Operations & Transport
Assign & brief first
  • P1 = Airway/Procedures lead (head of bed, intubates, calls the plan). P2 = Resus (access, drugs, monitor, times). Verbalize the plan + the failed-airway plan (cric: who cuts, who bags).
  • Resuscitate BEFORE you intubate — fix hypotension/hypoxia first; induction can arrest a shocked patient (TS-AW-001).
① Resuscitate & pre-oxygenate
Optimize the patient
P1 Pre-oxygenate (NRB + nasal cannula apneic O₂); position (ear-to-sternal-notch / ramp); assess for difficult airway (LEMON).
P2 Two patent IV/IO; treat hypotension (push-dose epi/phenylephrine, blood — TS-HD-001/002); SpO₂/ETCO₂/NIBP/ECG on.
② Prepare — gear, drugs, plan
Set up (out loud)
P1 Check laryngoscope/video, 2 tube sizes + stylet, bougie, suction (on, at HOB), BVM + PEEP, supraglottic + cric kit ready.
P2 Draw & label: induction + paralytic + post-intubation sedation/analgesia ready before the push; vent set; pressor/blood at hand.
③ Push & intubate (closed-loop)
On P1's command
P1 "Push induction, then paralytic." Laryngoscopy at apnea/relaxation; intubate; inflate cuff. Verbalize view/attempt #.
P2 Induction: Ketamine 1–2 mg/kg (0.5–1 if shock) → Rocuronium 1–1.2 mg/kg. Read back each; watch SpO₂, call "90%/85%" aloud; ready BVM rescue.
First-pass focus. Desat → stop, BVM to recover, reattempt or escalate. ≤ 2–3 attempts → supraglottic → surgical cric.
④ Confirm & secure
Confirm placement
P1 Waveform EtCO₂ (gold standard) + bilateral sounds + chest rise; note depth; secure tube.
P2 Connect vent (lung-protective); confirm EtCO₂ trace; recheck BP (anticipate post-intubation hypotension → fluid/pressor).
⑤ Post-intubation — NEVER paralyzed & aware
Sedate / analgese immediately
P1 Confirm sedation running before paralysis wears off; ongoing airway/vent watch; ETT depth secure for transport.
P2 Start analgesia + sedation (TS-AS-004): Fentanyl + Ketamine/Propofol/Midazolam; target RASS −1 to −5; document drugs/times.
Rocuronium outlasts a single induction dose — sedation must be running before the patient could be awake. Re-dose paralytic only with deep sedation.
Documentation
  • Indication, pre-ox/resus, drugs (dose/time), attempts/grade, EtCO₂ confirmation, ETT size/depth.
  • Post-intubation sedation, vent settings, BP response; rescue steps if used.
Overview — the highest-risk thing two people do

Prehospital RSI is the procedure with the least margin for error and the most simultaneous tasks, which makes it the prime candidate for a scripted two-person choreography. By pre-assigning P1 (airway/procedures) and P2 (resuscitation/drugs), the crew converts a chaotic flurry into a sequenced drill where the airway is never unwatched and no drug is pushed without a read-back.1,2 The doses live in the airway protocol (TS-AW-001); this card is the teamwork layer that makes them safe with only two providers.

Resuscitate before you intubate

The most lethal RSI error is inducing a hypotensive or hypoxic patient: induction agents blunt sympathetic tone and positive-pressure ventilation drops preload, so a marginal patient can arrest on the push.1,3 The choreography therefore makes P2's first job optimizing physiology — two lines, correcting blood pressure (push-dose pressor/blood) and pre-oxygenation/apneic oxygenation — before any drug is drawn into the patient. Ketamine is dose-reduced in shock, and a pressor/blood is staged for the predictable post-intubation hypotension.1,3

Closed-loop drug administration & first-pass success

Every drug in the sequence is delivered by closed loop — P1 names the drug and dose, P2 reads it back and confirms when in — which is the proven defense against the dose and wrong-drug errors that cluster around RSI.2,4 P2 also serves as the dedicated monitor, calling the saturation aloud so P1 isn't distracted from the larynx, and standing ready to bag-rescue. The whole drill is organized around first-pass success: optimal positioning, the best device, a bougie, and a hard limit on attempts before escalating to a supraglottic airway and then a surgical cric — predeclared so the failed-airway plan is executed, not invented.1,2

Confirmation & the cardinal post-intubation rule

Placement is confirmed with waveform capnography (the gold standard) plus auscultation and chest rise, and the tube depth is secured for the vibration of transport.1 The single most important post-intubation safety rule the choreography enforces is that analgesia and sedation are running before the paralytic could wear off — rocuronium outlasts one dose of induction agent, and an awake-but-paralyzed patient is a catastrophic, preventable harm. P2 starts the post-intubation analgosedation regimen (TS-AS-004) immediately and documents it, while P1 keeps the airway and ventilator under continuous watch. Anticipating and treating post-intubation hypotension closes the loop.1,3
