Glycemic Emergencies

Hypoglycemia · DKA · HHS
TS-MED-006 · Sec 10 Medical
Hypoglycemia — treat straight away
  • BG < 60 mg/dL (adult/peds) or < 50 (neonate), or neuro deficit/diaphoresis/pallor.
Dextrose
Adult
10–25 g IV over 5 min, repeat PRN (see concentration guide).
Pediatric
0.5–1 g/kg IBW IV over 5 min (max 25 g), repeat PRN.
Concentration guide
D50 adult & 8–17 yr · D25 ≥ 1 yr · D10 all incl. neonate. (Use lower concentration in younger children/neonates.)
No access / no dextrose → Glucagon
Adult 1 mg IM · Peds ≥ 20 kg 1 mg IM / < 20 kg 0.5 mg IM; repeat in 10 min PRN.
After correction
  • Recheck glucose q15 min until 4 consecutive normal results; feed if able; watch for recurrence (esp. sulfonylureas, long-acting insulin).
DKA / HHS — medical control directs
  • Sending/receiving physician (or PHI MD) is medical control — clarify fluids, electrolytes, insulin BEFORE transport.
  • NS for initial resuscitation & CV instability. Insulin only after K⁺ ≥ 3.3 (or per physician); verify type/dose/route.
  • BG q15 min on an insulin drip; if it falls > 100 mg/dL/hr, stop the drip & recheck. Hourly neuro exams.
Intubating the acidotic patient — caution
  • Match the patient's high minute ventilation pre/peri/post intubation — losing their respiratory compensation worsens acidosis and can cause arrest.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Serial glucose + times; dextrose/glucagon dose & response; suspected cause/agent.
  • DKA/HHS: physician orders (fluids/insulin/K), drip rate, q15 glucose, neuro exams.
  • Ventilation strategy if intubated; destination.
Overview — one too low, two too high

This protocol covers the three glucose emergencies: hypoglycemia (the fastest reversible cause of altered mental status and a true "can't-miss"), and the two hyperglycemic crises — diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) and hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (HHS).1,2 Hypoglycemia is fixed in seconds at the bedside; DKA/HHS are slow, complex derangements of fluid, electrolytes, and acid-base that are managed collaboratively under physician control during transport rather than by standing order.

Hypoglycemia — fast, but match the sugar to the patient

Because the brain runs almost exclusively on glucose, hypoglycemia rapidly causes confusion, seizures, focal deficits (a classic stroke mimic), and death — so any altered patient gets a glucose check and is treated immediately if low.1,2 The dextrose concentration guide matters: concentrated D50 is appropriate for adults but is hyperosmolar and vein/tissue-toxic in small children and neonates, so progressively more dilute solutions (D25, D10) are used as age decreases, with D10 safe for all including neonates.1 When there is no IV access, IM glucagon mobilizes hepatic glycogen — but it works slowly and fails in glycogen-depleted patients (alcoholics, malnourished, prolonged fasting), so it is a bridge to dextrose, not a substitute.1 Critically, hypoglycemia from long-acting insulin or sulfonylureas recurs, so glucose is rechecked until four consecutive normal values and the patient is fed and observed.1

DKA/HHS — why this is a physician-directed transport

DKA and HHS involve profound dehydration (often 5–10 L deficits), total-body potassium depletion, and, in DKA, an anion-gap metabolic acidosis from ketones.2 The protocol makes these medical-control-directed because the management has dangerous nuances best decided with a physician: fluids first (normal saline for initial resuscitation), and insulin only after potassium is confirmed adequate. The clarifications required before transport — fluids, electrolytes, insulin type/dose — exist precisely so the crew is not improvising a complex ICU-level regimen in the aircraft.1,2

The potassium and "drop-rate" rules

Two specific safety rules anchor DKA management. First, insulin is withheld until serum K⁺ is ≥ 3.3 mEq/L (or the physician's threshold): although the measured potassium is often high initially, total-body potassium is depleted, and insulin drives K⁺ into cells — giving insulin to a hypokalemic patient can precipitate fatal arrhythmia.2 Second, glucose is checked every 15 minutes on an insulin drip, and if it falls faster than ~100 mg/dL/hr the drip is stopped — too-rapid correction of glucose (and osmolality) risks cerebral edema, which is especially feared in children.2 Hourly neurologic exams watch for that complication.

The intubation trap in metabolic acidosis

The most dangerous moment in a severely acidotic DKA patient is intubation. These patients survive by breathing fast and deep (Kussmaul respirations) to blow off CO₂ and compensate for their acidosis; their minute ventilation may be two to three times normal.2 If the crew sedates/paralyzes them and then under-ventilates (a "normal" rate of 12–16), the pH crashes and the patient can arrest on the table. The protocol therefore demands matching the patient's high minute ventilation before, during, and after intubation — a reason many experienced clinicians try hard to avoid intubating DKA at all and instead treat the underlying acidosis aggressively.2
