Trauma Assessment & Hemorrhage Control

ABCDE · MARCH · bleeding control
TS-TR-001 · Sec 9 Trauma
Primary survey — life threats first
  • Control catastrophic external hemorrhage immediately as you find it (before/with airway).
  • Airway (+ C-spine) · Breathing · Circulation · Disability · Exposure / temp.
  • Confirm any advanced airway (placement/position/function); BVM if RR < 10 or > 30 or ineffective.
Oxygen — empiric for trauma
  • Adult & peds: FiO₂ 100% / 15–25 L/min. Acutely/critically injured → high-flow; avoid down-titrating FiO₂ (blood loss = reduced O₂ carrying).
Chest — open & tension pneumothorax
  • Open chest wound: seal with a vented chest seal (Asherman preferred), or 3-sided occlusive dressing; gloved palm as a temporary measure.
  • Tension (esp. ventilated): falling SpO₂ + hypotension + high vent pressures + ↓ lung sounds → needle / simple thoracostomy — low threshold in ventilated trauma.
Hemorrhage control — in order
  • Direct pressure with gauze (hemostatic gauze if available).
  • Wound packing for deep extremity / junctional wounds — pack tightly, hold pressure.
  • Pressure bandage (SWAT-T) to maintain control.
  • Tourniquet (CAT / SOF-TT) immediately for life-threatening extremity bleeding or if pressure/packing fail.
  • Pelvic binder (SAM sling / sheet) if pelvic fracture likely; splint long bones.
  • Re-bleeding through a dressing → remove to the source, clear clot, re-control. Reassess all dressings.
Circulation / shock
  • Vascular access; resuscitate per Shock / CV-instability — favor blood products (see TS-HD-002 whole blood). Permissive hypotension (SBP ~90; SBP 100–110 if TBI).
  • TXA 2 g (initial) within 3 h for significant hemorrhage (see TS-HD-002).
Disability / exposure
  • AMS → check glucose, treat hypoglycemia. Baseline GCS, pupils & motor/sensory before any paralytic.
  • SMR for significant blunt mechanism meeting criteria; assess spine/back.
  • Expose to assess, then prevent hypothermia (warm cabin, blankets, warm fluids).
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Mechanism, time of injury; primary-survey findings + interventions and times.
  • Hemorrhage-control devices (tourniquet time!), chest seals/decompression, access, fluids/blood/TXA.
  • Serial vitals/GCS/pupils, SMR, temperature; why rotor-wing / trauma-center destination.
Overview — an organized, prioritized approach

Trauma kills in a predictable order, so the assessment is built to find and fix the fastest killers first. The ABCDE primary survey (airway with C-spine, breathing, circulation, disability, exposure) is a prioritization framework, not a rigid checklist — the crew treats each life threat as it is found rather than completing one letter before moving on.1 The single most important modern modification is that catastrophic external hemorrhage is controlled first, even before the airway, because a patient can exsanguinate from a limb in minutes; this is the logic behind the military MARCH sequence (Massive hemorrhage, Airway, Respiration, Circulation, Head/Hypothermia) that complements ABCDE in the bleeding patient.1,2

Why high-flow oxygen and why permissive hypotension coexist

These two ideas seem contradictory but address different problems. Trauma patients lose red cells and therefore oxygen-carrying capacity, so empiric high-flow oxygen (FiO₂ 100%) maximizes the oxygen carried by whatever hemoglobin remains, and down-titration is avoided in the acutely injured.5 Meanwhile, permissive hypotension (titrating to a radial pulse / SBP ~90) limits hydrostatic disruption of fresh clot and dilutional coagulopathy before surgical control.2,5 The crucial exception is traumatic brain injury, where the SBP target rises to 100–110 mmHg because the injured brain needs perfusion pressure — hypotension and hypoxia each multiply TBI mortality (see TS-TR-002).3,5 Resuscitation favors blood products over crystalloid, consistent with damage-control principles (see TS-HD-002).2

Hemorrhage control — the evidence-based ladder

Field hemorrhage control follows a stepwise ladder validated by military and civilian experience and codified by Stop the Bleed and TCCC: direct pressure → wound packing (with hemostatic gauze where available) → pressure dressing → tourniquet for life-threatening extremity bleeding.2,6 Tourniquets were once feared but battlefield data showed that early application saves lives with rare limb loss, reversing decades of teaching — so a CAT/SOF-TT is applied immediately for a clearly life-threatening extremity wound rather than as a last resort, and the application time is documented because it drives downstream limb-salvage decisions.2,6 Junctional and deep wounds are packed tightly to the bleeding vessel; a pelvic binder closes the pelvic ring volume to tamponade venous bleeding in suspected pelvic fracture, and long-bone splinting reduces ongoing blood loss and pain.1,2 Re-bleeding through a dressing is managed by going down to the source rather than piling on more gauze.

Chest decompression — recognizing tension early

Tension pneumothorax is the classic preventable trauma death and is far more common and faster in the ventilated patient, because positive pressure forces air into the pleural space.1,4 The teaching axiom — difficult ventilation and hypoxia followed by hypotension in a newly intubated trauma patient is tension until proven otherwise — drives a low threshold for needle or simple thoracostomy.4 In the ventilated patient the order of signs is rapid: falling SpO₂, falling cardiac output/hypotension, high ventilation pressures, then hyperexpansion and decreased breath sounds; awake patients present more insidiously with chest pain and tachycardia. Open ("sucking") chest wounds are sealed with a vented chest seal to restore respiratory mechanics, then watched for conversion to a tension as air accumulates — at which point the seal is burped or the chest decompressed.1,4

Disability, exposure & the lethal triad

A brief neurologic exam (GCS, pupils, gross motor/sensory) is obtained before any paralytic, because once a patient is paralyzed and sedated that baseline is lost forever and it anchors all downstream neuro decisions.1 Altered mental status prompts a glucose check, and significant blunt mechanisms meeting criteria get spinal motion restriction. Finally, exposure must be balanced against hypothermia: trauma patients are uncovered to be assessed but then actively kept warm, because hypothermia is one corner of the lethal triad (hypothermia, acidosis, coagulopathy) — a cold patient cannot clot, which worsens the very hemorrhage the crew is fighting.2,5 Warm cabin, blankets, and warmed fluids/blood are therefore part of hemorrhage control, not comfort care.
