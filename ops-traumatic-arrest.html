Traumatic Arrest Drill

Treat the cause (HOTT) · not standard ACLS
TS-OPS-005 · Sec 4 Operations & Transport
Principle — fix reversible causes FIRST
  • Traumatic arrest ≠ medical arrest. CPR & epinephrine are low-yield until the reversible cause is fixed. Address H · O · T · T in parallel within the first minute.
  • Hypovolemia · Oxygenation/airway · Tension pneumothorax · Tamponade. Hypoxia & hemorrhage cause most traumatic arrests.
  • Best survival: witnessed, short downtime, signs of life, penetrating chest. Blunt arrest with prolonged downtime/asystole = poor — consider termination per protocol/medical control.
The drill — both providers, simultaneously
First 60 seconds (HOTT)
P1 · Airway + chest Open/secure airway, oxygenate (BVM/SGA/ETT). Bilateral finger thoracostomy (decompress both sides) — TS-TR-013.
P2 · Bleeding + volume Control external hemorrhage (tourniquet/packing); pelvic binder; IV/IO ×2; start blood (TS-HD-002) + calcium + TXA.
If signs of life return → continue resuscitation; if pulse → titrate blood to radial pulse / SBP ~90 (100–110 TBI).
Tamponade (penetrating chest / box)
  • Suspect with penetrating cardiac-box wound + arrest/PEA → pericardiocentesis (TS-HD-006) if no other cause and within scope/medical control. Definitive = thoracotomy at the trauma center.
Then (after HOTT addressed)
  • High-quality chest compressions & rhythm check — but compressions are secondary to fixing the cause in trauma (poor output against an empty tank).
  • Epinephrine is de-emphasized in traumatic arrest (squeezing an empty tank) — volume/blood is the priority; give per medical control.
  • Treat hypothermia; correct hyperkalemia if crush (TS-TR-005); glucose.
Disposition
  • ROSC or signs of life → load-and-go, trauma center, MTP activated, pre-notify.
  • No response despite HOTT, prolonged downtime/asystole in blunt trauma → consider termination per protocol/medical control. EtCO₂ trend informs prognosis.
Documentation
  • Downtime, witnessed?, signs of life, mechanism; HOTT interventions + times (decompression sites, blood, hemorrhage control).
  • Rhythm, EtCO₂ trend, ROSC; medical-control contact; termination decision if applicable.
Overview — a different arrest

Traumatic cardiac arrest is mechanistically different from medical arrest: the heart usually stops because it has no blood to pump, no oxygen, or is being mechanically obstructed, not because of a primary dysrhythmia.1,2 Standard ACLS — chest compressions and epinephrine — is therefore low-yield until the reversible cause is corrected, and the entire drill reorders priorities around fixing those causes first and simultaneously. With two providers this is only possible as a rehearsed, role-split drill.2,3

HOTT — the reversible causes, in parallel

The mnemonic HOTT — Hypovolemia, Oxygenation, Tension pneumothorax, Tamponade — captures the treatable causes, and the choreography assigns them to two providers working at once.2 P1 owns oxygenation and the chest: securing the airway and performing bilateral finger thoracostomy (decompressing both sides empirically, since tension is common and you can't reliably exclude it in arrest). P2 owns hemorrhage and volume: external hemorrhage control, a pelvic binder, vascular access, and blood with calcium and TXA. Because hypoxia and hemorrhage cause most traumatic arrests, these two providers' tasks are the actual resuscitation; compressions are secondary.1,2

Why epinephrine and compressions take a back seat

In a patient who is empty and obstructed, chest compressions generate little forward flow and epinephrine squeezes an empty tank, raising afterload without addressing the cause — both are de-emphasized in traumatic arrest in favor of filling the tank (blood), opening the chest (decompression), and oxygenating.1,2 This is a deliberate departure from medical-arrest reflexes and is one of the most important mindset shifts the drill trains. Tamponade — especially after a penetrating cardiac-box wound — is addressed with pericardiocentesis as a temporizing measure (TS-HD-006) where it is the suspected cause, recognizing that definitive treatment is surgical (thoracotomy).2,3

Who survives — and when to stop

Outcomes guide effort. The best survival is in the witnessed arrest with a short downtime, retained signs of life, and a penetrating chest injury (the classic candidate for an ED thoracotomy), where aggressive HOTT and rapid transport can work.1,2 Conversely, blunt traumatic arrest with prolonged downtime and asystole has a dismal prognosis, and the drill explicitly includes termination of resuscitation per protocol and medical control as an appropriate, evidence-based decision — with the end-tidal CO₂ trend as a useful prognostic input.2 For the salvageable patient, the team load-and-goes with the trauma center's massive-transfusion protocol activated; the helicopter exists to collapse the time to a surgeon.1,2
