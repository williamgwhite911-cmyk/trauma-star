Preeclampsia / Eclampsia / HELLP

Magnesium · severe-range BP · seizure
TS-OB-005 · Sec 8 Obstetrics
Recognize severe features
  • Severe-range BP: SBP ≥ 160 or DBP ≥ 110 (2 readings in 15 min).
  • Headache, visual changes, RUQ/epigastric pain, SOB, hyperreflexia, clonus.
  • Seizure = eclampsia. HELLP = hemolysis, ↑LFTs, low platelets.
  • Reduce stimuli: hearing + vision protection, calm cabin.
Magnesium — seizure prophylaxis (preeclampsia)
Magnesium sulfate — PIH / Preeclampsia
4–6 g IV over 20–30 min loading bolus, then 2 g/hr infusion. Increase dose (via medical control) for 4+ reflexes & sustained clonus, edema to hips, headache + visual changes with ↑BP.
Severe-range BP — antihypertensive
Trigger / target
Treat 2× SBP > 160 or DBP > 110 within 15 min; begin within 10–20 min. Target SBP < 160 / DBP < 110. Max out one agent before switching.
Labetalol
20 mg IV over 2 min → 40 mg in 10–20 min → 80 mg q10–20 min to max 300 mg.
Hydralazine
10 mg IV over 2 min → repeat 10 mg at 20 min → 5 mg at 20 min. Max 30 mg. Hold if HR > 120.
Nifedipine
10 mg PO → 20 mg at 30 min → 20 mg again PRN. Max 50 mg. Recheck BP q15 min.
Eclamptic seizure
  • Airway + O₂; benzodiazepine (choose one): Lorazepam 2 mg IV/IM/NAS (repeat q5 min until controlled) or Midazolam 5 mg IV/IM/NAS.
  • Magnesium 6 g IV over 15–20 min load, then 2 g/hr; top up to a total 6 g bolus if < 6 g given PTA.
Supportive
  • Total fluids 125 mL/hr (↑ only for hypovolemia / intrauterine resuscitation — beware pulmonary edema).
  • Headache: fentanyl 0.5–1 mcg/kg IBW or acetaminophen 650–1000 mg PO.
  • Mag toxicity (DTR loss/weakness/resp depression) → calcium 0.5–1 g IV over 3 min, stop mag.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Serial BPs (2-reading triggers), severe-feature symptoms, reflexes/clonus.
  • Magnesium load + rate; antihypertensive (agent/dose/time/response/HR); seizure + benzo given.
  • Fluid totals; FHR/NICHD trend; medical-control contacts/orders; level-of-care rationale.
Overview — a multisystem disease of the placenta

Preeclampsia is a pregnancy-specific syndrome of new hypertension plus end-organ dysfunction, rooted in abnormal placentation: poorly remodeled spiral arteries lead to placental ischemia, which releases anti-angiogenic factors that injure the maternal vascular endothelium throughout the body.1,2 That systemic endothelial injury explains the whole picture — hypertension, proteinuria, cerebral edema and seizures (eclampsia), hepatic swelling with RUQ pain, and the microangiopathic HELLP syndrome (Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets). It is a leading cause of maternal death, and most fatal cases involve a preventable failure to treat severe-range blood pressure promptly.1,3 The only definitive cure is delivery, so transport exists to control BP, prevent/treat seizures, and reach a facility that can deliver.

Magnesium prevents seizures — and it is not an antihypertensive

The single most important concept is that magnesium sulfate and blood-pressure control are two separate jobs. Magnesium is the proven agent to prevent and treat eclamptic seizures: the Magpie trial showed it more than halves the risk of eclampsia, and the Collaborative Eclampsia Trial showed it is superior to both diazepam and phenytoin for treating eclamptic seizures and preventing recurrence.4,5 But magnesium does not meaningfully lower blood pressure — a patient can be fully magnesium-loaded and still stroke from uncontrolled hypertension. The protocol therefore runs both pathways in parallel: a magnesium load and infusion for seizure prophylaxis (4–6 g load then 2 g/hr in preeclampsia; 6 g load then 2 g/hr for eclampsia), and a separate antihypertensive for severe-range pressures.3,6

Why severe-range BP is a stroke emergency with a clock

The leading cause of death in preeclampsia/eclampsia is hemorrhagic stroke, driven by sustained severe-range systolic hypertension overwhelming cerebral autoregulation.3 This is why ACOG and this protocol define SBP ≥ 160 or DBP ≥ 110 (confirmed over ~15 minutes) as a hypertensive emergency that must be treated within 10–20 minutes, not observed.3,6 The target is controlled — SBP < 160 / DBP < 110 — not normalization, because an abrupt or excessive drop reduces uteroplacental perfusion and can cause fetal distress. First-line agents are labetalol (IV, avoided in asthma/bradycardia), hydralazine (IV, held for maternal HR > 120 because of reflex tachycardia), and oral nifedipine; the protocol deliberately instructs maxing one agent through its sequence before switching, both to reach target reliably and to avoid stacking effects that cause precipitous hypotension.6

Managing the eclamptic seizure in the cabin

Most eclamptic seizures are self-limited, so the immediate priorities are protecting the airway, providing oxygen, and preventing aspiration and injury — not aggressive polypharmacy.3,5 Magnesium is the definitive agent (a load plus infusion, topped up to a full 6 g if a partial dose was given before arrival), and a benzodiazepine (lorazepam or midazolam) is used to abort an active or refractory convulsion. Because the noise, vibration, and visual stimulation of a helicopter cabin can themselves precipitate seizures in a hyperexcitable preeclamptic brain, the protocol specifically calls for reducing stimuli — hearing protection, eye covering, and a calm environment — as genuine seizure prophylaxis, not a comfort measure.3

Fluids, HELLP, and magnesium safety

Fluid management is deliberately restrictive (≈ 125 mL/hr total) because the leaky endothelium of preeclampsia makes these patients highly prone to pulmonary edema; volume is given only for a clear indication.3,6 HELLP adds the danger of thrombocytopenia and hepatic injury — including spontaneous hepatic hematoma/rupture suggested by severe RUQ pain — so coagulation status is relayed and the destination must have obstetric and blood-bank capability.1,2 Finally, because magnesium is being infused, the crew watches continuously for toxicity: loss of deep-tendon reflexes precedes respiratory depression, and IV calcium is the immediate antidote alongside stopping the infusion.6 The whole protocol is a balance — enough magnesium to prevent seizures, enough antihypertensive to prevent stroke, and enough restraint with fluids and BP-lowering to protect the lungs and the fetus.3,6
