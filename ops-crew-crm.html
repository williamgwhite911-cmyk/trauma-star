Crew Roles & CRM (RN + Medic)

2-person team · role assignment · closed-loop
TS-OPS-002 · Sec 4 Operations & Transport
The two roles — assign BEFORE every call
Default split (swap each call to stay sharp)
P1 · Airway / Procedures "Head of the bed." Airway/RSI, cric, chest decompression, POCUS. Calls the plan.
P2 · Circulation / Resus Access (IV/IO/port), blood/TXA/calcium, drips & pumps, pushes meds on command, monitor, times & documents, talks to medical control.
Two-deep rule: BOTH providers must independently run an RSI, hang blood, decompress a chest, run POCUS, and manage the vent/pumps. RN or medic can be P1 or P2.
Pre-flight brief (60 seconds, every launch)
  • Roles: "You're P1 airway, I'm P2 resus." State the working diagnosis & the plan.
  • Contingencies out loud: "If the airway fails → cric, you cut, I bag." "If they code → you airway, I compressions + blood."
  • Gear staged: airway/RSI kit + suction at HOB; blood warm & checked; drugs drawn & labeled; IO ready; monitor/capnography on.
  • Go/No-Go: weight/altitude/weather; can we control this patient for flight? (agitation → TS-MED-001). Extra hands needed?
Closed-loop communication (always)
  • Order → read-back → confirmation: "Push 100 of fentanyl" → "Pushing 100 of fentanyl" → "Fentanyl in."
  • Name the receiver for every order; no "anonymous" tasks. State drug + dose + route.
  • Think out loud — shared mental model. Verbalize vitals trends & concerns.
Speak up — flattened hierarchy
  • Either provider can & must challenge. Use a graded assertion: "I'm concerned… I'm uncomfortable… this is unsafe — STOP."
  • 10-for-10: on a big decision, stop, take 10 sec, re-confirm the plan together.
  • No rank in the back — the safer plan wins, regardless of who said it.
Task-load management (only 2 hands ×2)
  • Pre-task on the ground — draw drugs, prime lines, set the vent, stage the airway before moving.
  • One problem at a time; load-and-go for time-critical surgical disease.
  • Call early — online medical control + receiving pre-notification; request a 3rd set of hands (fire/ground) when high workload is predictable (entrapment, MTP, combative).
After every significant call
  • AAR (after-action review): what went well, what to fix, one thing to drill. Restock; reset roles.
  • Flag QA/M&M items to the Medical Director.
Overview — making two providers move like four

A two-person RN + paramedic crew can deliver special-operations-grade critical care, but only by importing the discipline that lets small elite teams perform: fixed role assignment, relentless cross-training, closed-loop communication, and rehearsed drills.1,2 With only two providers there is no slack — every critical skill must live in both heads (the "two-deep" rule), and every high-acuity event must be choreographed in advance so two sets of hands accomplish what would otherwise take four.1,3

Why pre-assigned roles (the "battle drill")

The most common failure mode of a two-person resuscitation is both providers reaching for the same task while no one watches the airway or the bleeding. Assigning P1 (airway/procedures) and P2 (circulation/resuscitation) before every call — and swapping each call so both stay current — eliminates that, the same way special-operations and aviation teams run pre-briefed role drills.1,2 The roles are a default, not a cage: whoever is free takes the next emergent task, but the default prevents collisions and gaps. Crucially, the split is function-based, not title-based — the medic or the nurse can be P1 or P2, because both are trained to do everything.3

Closed-loop communication & the shared mental model

Borrowed directly from aviation crew-resource-management and surgical-team science, closed-loop communication (directed order → read-back → confirmation) is the single highest-yield habit for preventing medication and task errors in a noisy cabin.2,4 Pairing it with thinking out loud builds a shared mental model so both providers anticipate the next step rather than waiting to be told. High-reliability teams also flatten the hierarchy: any team member can stop the line, using graded-assertiveness language ("I'm concerned → I'm uncomfortable → this is unsafe, stop"), because in a two-person crew the junior provider's catch may be the only catch.2,4

Working within two-person limits

Two people physically cannot do everything at once in flight, so elite dyads win by front-loading work: drawing and labeling drugs, priming lines, setting the ventilator, warming and checking blood, and staging the airway on the ground before movement.3 They triage to one problem at a time, accept load-and-go for time-critical surgical disease, and aggressively use external resources — early online medical control, receiving-facility pre-notification, and requesting a third set of hands when a high-workload scenario (entrapment, massive transfusion, a combative patient) is predictable. This is the same scalability principle the military uses to push small surgical slices forward (see the Army's ERST concept): a small team stabilizes and hands off rather than trying to be definitive.1,5

Training, cohesion & the after-action review

Capability comes from standards and repetition, not headcount. Fixed crews trained together develop the wordless coordination characteristic of special-operations units; recurrent, no-notice simulation of the scripted drills (RSI, massive transfusion, traumatic arrest, the violent patient) keeps both providers two-deep on every skill.1,3 Every significant call ends with an after-action review — what went well, what to fix, one thing to drill next — feeding a tight QA/M&M loop with the Medical Director. The protocols, the drills, and the AAR together are what convert a nurse-and-medic into a single high-performing unit.1,2
