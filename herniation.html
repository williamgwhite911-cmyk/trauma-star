Recognize — signs of herniation
  • Breathing patterns — Cheyne-Stokes, central hyperventilation, ataxic.
  • Pupils — asymmetric dilation, fixed.
  • Motor — decerebrate (extensor) or decorticate (flexor) posturing, hemiparesis/plegia, flaccidity.
  • Cushing reflex — bradycardia + respiratory depression + hypertension.

Supportive / ICP measures
  • HOB 30°, head midline; avoid venous obstruction (tight collar/ties).
  • Optimize oxygenation/ventilation — avoid hypoxia and hypotension (secondary injury).
  • Treat per medical control; brief controlled hyperventilation only as a temporizing measure for active herniation per protocol.
  • Manage collaboratively with treating provider / PHI–style medical direction where practicable.

  • Herniation signs (breathing, pupils, motor, Cushing) with times; serial GCS/pupils.
  • Hyperosmolar agent — drug, dose, route, time; online order for mannitol.
  • HOB/positioning, ventilation parameters (EtCO₂), oxygenation.
  • Suspected cause (ICH, stroke, mass, hydrocephalus); destination (neurosurgical).

Overview — a time-critical final common pathway

Brain herniation is the mechanical displacement of brain tissue across a dural or bony boundary when intracranial pressure (ICP) rises beyond the skull's capacity to compensate. It is the final common pathway by which expanding mass lesions kill — intracranial hemorrhage, large ischemic stroke with edema, traumatic hematoma, abscess, tumor, or acute hydrocephalus.1,2 The Monro-Kellie doctrine explains why it accelerates: the rigid adult skull holds a fixed volume of brain, blood, and CSF, and once the small buffers of CSF and venous blood are exhausted, any further increase in volume produces a steep, nonlinear rise in ICP and a rapid fall in cerebral perfusion.2 Recognizing the syndrome early and lowering ICP buys minutes-to-hours of brain viability while transport to definitive neurosurgical care proceeds — herniation is treated as it is recognized, in parallel with moving the patient.1,3

Why cerebral perfusion is the real target

The brain's blood supply depends on cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP = mean arterial pressure − ICP). As ICP climbs, CPP falls unless MAP rises to compensate — which is exactly what the Cushing reflex (hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respirations) represents: a brainstem attempt to preserve perfusion against a failing pressure gradient, and an ominous late sign.2 This relationship is why two field errors are catastrophic in the herniating patient: allowing hypotension (which collapses CPP from the MAP side) and allowing hypoxia or hypercarbia (CO₂ is a potent cerebral vasodilator that raises intracranial blood volume and therefore ICP). Even a single episode of hypotension or hypoxia measurably worsens outcome in acute brain injury, so maintaining oxygenation, ventilation, and blood pressure is as important as any drug given.2,3

Recognizing herniation in the cabin

The actionable signs are a declining level of consciousness plus one or more of: a new fixed or asymmetric (blown) pupil from uncal compression of the third cranial nerve; posturing (decorticate or decerebrate); the Cushing reflex; or an abnormal/irregular respiratory pattern.1,2 Serial exams matter more than any single reading, because the trajectory (a GCS falling by 2 or more points, a pupil that was reactive becoming fixed) is what defines impending herniation and triggers hyperosmolar therapy. In the dark, vibrating, noisy aircraft these subtle changes are easy to miss, so a disciplined cadence of pupil checks and GCS scoring is part of the protocol, not optional.

Hyperosmolar therapy — how it works and which agent

Hyperosmolar agents raise serum osmolality, creating an osmotic gradient that draws water out of brain tissue across an intact blood-brain barrier, shrinking brain volume and lowering ICP. 3% sodium chloride is first-line in this protocol — adult 250 mL IV over 5–15 min, or pediatric 3–5 mL/kg ideal body weight over 5–15 min — and has the advantage of also supporting blood pressure and intravascular volume, which is valuable in a patient who must not become hypotensive.4 Mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg IV over 30–60 min is an alternative requiring an online order; it is an effective osmotic diuretic but can cause hypovolemia and hypotension through diuresis, which is a real liability in the hemodynamically marginal or hemorrhaging patient.4 Comparative trials show both lower ICP, with hypertonic saline often achieving a greater and more durable reduction; agent choice therefore weighs volume status and access against availability.4

Bundle of ICP measures & controlled ventilation
  • Elevate head ~30° and keep the head midline (a turned head or tight C-collar obstructs jugular venous drainage and raises ICP).2
  • Optimize oxygenation and target normocapnia (EtCO₂ ~35–40); brief, controlled mild hyperventilation is a temporizing rescue only for active herniation, never routine, because aggressive hypocapnia causes ischemic vasoconstriction.2,3
  • Avoid hypotension; treat pain/agitation and ventilator dyssynchrony, which spike ICP.
  • Expedite to a neurosurgical-capable center and pre-notify; definitive treatment is surgical decompression / lesion evacuation.1,3
