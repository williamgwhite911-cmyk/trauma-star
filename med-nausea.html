Nausea / Vomiting & EPS

Ondansetron first · dystonia reversal
TS-MED-011 · Sec 10 Medical
First-line — Ondansetron
Adult
4 mg IV over 2–5 min / IM or 4 mg ODT; may repeat once in 30 min PRN.
Pediatric
0.1 mg/kg IBW IV over 2–5 min (max 4 mg); repeat once in 30 min PRN.
Second-line (after ondansetron)
Prochlorperazine
Adult 5–10 mg IV over 2 min / IM (max 10 mg) · Peds 10–40 kg 2.5 mg (max 2.5). Contraindicated < 2 yr or < 10 kg.
Promethazine
Adult 12.5–25 mg diluted in 10 mL IV over 2 min or 25 mg deep IM · Peds ≥ 2 yr 0.25–0.5 mg/kg in 10 mL (max 12.5 mg); q4–6h PRN.
Promethazine — cautions
  • Boxed warning: NOT in children < 2 yr (fatal respiratory depression).
  • Severe tissue injury if extravasated — check IV patency, dilute, prefer large vein; reduce dose in elderly/sedated.
Extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS) → Diphenhydramine
Recognize (after prochlorperazine/promethazine)
Acute dystonia, tremor, akathisia (restlessness), slurred speech, anxiety/distress, oculogyric crisis.
Diphenhydramine
Adult 25–50 mg IV/IM · Peds 1–2 mg/kg IV/IM (max 50 mg); repeat q4h PRN.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Cause of nausea (consider serious mimics — MI, ↑ICP, obstruction, DKA, pregnancy).
  • Antiemetic (drug/dose/route/time/response); IV patency for promethazine.
  • Any EPS + diphenhydramine response.
Overview — comfort, safety, and a search for the cause

Nausea and vomiting are common in transport and are more than a comfort issue: vomiting in a supine, restrained, or sedated patient risks aspiration, raises intracranial and intraocular pressure, and worsens pain and fluid/electrolyte status.1 Treating it improves safety. At the same time, vomiting is a symptom, so the crew keeps the dangerous causes in mind — myocardial infarction (especially inferior), raised intracranial pressure, bowel obstruction, DKA, toxic ingestion, and pregnancy — because the antiemetic should not mask a condition that changes destination or management.1

Why ondansetron is first-line

Ondansetron, a 5-HT3 antagonist, is first-line because it is highly effective with a clean side-effect profile — it does not cause the sedation, hypotension, or extrapyramidal reactions of the older agents, making it ideal for a moving aircraft where an alert, stable patient is preferable.1,2 It is available IV, IM, and as an orally disintegrating tablet. Its main caution is mild QT prolongation, relevant mainly with very high doses or pre-existing long-QT/other QT-prolonging drugs — a consideration but rarely limiting at these doses.2

Second-line agents and the dopamine trade-off

When ondansetron is insufficient, the dopamine-antagonist antiemetics prochlorperazine and promethazine are added.1 They are effective but carry the side effects of their class: sedation, hypotension, and extrapyramidal reactions. Promethazine has two specific, serious warnings that shape its use: a boxed warning against use under age 2 (fatal respiratory depression has occurred) and a risk of severe tissue injury/necrosis with extravasation or arterial injection — which is why the protocol dilutes it in 10 mL, gives it slowly into a confirmed-patent large vein, and reduces the dose in the elderly or already-sedated.1 Prochlorperazine is likewise contraindicated in the youngest/smallest children.1

Extrapyramidal symptoms — recognize and reverse

Because the second-line antiemetics block dopamine, they can cause acute extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS) — most dramatically an acute dystonic reaction (involuntary muscle spasms of the neck, face, tongue, or eyes/oculogyric crisis) and akathisia (an intense, distressing restlessness that can be mistaken for worsening anxiety or agitation).1 These are frightening but not allergic and are rapidly reversible: diphenhydramine (an anticholinergic antihistamine) counteracts the dopamine-blockade imbalance and relieves the reaction within minutes.1 Recognizing EPS for what it is — a treatable medication effect, not a new neurologic event or an escalation of agitation — prevents both unnecessary alarm and inappropriate sedation. This is why the antiemetic and EPS protocols are paired: the antidote travels with the drug that causes the problem.
