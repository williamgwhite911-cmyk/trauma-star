Intra-Aortic Balloon Pump Transfer

IABP counterpulsation · MCS interfacility
TS-HD-004 · Sec 2 Hemodynamics & Shock
Keep it pumping — never let it sit idle
  • Never stop augmentation for more than a few minutes — a stationary balloon forms thrombus. If the console fails, switch to backup/battery; if it cannot pump, the balloon must be manually fluttered (per console) every ~5 min or removed at the sending facility.
  • Confirm trigger, timing, and augmentation are correct before leaving and again after every move (loading, lift-off).
  • Bring a fully charged console + spare battery and a full helium tank (+ backup).
How it works (set-up check)
  • Inflates in diastole (↑ coronary perfusion — augmented diastolic pressure) and deflates just before systole (↓ afterload, ↓ myocardial O₂ demand).
  • Trigger: ECG (R-wave, preferred) or arterial pressure. Ensure a clean ECG + a good arterial waveform.
  • Ratio: 1:1 (every beat) typical; 1:2/1:3 for weaning. Confirm augmentation is set to the ordered ratio & %.
Catheter & positioning
  • Femoral insertion: keep the leg straight (knee immobilizer); HOB ≤ 30°; do not flex the hip — kinking stops augmentation.
  • Check distal pulses, color, temp of the cannulated leg frequently (limb ischemia).
  • Position-sensitive: too high → may occlude the left subclavian (lost left radial pulse) or mesenteric/renal flow (↓ urine); too low → ineffective. Don't reposition in the field — note & relay.
  • Do NOT flush the balloon (gas) lumen. Maintain the arterial line for waveform.
Emergencies
  • Blood in the balloon tubing / loss of augmentation = possible balloon rupture → STOP the pump, clamp the catheter, place patient left-lateral/Trendelenburg, notify receiving/cardiology immediately (gas/clot embolism risk). Prepare for removal at a facility.
  • Console/power failure → switch to backup/battery; if it can't pump, manually inflate/deflate per console q5 min — do not leave idle.
  • Cardiac arrest: CPR as indicated; many consoles can switch trigger to "pressure"/internal — follow console arrest mode; defibrillate normally.
Hemodynamics & anticoagulation
  • The IABP augments — it does not replace volume or inotropy. Treat hypotension/shock with fluids, pressors/inotropes per medical control (and Shock/CV-instability).
  • Patient usually on a heparin infusion — continue per orders; watch the femoral site for bleeding/hematoma and for thrombocytopenia.
  • Monitor: arterial waveform (augmented diastolic), ECG trigger, distal limb perfusion, urine output, MAP.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Indication, trigger source, ratio & augmentation %, MAP/augmented pressures, timing checks (incl. after each move).
  • Distal pulses/leg checks, urine output, heparin, site; any alarms/rupture response.
  • Console/gas status & backups; receiving cardiac center + pre-notification.
Overview — counterpulsation, and the duty to keep it running

The intra-aortic balloon pump (IABP) is a catheter-mounted balloon sitting in the descending aorta that performs counterpulsation: it inflates during diastole to push blood back toward the coronaries (raising coronary perfusion) and deflates just before systole to create a vacuum that reduces the afterload the heart must eject against (lowering myocardial oxygen demand and modestly improving forward output).1,2 It is used for cardiogenic shock, refractory ischemia, mechanical complications of MI, and as a bridge. The transport mission is essentially to keep the device pumping correctly and the patient stable while moving to a cardiac center — the IABP itself does the therapy, and the crew's job is to protect it.1,2

Why an idle balloon is dangerous

The single most important transport rule is that the balloon must not sit motionless in the aorta: a stationary balloon rapidly accumulates thrombus on its surface, which can then embolize.1,2 Therefore augmentation is never paused for more than a few minutes; if the console fails it is switched to backup or battery power, and if it truly cannot pump, the balloon is manually inflated/deflated (per the console) about every five minutes or removed at the sending facility before transport. This is why the crew brings a charged console with a spare battery and a full helium gas tank (plus backup) and confirms the trigger, timing, and augmentation are correct before departure and again after every patient move, since transfers and lift-off can disturb leads and waveforms.2

Timing, triggering & the catheter position

Effective counterpulsation depends on correct timing relative to the cardiac cycle, driven by a trigger — usually the ECG R-wave (preferred) or the arterial pressure waveform — so a clean ECG and a good arterial line are prerequisites, and arrhythmias or a poor signal degrade augmentation.1,2 The catheter's position is critical and fragile: its tip sits just distal to the left subclavian artery, so migration upward can occlude the left subclavian (loss of the left radial pulse) or the mesenteric/renal vessels (falling urine output), and migration downward makes it ineffective. The crew protects position by keeping the cannulated leg straight (knee immobilizer), the head of bed at ≤ 30°, and the hip un-flexed — kinking the catheter stops augmentation — and does not reposition it in the field, instead noting and relaying any concern.2 The gas (balloon) lumen is never flushed.

Recognizing the emergencies

Two failures must be recognized instantly. Balloon rupture presents as blood in the balloon/gas tubing (and loss of augmentation); because helium or clot can embolize, the response is to stop the pump, clamp the catheter, place the patient left-lateral and head-down, and notify the receiving cardiology/surgical team immediately for urgent removal.1,2 Limb ischemia from the large femoral catheter is the most common complication, so distal pulses, color, and temperature of the cannulated leg are checked frequently. Console or power failure triggers the switch to backup/battery and, if needed, manual fluttering. In cardiac arrest, CPR proceeds and defibrillation is performed normally; most consoles offer a pressure/internal trigger or arrest mode to maintain some counterpulsation, which the crew selects per the device.2

The IABP is an adjunct, not a substitute

A crucial conceptual point is that the balloon pump augments the circulation but does not replace volume or contractility: a hypovolemic or profoundly failing patient still needs fluids, blood, and pressors/inotropes, managed per the Shock & Cardiovascular Instability pathway and online medical control.1,2 Patients are typically on a heparin infusion to prevent catheter thrombosis, which is continued per orders while the crew watches the femoral site for bleeding/hematoma and monitors for thrombocytopenia. Continuous monitoring of the augmented arterial waveform, the ECG trigger, distal limb perfusion, urine output, and MAP tells the crew both how the patient and how the device are doing. The destination is a cardiac center capable of definitive management (cath lab, cardiac surgery), and from the Keys the rotor-wing collapses the multi-hour ground option for these unstable patients.1,2
