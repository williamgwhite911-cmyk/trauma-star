Gunshot Wounds (Ballistic Trauma)

Penetrating · hemorrhage control · scene safety
TS-TR-008 · Sec 9 Trauma
Scene & priorities
  • Scene safety first — stage until law enforcement secures the scene; weapon accounted for. Preserve evidence/clothing when feasible.
  • Control catastrophic hemorrhage first (tourniquet / wound packing / pressure), then airway → breathing → circulation (MARCH). See TS-TR-001.
  • Count & document every wound — fully expose (back, axillae, groin, scalp, perineum); wounds come in pairs/odd numbers. Don't label entry vs exit.
Hemorrhage control
  • Extremity: tourniquet (CAT/SOF-TT) high & tight for life-threatening bleeding — record time.
  • Junctional (groin/axilla/neck): wound packing + hemostatic gauze + direct pressure.
  • Do NOT probe or remove deep clot/foreign body in the field. Pelvic binder if pelvis involved.
Chest / neck / abdomen
  • Any torso wound: vented chest seal over open chest wounds; watch for tension pneumothorax → needle/finger thoracostomy (TS-TR-003).
  • Neck: direct pressure (don't circumferentially wrap); watch airway/expanding hematoma — secure airway early.
  • Abdomen: assume internal hemorrhage; cover eviscerated bowel with saline-soaked gauze; do not reduce.
Resuscitation
  • Whole blood / blood products preferred (TS-HD-002); permissive hypotension SBP ~90 (radial pulse), 100–110 if TBI.
  • TXA within 3 h — ≥ 12 yr 2 g IV over 10 min; < 12 yr 15 mg/kg (max 1 g).
  • Two large-bore IV/IO; warm patient (prevent the lethal triad); high-flow O₂.
Transport
  • Load & go — minimize scene time; highest-level trauma center; pre-notify (activate massive transfusion).
  • Serial exam — penetrating injuries declare over time; reassess wounds/chest/abdomen.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Number/location of wounds; mechanism/weapon if known; time.
  • Tourniquet time(s); chest seals/decompression; blood/TXA; access; serial vitals/GCS.
  • Trauma-center destination + pre-notification; evidence/clothing handling.
Overview — control bleeding, buy time to the OR

Gunshot wounds are penetrating trauma in which the only definitive treatment for the common killer — non-compressible truncal hemorrhage — is the operating room. The transport mission is therefore narrow and urgent: control the bleeding you can reach, replace blood, protect the airway, and get to a trauma center fast, minimizing scene time.1,2 Everything is layered on the core trauma assessment (TS-TR-001) and hemorrhage-control ladder, with ballistic-specific emphasis on scene safety, full exposure to find every wound, and a high index of suspicion for injuries the surface wounds don't reveal.1,3

Scene safety & the wound count

A GSW scene is a potential crime scene and an active-threat environment, so crews stage until law enforcement secures it and the weapon is accounted for — a wounded provider helps no one.1 Once safe, the patient is fully exposed and every wound counted, because bullets travel in straight or unpredictable paths and wounds hide in the axillae, groin, perineum, buttocks, scalp, and back; a missed posterior wound is a missed hemorrhage. The protocol deliberately avoids labeling wounds "entry" vs "exit" — that is a forensic determination that field providers frequently get wrong and that can mislead the legal record; instead, wounds are described by location and number.1 Clothing and evidence are preserved where it does not delay care.

Why hemorrhage control is the whole game

Hemorrhage is the leading cause of preventable death in penetrating trauma, and the lessons of military medicine apply directly: early tourniquets for extremity bleeding and wound packing for junctional sites save lives.2,3 A tourniquet is placed high and tight for any life-threatening extremity wound and its time recorded; junctional wounds (groin, axilla, neck base) are packed tightly with hemostatic gauze and held under pressure. The field does not probe wounds or remove deep clot/foreign bodies, which can dislodge tamponade and unleash bleeding that cannot be controlled outside the OR. A suspected pelvic injury gets a binder to tamponade the pelvic venous plexus.3

Cavitation & the hidden-injury principle

Ballistic energy creates a temporary cavity far larger than the bullet track, so tissue damage extends well beyond the visible wound and the bullet's path through the body is unpredictable (it can deflect off bone).1 Practically, this means a single small skin wound can overlie devastating chest, abdominal, vascular, or spinal injury, and a thoraco-abdominal wound may have crossed the diaphragm. The crew therefore treats the trajectory and the body cavities at risk, not just the skin: any torso GSW prompts vigilance for tension pneumothorax (vented seal, low threshold to decompress — TS-TR-003), internal hemorrhage, and, with neck wounds, airway compromise from an expanding hematoma (secure the airway early before swelling makes it impossible).1 Penetrating injuries also declare themselves over time, so serial reassessment in transport is essential.

Resuscitation & the load-and-go imperative

Resuscitation follows damage-control principles: blood products over crystalloid (whole blood — TS-HD-002), permissive hypotension to a radial pulse / SBP ~90 (higher, 100–110, with concurrent TBI to protect the brain), early TXA within 3 hours, and aggressive prevention of the lethal triad (warming, calcium with transfusion).2,3 The single most outcome-determining decision is minimizing scene time and reaching the highest-level trauma center with massive-transfusion capability and a surgeon — for truncal hemorrhage, the helicopter's value is collapsing the time to the OR. The crew pre-notifies so the trauma team and blood bank are ready on arrival.1,2
