Agitation & Chemical Restraint

In-flight safety · droperidol · ketamine
TS-MED-001 · Sec 10 Medical
Assess & pre-empt (treat before loading)
  • Risk factors: prior violence, intoxication / under the influence (alcohol, stimulants, hallucinogens, sympathomimetics, PCP/ketamine), drug/alcohol withdrawal, delirium/psychosis, cognitive impairment, head injury, hypoxia/hypoglycemia/shock.
  • Find & treat reversible causes first — check glucose & SpO₂; agitation may be hypoxia, hypoglycemia, hypotension, or head injury, not just behavior.
  • Score with SAS or RASS; reassess. Treat agitation before loading when possible; notify the pilot of risk.
  • Prep HOB: I-gel, BVM, meds, full airway/RSI kit — agitation can escalate to dangerous at any time.
At-risk / anxious — Droperidol first-line (physician order)
Droperidol (adult, preferred)
2.5 mg IV/IM/IO, repeat in 3 min PRN, max 10 mg. Peak 20–30 min. Mono or with a benzo. 50% reduction in elderly/comorbid. Haloperidol = alternative.
Midazolam (adult, co-therapy)
5 mg IV/IM/IO, repeat in 5 min PRN. 50% reduction elderly/comorbid.
Note
Alcohol-withdrawal / stimulant-intoxicated: prefer benzo q5 min + antipsychotic (ketamine less effective). No pediatric standing orders — physician order required.
Flight go / no-go — reject OR sedate & intubate
  • Do NOT fly a dangerously agitated patient who is only partially controlled — re-escalation in a confined, single-pilot cabin is catastrophic and light/intermittent sedation can wear off mid-flight.
  • DEFAULT = REJECT the flight. When control is in any doubt, decline air transport / move by ground (more space, can stop, more hands, safer cabin). When unsure — reject.
  • Only if air transport is medically necessary AND the patient can be fully controlled: definitively SEDATE & INTUBATE (RSI) before launch to secure the patient and airway (Airway TS-AW-001; post-intubation sedation TS-AS-004).
  • "Snow them just enough to load" is not a safe plan. Decide before takeoff, then commit.
Dangerous agitation → CLINICAL MAYDAY
  • DECLARE A CLINICAL MAYDAY — NOTIFY PILOT (request land / stop) for uncontrolled agitation that threatens flight safety.
  • Rapid control with a dissociative dose of ketamine; establish SpO₂, ECG, NIBP, waveform capnography, RR ASAP.
Ketamine — dissociative (chemical restraint)
Adult & Pediatric
3 mg/kg IBW IV/IO push OR 4 mg/kg IBW IM (most concentrated form). Give the full calculated dose regardless of comorbidity/intoxicants.
Immediately follow with infusion
Adult 4 mg/kg/hr · Peds 2 mg/kg/hr. Prepare airway/ventilation before dosing; ondansetron PRN.
🚁 IN-FLIGHT emergency — immediate threat to the aircraft
  • A patient fighting free of restraints/airway, striking the crew, or reaching for controls in flight is a threat to everyone on board — this is a CLINICAL MAYDAY; notify the pilot, request immediate land/divert.
  • If a dissociative ketamine dose cannot be given/has not worked fast enough and there is an immediate threat to flight safety, give a paralytic NOW to stop the threat — Rocuronium 1.5 mg/kg IBW IV/IO / 2 mg/kg IM, or Succinylcholine 4 mg/kg IM (fastest IM onset ~3 min).
  • A paralytic = a committed airway. The patient cannot breathe — provide immediate BVM then a definitive airway, and give sedation/analgesia (ketamine ± fentanyl/midazolam) so the patient is NEVER paralyzed-but-aware. Capnography/SpO₂ on.
Paralysis (adult only; with ketamine + airway)
  • Rocuronium 1.5 mg/kg IBW IV/IO or 2 mg/kg IM (onset ~5 min IM; faster IV).
  • Succinylcholine 4 mg/kg IM (onset 3–4 min) — fastest if no IV. Avoid if hyperkalemia risk (crush, burns > 24 h, denervation).
  • Airway management is mandatory with any NMB; confirm post-paralysis sedation is running.
Physical restraint
  • Least-restrictive method; quick-release, full care access, airway control + monitoring preserved. Verbal de-escalation first; consult medical control where practicable.
  • Refuse air transport (use ground) if the patient cannot be safely controlled.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Risk assessment + SAS/RASS scores/times; de-escalation attempts.
  • Meds (drug/dose/route/time/response); monitoring established; airway readiness.
  • Restraint type + neurovascular checks; clinical mayday; physician orders.
Overview — agitation is a flight-safety emergency

In the confined, single-pilot rotor-wing cabin, an agitated patient is not only a clinical problem but a flight-safety emergency: an unrestrained, combative patient can strike the crew, dislodge lines and the airway, or destabilize the aircraft.1,5 The protocol therefore treats agitation proactively — assessing risk and treating before loading whenever possible — and uses objective scales (Riker SAS or RASS) so that severity and the response to treatment are tracked the same way by every crew member and the receiving team.2 The unifying principle is to control agitation early and adequately, because a half-treated patient who escalates mid-flight is far more dangerous than one fully managed on the ground.1

Find the cause — agitation is a symptom

Dangerous agitation is frequently driven by a reversible medical cause, and missing it is both a clinical and a safety error. Hypoxia, hypoglycemia, hypotension/shock, head injury, pain, sepsis, and drug or alcohol intoxication/withdrawal all present as agitation, so the workup includes glucose, oxygenation, and a search for occult injury or illness rather than assuming a primary psychiatric cause.1 This matters for drug choice too: the protocol notes that ketamine may be less effective in the alcohol-withdrawal or acutely stimulant-intoxicated patient, where escalating benzodiazepines (treating the underlying GABA deficit of withdrawal) plus an antipsychotic are preferred.1

Why droperidol first for the at-risk patient

For the anxious or at-risk-but-not-yet-dangerous patient, droperidol is first-line because it provides reliable antipsychotic sedation with a favorable profile and a large body of emergency-department evidence supporting its efficacy and safety for acute agitation; the historical QT "black box" concern has not borne out at these doses in ED studies.3 It can be used alone or combined with a benzodiazepine, and the dose is reduced by half in the elderly or comorbid to limit respiratory depression and over-sedation. Because its peak effect can take 20–30 minutes, the crew must avoid dose-stacking impatiently. All of this is delivered under a physician order (no pediatric standing order), reflecting that calming a patient is a medical act with real airway risk.1,3

Dissociative ketamine for true dangerous agitation

When agitation becomes dangerous — an immediate threat to the flight — the priority shifts to rapid, reliable control, and a dissociative dose of ketamine (3 mg/kg IV/IO or 4 mg/kg IM) achieves this faster and more predictably than titrated sedation, while largely preserving airway reflexes and respiratory drive.1,4 The protocol's instruction to give the full calculated dose regardless of comorbidity or intoxicants reflects a deliberate safety trade-off: an under-dosed, still-combative patient at altitude is the greater danger. Because dissociative ketamine can cause transient laryngospasm, hypersalivation, hypertension, and (rarely) apnea, full monitoring and airway readiness are established before dosing, an infusion follows the bolus to prevent re-emergence, and the event triggers a clinical mayday so the pilot can land or the driver can stop.1,4

Paralysis, physical restraint & refusing transport

If chemical sedation is insufficient and a definitive airway is required, a neuromuscular blocker may be added (adult only) — but any paralytic mandates immediate airway management, because a paralyzed patient cannot breathe.1 Physical restraints are used only as needed and always with least-restrictive technique, quick release, and preserved access to the airway and monitoring, never in a position (e.g., prone hog-tie) that risks positional asphyxia.1,5 Finally, the protocol explicitly permits refusing air transport in favor of ground if the patient cannot be controlled to a level safe for flight — recognizing that the safest decision is sometimes not to fly.1
