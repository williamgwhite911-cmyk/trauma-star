Jet Ski / High-Pressure Water Injury

PWC orifice injury · occult rectal/vaginal trauma
TS-TR-010 · Sec 9 Trauma
Suspect it — high index of suspicion
  • Fall off the back/rear of a PWC (jet ski) into the jet thrust — high-pressure water forced into the rectum or vagina.
  • Rectal/vaginal bleeding, perineal/lower-abdominal pain after a watercraft fall = orifice injury until proven otherwise.
  • External exam can look minor while internal injury is severe — rectal/colon/vaginal lacerations, perforation, retroperitoneal injury. This is a surgical emergency.
Assessment
  • Full trauma survey (TS-TR-001) — also consider blunt abdominal/pelvic injury, drowning/aspiration, C-spine, extremity injury, hypothermia from immersion.
  • Inspect perineum/external genitalia for bleeding; note blood at meatus/vagina/rectum. Do NOT insert anything or pack the orifice.
  • Serial abdominal exam; watch for peritonitis/shock developing over time.
Management
  • NPO (likely OR). Two large-bore IV/IO.
  • Treat shock per Shock/CV-instability — blood products preferred (TS-HD-002); fluids to perfusion; keep warm.
  • Analgesia (TS-AS-002): IV acetaminophen first, then opioids/ketamine titrated.
  • External bleeding: gentle direct pressure / perineal pad — do not pack inside the orifice. Antiemetic PRN.
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics may be indicated (fecal/vaginal contamination) — per online medical control.
Transport
  • Trauma/surgical center — these injuries frequently need OR exploration ± diverting colostomy; delayed diagnosis → sepsis.
  • Pre-notify; relay the mechanism clearly (fall off rear of PWC into jet stream) so the receiving team anticipates occult injury.
  • Position of comfort; continuous monitoring for occult hemorrhage.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Exact mechanism (PWC fall, position, protective wear), water entry site, time.
  • Perineal/rectal/vaginal findings; that nothing was inserted/packed internally.
  • Fluids/blood, analgesia, antibiotics (if given/ordered), serial vitals/abdomen; surgical-center destination.
Overview — a deceptive Keys mechanism

Personal watercraft (PWC / "jet ski") produce a powerful pressurized water jet for propulsion. When a rider or passenger falls off the back into that jet stream, water can be driven under high pressure through a swimsuit and into a body orifice — most often the rectum or vagina — causing hydrostatic lacerations and perforation of the rectum, colon, and vaginal vault, sometimes with retroperitoneal extension.1,2 In a high-traffic recreational-boating environment like the Florida Keys this is a recognized, recurring injury, and the protocol exists because it is so easy to under-triage.1,3

Why suspicion is the entire protocol

The defining feature of these injuries is the mismatch between a benign-appearing exterior and severe internal damage.1,2 A patient may walk up with only perineal discomfort or a little rectal/vaginal bleeding, yet have a full-thickness rectal or colon tear with developing peritonitis. Because field providers cannot exclude internal injury, the rule is a high index of suspicion based on mechanism: any fall off the rear of a PWC with rectal/vaginal bleeding, perineal pain, or lower-abdominal pain is treated as an occult orifice injury and transported to a surgical center, even if the patient looks well. Delayed diagnosis is the main source of morbidity — missed perforations progress to fecal peritonitis and sepsis.1,3

What NOT to do — and the associated injuries

The crew does not insert anything into, or pack, the rectum or vagina — blind instrumentation can extend a laceration, worsen bleeding, and contaminate tissue planes; internal control and examination belong in the OR.1 External bleeding is managed with gentle pressure and a perineal pad. Crucially, the PWC mechanism is rarely isolated: the same fall and impact can cause blunt abdominal/pelvic trauma, spinal injury, extremity fractures, drowning/aspiration, and immersion hypothermia, so a complete trauma survey (TS-TR-001) is performed and the patient is kept warm.1,2 The patient is kept NPO in anticipation of surgery.

Resuscitation, antibiotics & disposition

Hemorrhage from rectal/pelvic/retroperitoneal injury can be significant and occult, so shock is treated on the damage-control pathway — blood products preferred (TS-HD-002), titrated to perfusion, with warming.4 Pain is managed multimodally (IV acetaminophen first, then opioids/ketamine — TS-AS-002). Because these wounds are heavily contaminated (stool, vaginal flora, lake/sea water), early broad-spectrum antibiotics are often appropriate and are given under medical control. Definitive care is surgical — exploration, repair, washout, and frequently a diverting colostomy — so the destination is a trauma/surgical center, and the crew clearly relays the mechanism so the receiving team anticipates the occult injury rather than being reassured by the exterior.1,3

Prevention note (for crew & public education)

Manufacturers and the U.S. Coast Guard advise that PWC riders and passengers wear a wetsuit bottom or thick protective neoprene shorts precisely to prevent these orifice injuries — a useful point for crew safety briefings and community injury-prevention outreach in a dive- and watercraft-heavy region.2
