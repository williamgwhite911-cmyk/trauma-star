Post-Intubation Analgesia & Sedation

Analgesia-first · RASS −1 to −5
TS-AS-004 · Sec 11 Analgesia & Sedation
Principles
  • Never leave a paralyzed/intubated patient unsedated. Establish capnography, SpO₂, ECG, NIBP, RR.
  • Set a goal (RASS −1 to −5); assess with RASS/SAS/CPOT/BPS. Analgesia first, then sedation.
  • Bolus to goal → then scheduled bolus/infusion; treat pain/agitation pre-emptively. Push IV agents slowly over 1–2 min.
Stratify the patient
  • Can assess (responds, no NMB) → titrate to scale.
  • Cannot assess (NMB/unassessable) → empiric targeted dosing (scales invalid).
  • Hemodynamically unstable (hypotension, SI ≥ 1) → ketamine preferred; reduce other sedatives by ½–¼.
First-line (use ONE; optimize before adding)
Fentanyl (analgesia + light sedation)
0.5–2 mcg/kg IBW IV q10 min, or 1–2 mcg/kg/hr infusion. Titrate to goal.
Ketamine (preferred if unstable)
Bolus 1–2 mg/kg IBW IV first, then 1–4 mg/kg/hr infusion (or rebolus q20–30 min). Bolus before infusion or under-sedation/dysphoria.
Add ONE sedative if RASS goal unmet (pain controlled)
Propofol (no analgesia — keep opioid running)
5–100 mcg/kg/min (titrate 5–10 q5 min; >100 needs physician). Bolus 0.5–1 mg/kg w/ infusion. Anticipate hypotension (fluids/pressors).
Midazolam (preferred if seizure risk masked by NMB)
0.025–0.05 mg/kg IV q10 min, or 0.05–0.2 mg/kg/hr.
Dexmedetomidine
0.2–1.5 mcg/kg/hr (titrate 0.2 q30 min). Lorazepam if no midazolam (0.025–0.1 mg/kg, max 2).
Controlled-substance infusions
  • Do not take possession of another facility's controlled-substance infusion — prepare a NEW infusion with PHI/Trauma Star drugs.
  • Waste & document residual controlled substances after handoff.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • RASS/pain goal & serial scores; stratification category; capnography.
  • Analgesic + sedative (agent/dose/infusion/time/response); ketamine bolus-before-infusion; hemodynamic support.
  • Controlled-substance waste; receiving handoff.
Overview — the intubated patient is awake-able and in pain

An endotracheal tube is intensely noxious, and transport adds noise, vibration, movement, and procedures. The cardinal rule is that no intubated — and especially no paralyzed — patient is ever left without analgesia and sedation; awareness during paralysis is a devastating, preventable harm.1,2 The protocol's structure is analgesia-first, then sedation, titrated to a defined target (RASS −1 to −5), because much of what looks like "agitation" in an intubated patient is actually pain, and treating pain first often achieves the sedation goal with less sedative.1,2

Analgesia-first (A1) and avoiding over-sedation

Modern ICU practice (the SCCM PADIS guidelines) favors an analgesia-first / analgosedation approach and targeting light sedation when possible, because deep sedation prolongs ventilation, causes hypotension, and worsens outcomes.2 The protocol mirrors this: choose one agent and optimize it before adding a second (alternating/stacking agents causes adverse events and confusion), use fentanyl for analgesia ± light sedation, and add a dedicated sedative only when an adequate opioid controls pain but the RASS goal still isn't met.1,2 Drugs are pushed slowly (1–2 min) and titrated by repeat bolus to goal, then maintained by scheduled bolus or infusion, with pre-emptive dosing before predictable stimulation (moving the patient, turbulence).1

Stratify — and why the unstable patient gets ketamine

The protocol stratifies into three groups.1 The patient who can be assessed is titrated to a scale. The patient who cannot (paralyzed/unassessable) gets empiric targeted-dose therapy, because behavioral scales are meaningless under paralysis (see TS-AS-001). The hemodynamically unstable patient (hypotension, shock index ≥ 1) is the one in whom drug choice matters most: most sedatives (propofol, benzodiazepines, even opioids) drop blood pressure, so their doses are reduced by one-half to one-quarter — with the deliberate exception of ketamine, which supports hemodynamics and is therefore the preferred agent for post-intubation analgosedation in shock.1 The ketamine-specific teaching point is to give a dissociative bolus before starting the infusion; an infusion alone ramps up too slowly and leaves the patient under-sedated and dysphoric.1

Choosing the second-agent sedative

When a sedative must be added, the choice is matched to the clinical situation.1,2 Propofol gives rapidly titratable deep sedation but no analgesia (the opioid must keep running) and causes dose-dependent hypotension, so fluids/pressors are anticipated and high doses need a physician. Midazolam is the preferred sedative when there is a risk of seizure masked by a paralytic (it is also an anticonvulsant) — an important link to the seizure protocol, since a paralyzed status patient shows no convulsions. Dexmedetomidine provides cooperative sedation with minimal respiratory depression (useful for lighter targets) but can cause bradycardia/hypotension and cannot achieve deep sedation alone. Lorazepam substitutes for midazolam when needed.1,2

Controlled-substance chain of custody

A non-clinical but mandatory element: the crew may not take possession of, continue, or leave behind another facility's controlled-substance infusion. If an infusion (e.g., fentanyl, midazolam, ketamine) is to be continued, a new infusion is prepared with the program's own controlled substances, and any residual is wasted and documented after handoff.1 This protects the chain of custody and the crew legally, and it is built into the workflow so that continuing sedation across the transfer is planned rather than improvised. The overarching aim throughout is a comfortable, safely sedated patient at the lightest effective depth, with the airway and hemodynamics protected the entire flight.1,2
