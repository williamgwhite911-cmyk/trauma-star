Decompression Sickness (Bends)

Diving injury · decompression illness
TS-ENV-001 · Sec 5 Environmental & Diving
📞
DAN Emergency Hotline (24/7)
+1-919-684-9111 — dive physician + nearest recompression chamber
1 · Immediate actions
  • Assume decompression illness until proven otherwise.
  • Notify the pilot now — request the lowest feasible cabin altitude and the fastest safe landing/diversion plan. Stop nonessential tasking.
  • 100% oxygen, highest-flow available (NRB 15 L/min), continuous — even if symptoms improve.
  • Position supine if tolerated (no head-down Trendelenburg) unless airway/respiratory compromise.
  • IV/IO access — LR or NS 150 mL/hr IV, cautious (avoid worsening pulmonary edema/CHF); bolus for hypotension per medical control.
  • Check glucose, vitals, SpO₂, mental status; serial neuro exams.
  • Call DAN +1-919-684-9111 + medical control → nearest recompression chamber. Neuro involvement = emergency — expedite hyperbaric consult.
2 · Symptom screen
Treat as possible bends if any of these follow a dive / pressure change (onset usually < 6 h, up to 24–48 h):
  • Joint / limb pain — shoulder, elbow, knee, or back.
  • Skin — itching, rash, marbling (cutis marmorata), swelling.
  • Constitutional — fatigue, dizziness, headache, nausea, vomiting.
  • Neuro — numbness, weakness, gait change, confusion, speech or vision change.
  • Cardiopulmonary — chest pain, dyspnea, cough, worsening hypoxemia; severe decompression sickness can cause sudden LOC/seizure at surfacing.
3 · Dive-history checklist — gather & bring the computer
  • Max depth of each dive (ft / m)
  • Bottom time(s) and total dive time
  • Number of dives + repetitive dives in last 24–72 h; surface intervals
  • Breathing gas — air, nitrox (O₂ %), trimix, or rebreather
  • Ascent — rate, rapid/uncontrolled ascent, missed/omitted deco stops
  • Dive computer / profile — bring the computer & gear with the patient
  • Time of surfacing and time symptoms started (latency) + progression (better/worse)
  • Altitude after diving — any flying, driving over mountains, or planned flight
  • Water temp/conditions, exertion, equipment problems, near-drowning/aspiration
  • Prior DCS, known PFO, PMH, meds, alcohol; first aid given (O₂ start time), any in-water recompression
4 · Supportive care
  • Maintain 100% O₂ throughout transport until definitive evaluation.
  • Fluids cautiously (LR or NS) — support perfusion without worsening pulmonary edema / heart failure.
  • Avoid exertion, unnecessary movement, and heat loss.
  • Pain control with service-approved analgesia without masking neuro changes.
  • No Valsalva, no smoking, no further flight/altitude exposure.
  • Any airway concern → prepare for advanced airway early.
5 · Escalation criteria
Immediate hyperbaric / receiving-facility coordination if any of:
  • Any neurologic deficit.
  • Persistent or severe pain.
  • Respiratory symptoms.
  • Altered mental status.
  • Hemodynamic instability.
  • Symptoms that improve on O₂ but recur off O₂.
6 · Flight / transport — FLY LOW
  • Altitude expands bubbles and worsens DCS. Descend to the lowest safe altitude and land/divert as soon as practical.
  • Target below 1000 ft; ideally within ~150 m (≈500 ft) of takeoff/sea-level elevation. Avoid high cabin altitude and prolonged transport.*
  • Continuous 100% O₂ for the entire flight; keep patient horizontal.
  • Route directly to the nearest hyperbaric-capable facility / designated transfer point.
  • Rotor-wing role = stabilize, oxygenate, rapid descent/land, transfer — definitive treatment is recompression in a chamber, not in flight.
7 · Receiving handoff
  • Exposure / dive history — depth, duration, ascent rate, pressure events.
  • Symptom onset & progression.
  • Neuro exam findings.
  • O₂ therapy given & response.
  • Fluids, meds, and any complications.
  • ETA + need for hyperbaric consultation.
8 · Cautions & red flags
  • No nitrous oxide / Entonox — it expands gas bubbles. Caution with any air-filled space.
  • Symptoms may improve on O₂ but recur — the patient still needs a chamber.
  • Neuro deficits or severe decompression sickness = expedite recompression.
  • Don't delay O₂ or transport to obtain a "perfect" history.
9 · Documentation — medically necessary
  • Full dive profile (depths, bottom times, gas, ascent, missed stops, repetitive dives).
  • Symptom-onset time (latency) + progression.
  • Serial neuro exam findings.
  • O₂ start time & flow, position, fluids; DAN + medical control contact; receiving chamber.
  • Altitude profile — transport altitude kept low (record max cabin/flight altitude).
  • Why rotor-wing to the nearest recompression facility (time-critical, distance/capability).
Overview & why the Keys see this disease

Decompression sickness ("the bends") is one form of decompression illness (DCI), an umbrella term that also includes arterial gas embolism (AGE). They share a final pathway — gas bubbles in blood and tissue — and, importantly, they share the same field treatment, so the crew does not need to distinguish them definitively to act correctly.1,2 During a dive, the body absorbs inert gas (nitrogen, or helium with technical mixes) into tissues in proportion to depth and time. If ascent is too fast or too prolonged-at-depth for that gas to be exhaled in solution, it comes out as bubbles, producing mechanical obstruction and a secondary inflammatory cascade (endothelial injury, platelet activation, capillary leak).2,3 The Florida Keys have one of the highest recreational dive volumes in the United States and a finite number of recompression chambers, so for Trauma Star this is a recurring, time-critical mission in which early oxygen and the right destination materially change outcome.1,2

Pathophysiology — the gas laws that govern treatment

Two gas laws explain both the injury and the therapy. By Henry's law, the amount of gas dissolved in tissue is proportional to its partial pressure, so a diver at depth loads nitrogen into tissues; on ascent the ambient pressure falls and that gas can supersaturate and bubble. By Boyle's law, a bubble's volume is inversely proportional to pressure — so as ambient pressure drops, existing bubbles enlarge.2,3 These two principles drive every management decision. High-flow 100% oxygen works by replacing the nitrogen in the lungs with oxygen, creating a steep diffusion gradient that washes dissolved nitrogen out of tissue and shrinks bubbles, while simultaneously oxygenating tissue that bubbles have rendered ischemic — which is why oxygen is given even to mild cases and continued even after symptoms improve.2,3 The same Boyle's-law logic is the reason altitude is dangerous: any gain in cabin altitude above the dive site lowers ambient pressure and expands bubbles, potentially converting a mild case into a severe one mid-flight.4,5 Definitive treatment is hyperbaric recompression, which physically re-compresses bubbles and drives reabsorption while delivering hyperbaric oxygen.2,3

Clinical spectrum & recognition

DCI presents along a wide spectrum and symptoms can be deceptively mild at first. Type I (mild) DCS classically causes joint and limb pain ("the bends"), mottled or itchy skin (cutis marmorata), and lymphatic swelling. Type II (serious) DCS involves the nervous system — spinal-cord involvement with back/girdle pain, paresthesias, ascending weakness, and bladder dysfunction; inner-ear ("staggers") symptoms with vertigo, nausea, and hearing loss; and cardiopulmonary ("chokes") symptoms with cough, chest pain, and dyspnea.2,6 AGE typically presents abruptly within minutes of surfacing — often after breath-holding on ascent — with stroke-like deficits, seizures, or sudden loss of consciousness, and is a true emergency.2,6 Two pitfalls matter in the field: symptoms can be delayed for hours after surfacing (so a "well" diver who later develops fatigue, paresthesias, or pain should still be treated as DCI), and apparently trivial symptoms can herald serious disease, so the threshold to start oxygen and call for help should be low.2,6

Dive profile — what to gather and why it matters
  • Max depth & bottom time for each dive — the primary determinants of inert-gas loading and DCI severity.
  • Number of dives & surface intervals over the prior 24–72 h (repetitive diving accumulates residual nitrogen).
  • Ascent rate & missed decompression/safety stops — rapid or omitted stops are a leading trigger.
  • Breathing gas (air, nitrox, trimix) and any equipment problems or rapid/uncontrolled ascent.
  • Time of symptom onset relative to surfacing — minutes (suggests AGE) vs hours (suggests DCS), plus the trend since.
  • Any flying or altitude exposure after diving, water temperature, exertion, and dehydration — all modify risk. Pull the dive computer/log if available and bring it.1,2,5
In-flight considerations
  • Altitude is the central issue. Low-level helicopter transport is safe and effective; complications appear when flight rises well above the takeoff elevation. Stay below ~1000 ft and as near sea-level as the route safely allows.4,5
  • Maintain continuous 100% O₂ and a horizontal patient for the whole flight.
  • Confirm the destination is the nearest staffed recompression chamber; involve DAN early.1
  • Balance altitude/vibration risk against time-to-recompression — earlier recompression improves prognosis.5
Management — detail

Give the highest practical fraction of oxygen immediately and continue it even if symptoms resolve, since improvement on oxygen is often temporary without recompression.2,3 Keep the patient horizontal — the historical head-down/left-lateral position is no longer recommended and may cause harm.3,6 Hydrate with IV isotonic crystalloid — Lactated Ringer's (LR) or 0.9% normal saline (NS) 150 mL/hr (titrate/bolus for hypotension per medical control). Contact the DAN emergency line for dive-medicine consultation and to locate the nearest chamber.1,2 Trauma Star transports rotor-wing only: stay as low as safely possible — below ~1000 ft and as near sea-level/takeoff elevation as the route allows — throughout the flight.4,5 Avoid nitrous oxide and other interventions that expand gas spaces.
