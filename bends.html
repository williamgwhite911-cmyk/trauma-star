Decompression Sickness (Bends)

Diving injury · decompression illness
TS-ENV-001 · §5 Environmental & Diving
📞
DAN Emergency Hotline (24/7)
+1-919-684-9111 — dive physician + nearest recompression chamber
Recognize — decompression illness (DCI)
  • "Bends" (DCS I): deep aching joint/limb pain, skin mottling (cutis marmorata), itch, fatigue.
  • Serious (DCS II): numbness/paresthesia, weakness, vertigo, ataxia, bladder/bowel dysfunction, confusion.
  • Pulmonary ("chokes"): cough, chest pain, dyspnea.
  • Arterial gas embolism: sudden LOC, seizure, or stroke-like deficits at/just after surfacing — emergency.
  • Any neuro/chest symptom after a dive = serious DCI until proven otherwise (onset usually <6 h, up to 24–48 h).
Immediate treatment
  • 100% oxygen — non-rebreather 15 L/min, continuous, even if symptoms improve. This is the primary field treatment.
  • Position supine / horizontal — do not use head-down Trendelenburg.
  • IV fluids — Lactated Ringer's 150 mL/hr IV (titrate / bolus for hypotension per medical control). Keep warm (avoid overheating).
  • Call DAN +1-919-684-9111 and medical control; identify the nearest recompression chamber as destination.
  • Monitor — SpO₂, ECG, and serial neuro exams; treat pain per protocol (avoid masking neuro change).
Dive-history checklist — gather & bring the computer
  • Max depth of each dive (ft / m)
  • Bottom time(s) and total dive time
  • Number of dives + repetitive dives in last 24–72 h; surface intervals
  • Breathing gas — air, nitrox (O₂ %), trimix, or rebreather
  • Ascent — rate, rapid/uncontrolled ascent, missed/omitted deco stops
  • Dive computer / profile — bring the computer & gear with the patient
  • Time of surfacing and time symptoms started (latency) + progression (better/worse)
  • Altitude after diving — any flying, driving over mountains, or planned flight
  • Water temp/conditions, exertion, equipment problems, near-drowning/aspiration
  • Prior DCS, known PFO, PMH, meds, alcohol; first aid given (O₂ start time), any in-water recompression
Flight / transport — FLY LOW
  • Altitude expands bubbles and worsens DCS. Fly as low as safely possible.
  • Target below 1000 ft; ideally within ~150 m (≈500 ft) of takeoff/sea-level elevation.*
  • Continuous 100% O₂ for the entire flight; keep patient horizontal.
  • Balance altitude risk against the benefit of rapid recompression — do not delay reaching the chamber.
Cautions & red flags
  • No nitrous oxide / Entonox — it expands gas bubbles. Caution with any air-filled space.
  • Symptoms may improve on O₂ but recur — the patient still needs a chamber.
  • Neuro deficits, chokes, or AGE = severe DCI — expedite recompression.
  • Don't delay O₂ or transport to obtain a "perfect" history.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Full dive profile (depths, bottom times, gas, ascent, missed stops, repetitive dives).
  • Surfacing time and symptom-onset time (latency) + progression.
  • Serial neuro exam findings.
  • O₂ start time & flow, position, fluids; DAN + medical control contact; receiving chamber.
  • Transport altitude kept low (record max cabin/flight altitude).
  • Why rotor-wing to the nearest recompression facility (time-critical, distance/capability).
Overview

Decompression sickness ("the bends") is part of decompression illness (DCI), which also includes arterial gas embolism (AGE). During ascent, inert gas (nitrogen) dissolved in tissues under pressure comes out of solution and forms bubbles, causing mechanical and inflammatory injury. In the Florida Keys' high dive volume, rapid recognition, oxygen, and transport to a recompression chamber are time-critical.1,2

Pathophysiology / rationale

By Henry's law, gas dissolves in tissue proportional to pressure at depth; on ascent the pressure falls and bubbles form (Boyle's law — bubbles enlarge as ambient pressure drops). High-flow 100% oxygen creates a steep nitrogen washout gradient that shrinks bubbles and speeds inert-gas elimination, and improves tissue oxygenation.2,3 Because lower ambient pressure enlarges bubbles, any gain in altitude during transport can worsen DCI — the basis for low-altitude flight.4,5 Definitive treatment is hyperbaric (recompression) oxygen.2,3

In-flight considerations
  • Altitude is the central issue. Low-level helicopter transport is safe and effective; complications appear when flight rises well above the takeoff elevation. Stay below ~1000 ft and as near sea-level as the route safely allows.4,5
  • Maintain continuous 100% O₂ and a horizontal patient for the whole flight.
  • Confirm the destination is the nearest staffed recompression chamber; involve DAN early.1
  • Balance altitude/vibration risk against time-to-recompression — earlier recompression improves prognosis.5
Management — detail

Give the highest practical fraction of oxygen immediately and continue it even if symptoms resolve, since improvement on oxygen is often temporary without recompression.2,3 Keep the patient horizontal — the historical head-down/left-lateral position is no longer recommended and may cause harm.3,6 Hydrate with IV isotonic crystalloid — Lactated Ringer's 150 mL/hr (titrate/bolus for hypotension per medical control). Contact the DAN emergency line for dive-medicine consultation and to locate the nearest chamber.1,2 Trauma Star transports rotor-wing only: stay as low as safely possible — below ~1000 ft and as near sea-level/takeoff elevation as the route allows — throughout the flight.4,5 Avoid nitrous oxide and other interventions that expand gas spaces.
