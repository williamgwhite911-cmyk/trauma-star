Toxicology · naloxone

1-800-222-1222 — identify substance, amount, time of exposure
Indication
  • Opioid-induced life-threatening respiratory depression or apnea.
  • Ventilate first (BVM + airway) — oxygenation is the priority; titrate naloxone to adequate breathing, not full arousal.
Naloxone
Adult
1–2 mg IV / IM / IN — titrate to effective respiration; repeat PRN for full reversal.
Pediatric
0.01 mg/kg IV/IM/IN (max single 1 mg)
Newborn — respiratory depression
0.1 mg/kg IV/IM/IN if refractory to PPV with maternal opioid within 4 h.
Infusion (severe / recurrent)
Find the initial dose that reinstates breathing, then give ⅔ of that dose every hour; stop if withdrawal. Defer to sending/receiving physician for dose/titration.
Cautions & red flags
  • Re-sedation — naloxone is shorter-acting than many opioids (fentanyl analogs, methadone). Monitor closely; consider an infusion.
  • Avoid over-reversal → acute withdrawal, agitation, vomiting/aspiration, sympathetic surge.
  • Consider co-ingestants and other causes of ↓LOC (glucose, hypoxia, trauma).
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Suspected agent/amount/time; respiratory status & SpO₂/EtCO₂ before/after.
  • Naloxone — dose, route, times, response (RR, mentation); any infusion + physician orders.
  • Ventilation/airway support; recurrence of sedation.
  • Poison Control contact; why rotor-wing / level of care.
Overview — respiratory failure is the killer

Opioids kill by suppressing the brainstem's respiratory drive. They act on µ-opioid receptors in the medulla to blunt the ventilatory response to rising CO₂ and falling O₂, producing progressive hypoventilation, hypercarbia, hypoxia, and ultimately bradycardic hypoxic cardiac arrest.1 The classic toxidrome — depressed consciousness, pinpoint pupils, and a low respiratory rate — points to the diagnosis, but the lethal element is the breathing, not the pupils or the level of consciousness. This reframes the entire resuscitation: the first and most important intervention is oxygenation and ventilation (bag-valve-mask), and naloxone is an adjunct that buys time, not a substitute for airway management.1,2 A patient who is being ventilated effectively is, for the moment, safe even before any naloxone is given.

Naloxone — why "titrate to breathing," not to arousal

Naloxone is a competitive opioid antagonist that displaces the opioid from the receptor. The deliberate goal is to restore adequate spontaneous ventilation, not full alertness. Over-reversal precipitates acute withdrawal — agitation, vomiting with aspiration risk, catecholamine surge, and, rarely, flash pulmonary edema — and in a moving aircraft an abruptly combative patient is a genuine safety hazard to the crew and the airframe.1,2 The protocol therefore uses small, repeatable doses (adult 1–2 mg IV/IM/IN; pediatric 0.01 mg/kg, max 1 mg) titrated up to effect. The neonatal dose (0.1 mg/kg) is reserved for PPV-refractory respiratory depression with recent maternal opioid exposure, and is avoided when the mother is opioid-dependent because it can precipitate neonatal withdrawal seizures.1

Re-sedation, duration mismatch & infusions

A central pitfall is the duration mismatch: naloxone's clinical effect lasts roughly 30–90 minutes, which is shorter than many opioids — methadone, extended-release oxycodone, and especially the highly lipophilic, high-potency fentanyl analogs now dominating overdoses. A patient who wakes after one dose can re-sedate and stop breathing again after the naloxone wears off.1,2 This is why a single dose plus quick handoff is unsafe in transport and why severe or recurrent toxicity is managed with a continuous infusion: establish the bolus dose that restores breathing, then infuse roughly two-thirds of that dose per hour, titrating down for withdrawal and deferring infusion management to the sending/receiving physician.1 Continuous monitoring of respiratory rate, SpO₂, and ideally EtCO₂ is mandatory throughout, because hypoventilation precedes desaturation.

Consider co-ingestants & the fentanyl era

Many overdoses are not pure opioid. A response that is incomplete despite adequate naloxone should prompt consideration of co-ingestants (benzodiazepines, alcohol, stimulants in "speedballs"), hypoglycemia, hypoxic brain injury, or trauma — naloxone will not reverse these.2 High-potency synthetic opioids can also cause chest-wall rigidity ("wooden chest") that impairs ventilation and may require higher naloxone doses and, occasionally, paralysis with definitive airway control. Trace fentanyl exposure to responders is not a realistic cause of toxicity through intact skin, so resuscitation should not be delayed over decontamination fears.2 Contact Poison Control (1-800-222-1222) for guidance on atypical presentations and infusion management.
