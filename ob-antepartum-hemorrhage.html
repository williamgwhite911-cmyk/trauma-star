Antepartum Hemorrhage

Vaginal bleeding · placenta previa · abruption
TS-OB-002 · Sec 8 Obstetrics
Critical first move
  • No digital vaginal exam until placenta previa is ruled out — can provoke catastrophic hemorrhage.
  • Confirm placental location from current ultrasound (previa / abruption / vasa previa) before any exam.
History & assessment
  • Onset/precipitant of bleeding, EBL, color (pink / bright / dark red), clots.
  • Current ultrasound: placental location, abruption, vasa previa.
  • Meds at home; illicit drug use; bleeding disorders; recent ASA/NSAID; RhoGAM given?
  • Labs: blood type, CBC, PT/PTT, fibrinogen. Accurate I&O.
During transport — resuscitation
  • Maternal vitals q15 min (add temp if blood products given).
  • Second IV — 18 ga or larger for active bleeding or suspected abruption.
  • Fluid replacement (LR or NS): 3 mL per 1 mL EBL; maintain ≥ 125–150 mL/hr.
  • Treat instability/hypotension with fluids, blood products, and TXA per regional practice.
Tocolysis (if contractions)
Magnesium sulfate
6 g IV over 20–30 min loading bolus, then 3–4 g/hr infusion. Titrate q45–60 min to achieve < 1 contraction / 10 min.
Contact medical control (MFM/OB) if…
  • Concerning hemodynamic changes.
  • Strip worsens (Cat I → Cat II/III).
  • Questions on maternal/fetal stability for transport.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Bleeding onset/EBL/character; ultrasound placental findings; that no digital exam was performed.
  • IV access/size, fluids (type/volume), blood products + TXA, serial vitals + temp.
  • FHR/NICHD category trend; medical-control contacts and orders; level-of-care rationale.
Overview — bleeding from two compartments at once

Antepartum hemorrhage (bleeding after ~20 weeks) is dangerous because the pregnant uterus receives 600–700 mL of blood per minute near term — roughly 15% of cardiac output — so a placental bleed can exsanguinate a mother with frightening speed while simultaneously cutting off fetal oxygen.1,2 The two classic causes have opposite exam implications: placenta previa (placenta over the cervical os) bleeds painlessly and bright red, and placental abruption (premature separation) bleeds with pain and uterine tenderness — but abruption blood can be partly concealed behind the placenta, so visible blood badly underestimates true loss.2 A third, rarer entity, vasa previa, is fetal blood and can kill the fetus with even small maternal-appearing bleeding.

Why "no digital exam until previa is excluded"

This is the single most important rule of the protocol. A digital cervical examination in a patient with placenta previa can disrupt the low-lying placenta and trigger torrential hemorrhage.1,2 Therefore the placental location must be confirmed by current ultrasound before any vaginal examination; if previa cannot be excluded, no digital exam is performed and a sterile speculum exam is deferred to the appropriate setting. This is also why the history specifically seeks the most recent ultrasound result and why the crew documents that no digital exam was done.

Recognizing and resuscitating concealed loss

Because abruption loss is frequently concealed and because healthy young pregnant patients compensate until they crash (the increased blood volume of pregnancy masks loss until ~1500–2000 mL is gone), the crew must resuscitate to the clinical picture and the fetal tracing, not to the visible blood.2 The protocol places a second large-bore IV for active bleeding or suspected abruption and replaces volume at roughly 3 mL of crystalloid per 1 mL of estimated loss while moving early toward blood products for instability — consistent with modern hemorrhage practice that crystalloid is a bridge and that ongoing obstetric hemorrhage needs blood, not endless saline.3,4 A deteriorating fetal heart-rate category is often the earliest sign that uteroplacental perfusion is failing, sometimes before maternal vitals change.

Coagulopathy, TXA, and abruption

Abruption is the classic obstetric trigger of disseminated intravascular coagulation — thromboplastin from the disrupted decidua activates the clotting cascade and consumes fibrinogen, so a falling fibrinogen is an ominous marker and a reason the crew notes type/CBC/PT-PTT/fibrinogen.2 Tranexamic acid limits fibrinolysis and is used for instability per regional practice; the strongest obstetric evidence (WOMAN trial) is in postpartum hemorrhage, where early TXA reduces death from bleeding, with greatest benefit when given within 3 hours — the same early-administration principle is applied here.4 Rh status matters too: an Rh-negative mother who bleeds needs Rh immune globulin, so prior RhoGAM administration is documented.

Tocolysis is cautious, not automatic

When contractions accompany bleeding, magnesium sulfate may be used to quiet the uterus (target < 1 contraction per 10 min), but tocolysis in the setting of hemorrhage is deliberate and physician-guided, because contractions can be the body's response to abruption and because beta-agonist tocolytics cause maternal tachycardia that masks and mimics hemorrhagic shock.2,5 Magnesium is favored here for its tocolytic effect without that hemodynamic confusion. The unifying theme of this protocol — confirm placental location, never probe a possible previa, resuscitate to the patient and the fetus rather than to visible blood, and involve MFM/OB early — reflects how quickly a stable-appearing antepartum bleed can become a dual-patient emergency in flight.1,5
