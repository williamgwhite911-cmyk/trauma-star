Standard OB Transport & Supportive Care

Obstetrics · assessment · analgesia · antiemesis
TS-OB-001 · Sec 8 Obstetrics
Assess & prepare
  • OB history: G/P, gestational age, prior pregnancy complications, reason for transport, handover report.
  • Maternal vitals + glucose PRN; uterine tone/activity; FHR/pattern; membrane status; presenting part.
  • EFM strip → categorize with NICHD 3-tier (see TS-OB-007).
  • IV LR 50–100 mL/hr; combined intake 125–150 mL/hr (count drug infusions).
  • Left or right lateral position (uterine displacement); cot straps below the uterus.
In-flight monitoring
  • Maternal SpO₂, RR, HR, BP and uterine tone/activity q15 min.
  • EFM if ≥ 23 wk (or per fetal condition); FHR q15 min. 18–22 wk: FHR at referring + receiving. No EFM → Doppler tones + ask re: fetal movement.
  • O₂ target SpO₂ 96–99%: NC 2–4 L/min if SpO₂ 96–99% and Category I tracing; NRB 10–12 L/min if SpO₂ < 96%, non-Cat-I, or fetal status unknown.

Fentanyl (labor / headache pain)
0.5–1 mcg/kg IBW IV over 1 min, IM, or NAS repeat q1h PRN. Do not give if delivery is possible within 1 hour.

Magnesium toxicity → calcium
  • Suspect if loss of deep-tendon reflexes, muscle weakness, or impaired respiration on a mag infusion.
  • Calcium chloride 0.5–1 g IV over 3 min (repeat ×1 PRN), or calcium gluconate 0.5–1 g IV over 3 min (repeat ×1 PRN).

  • Maternal or fetal condition deteriorates from initial report.

  • GA, G/P, reason for transport, indication for level of care / rotor-wing.

  • Medical-control contacts and orders.
Overview — two patients, one transport

Every obstetric transport is the simultaneous care of two patients, and almost everything that helps the mother's perfusion and oxygenation also helps the fetus. The guiding physiology is uteroplacental: the placenta has essentially no autoregulation, so fetal oxygen delivery tracks maternal cardiac output, oxygen saturation, and the patency of uterine blood flow.1 This is why the standard bundle — lateral positioning, modest IV fluids, titrated oxygen, and continuous monitoring — is not "routine care" but active fetal resuscitation, and why the crew documents maternal vitals, uterine activity, and fetal heart rate together every 15 minutes.1,5

Why left/right lateral positioning

After roughly 20 weeks the gravid uterus compresses the inferior vena cava and aorta when the patient is supine (aortocaval compression), reducing venous return, maternal cardiac output, and therefore uteroplacental perfusion — sometimes dramatically (supine hypotensive syndrome).1,2 Lateral or lateral-tilt positioning relieves this and is one of the highest-yield, zero-cost interventions available in the cabin; it is maintained throughout transport, and the cot straps are placed below the uterus to avoid both compression and restraint injury. Fluids are kept deliberately modest (combined intake ~125–150 mL/hr) because many obstetric patients — especially preeclamptics — are prone to pulmonary edema, so volume is given for a reason (hypovolemia, intrauterine resuscitation) rather than by default.5

Oxygen titrated to the fetal tracing

Oxygen is dosed to a maternal target of SpO₂ 96–99% and escalated based on the fetal heart-rate category, because the relevant endpoint is fetal oxygenation, not maternal comfort.1,5 A reassuring (Category I) tracing with adequate maternal saturation needs only low-flow nasal cannula; a non-reassuring tracing, maternal desaturation, or unknown fetal status prompts a non-rebreather as part of intrauterine resuscitation. Routine high-flow oxygen to a well-oxygenated mother with a normal tracing is avoided, consistent with evidence that it provides no fetal benefit and may not be benign. Categorizing the strip with the standardized NICHD 3-tier system (TS-OB-007) gives the crew and the receiving team a shared, reproducible language for fetal status.3,4

Analgesia, antiemesis & anxiety — with fetal caveats

Pain, nausea, and anxiety all raise maternal catecholamines, which can reduce uterine blood flow, so treating them is part of fetal care — but each agent is chosen with the fetus in mind.5 Acetaminophen is first-line analgesia; fentanyl is reserved for labor or headache pain and is explicitly withheld if delivery is possible within an hour, because transplacental opioid causes neonatal respiratory depression at birth.5 Ondansetron and promethazine control nausea (and pre-empt magnesium-related nausea), and short-acting benzodiazepines (midazolam, lorazepam) manage anxiety in small, observed doses. The unifying principle is the lowest effective dose of a short-acting agent, dosed by ideal body weight, with neonatal effects anticipated.

Magnesium safety — recognizing toxicity early

Magnesium is used widely in OB (tocolysis, seizure prophylaxis, fetal neuroprotection), which means the transport crew is frequently managing an active magnesium infusion and must recognize hypermagnesemia before it becomes life-threatening.5,6 Toxicity progresses in a characteristic order: loss of deep-tendon reflexes first, then muscle weakness and somnolence, then respiratory depression, and finally cardiac effects. Because the reflexes disappear before respiration fails, serial DTR checks are the cheapest early-warning system, and the antidote — IV calcium (chloride or gluconate) — directly antagonizes magnesium at the neuromuscular junction and is given the moment clinically significant toxicity is suspected, alongside stopping the infusion and supporting ventilation.6

Communication & the Keys context

The protocol mandates early contact with the medical director / receiving MFM or OB hospitalist whenever the maternal or fetal picture worsens, when delivery en route becomes a real possibility, or when an intervention is needed that only the sending facility can provide.5 This matters acutely from the Keys, where a several-hour ground option is impractical and the decision to fly an unstable or rapidly-laboring patient must weigh delivery-in-transit against time-to-definitive-care; pre-notifying for neonatal or specialty resources lets the receiving unit be ready on arrival.
