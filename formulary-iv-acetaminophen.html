1. Proposed Dosing (TS-AS-002, Step ①)

PopulationDoseInterval / Max
Adult / adolescent ≥ 50 kg1000 mg IV over 15 minq6h (or 650 mg q4h) · max 4 g/24 h
Patient < 50 kg / pediatric (≥ 2 yr)12.5–15 mg/kg IV over 15 minq6h · max 75 mg/kg/day (≤ 3.75 g)
Reduce max to 3 g/24 hHepatic impairment / chronic alcohol use, malnutrition/low weight, advanced age, or dehydration.

Administration: IV infusion over 15 minutes. May be given concurrently with opioids and/or ketorolac as part of multimodal analgesia. Counts toward the patient's total 24-hour acetaminophen ceiling — reconcile with acetaminophen given prior to arrival (PO/PR/IV, including combination products).

Contraindications / cautions

  • Known acetaminophen hypersensitivity.
  • Severe hepatic impairment / active liver disease (relative — reduce or avoid).
  • Verify cumulative 24-hour acetaminophen dose before administration (avoid stacking with PTA doses / combination analgesics).

2. Clinical Rationale

Opioid-sparing multimodal analgesia. Giving a non-opioid first reduces the total opioid required to reach the pain goal. Randomized and emergency-department data show IV acetaminophen provides effective analgesia and reduces opioid consumption and opioid-related adverse effects.1,2

Safety profile favorable for flight. Unlike opioids, acetaminophen causes no respiratory depression, no sedation, and no hemodynamic effect — so it lowers opioid requirement without adding the very risks (apnea, hypotension) that make opioid titration hazardous in the noisy, vibrating, single-pilot cabin.2

Rapid, reliable onset. The IV route gives predictable plasma levels and faster onset than oral, useful when patients are NPO, vomiting, or in shock with unreliable GI absorption.1,2

Consistent with national pain-management direction (CDC/ACEP opioid-stewardship and multimodal analgesia guidance).3

3. Where It Fits the Protocol

TS-AS-002 is amended so the analgesia ladder reads: ① non-opioid first (IV acetaminophen ± ketorolac) → ② opioid titrated to goal → ③ sub-dissociative ketamine adjunct, with anxiolysis only after pain is controlled. IV acetaminophen does not replace opioids for moderate-severe pain; it lowers the opioid dose needed and is the default first analgesic when not contraindicated.
