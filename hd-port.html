Implanted Port (Port-a-Cath) Access

Central venous port · non-coring (Huber) needle
TS-HD-005 · Sec 2 Hemodynamics & Shock
Use / not
  • Access an existing implanted port when central venous access is needed (meds, fluids, blood) and within scope/protocol.
  • Emergency & no other access: a port is a central line — use it; if untrained/out of scope, use IO instead (don't delay resuscitation).
  • Identify port type — power-injectable "PowerPort" required for power contrast injection; single vs double lumen.
Two hard rules
  • ONLY a non-coring (Huber) needle — a standard needle cores & destroys the septum. Pick a length that reaches the base without tenting (deeper port/obese → longer).
  • 10 mL (or larger) syringes ONLY — small syringes generate high PSI that can rupture the catheter. Never force against resistance.
Procedure — sterile (ANTT)
  1. Mask patient & provider; hand hygiene; gather kit. Prime Huber needle + extension set with NS; clamp.
  2. Palpate & locate the port and septum; assess overlying skin (no infection/breakdown).
  3. Chlorhexidine scrub (back-and-forth 30 s); air-dry fully. Sterile gloves; maintain a sterile field.
  4. Stabilize the port with non-dominant thumb & fingers (triangulate). Insert the Huber perpendicular (90°) through skin & septum until it stops at the back plate. Do not rock/tilt.
  5. Aspirate for blood return to confirm patency/placement; withdraw & discard ~5–10 mL (or per policy) if drawing labs/before flush.
  6. Flush 10–20 mL NS (≥ 10 mL syringe, push-pause). Brisk flush, no resistance, no swelling/pain = good.
  7. Secure the needle with support; apply transparent occlusive dressing; connect the line. Clamp when not infusing (air-embolism precaution).
No blood return or resistance
  • Resistance to flush → STOP (occlusion / fibrin sheath / malposition) — do NOT force (rupture/embolism risk).
  • No blood return but flushes easily: reposition patient (raise arm, turn head, cough, sit up), re-confirm needle is fully seated. If still none, do not give vesicants; use cautiously for crystalloid per medical control, or use another access.
  • Swelling/pain over the port on flush = infiltration (needle not in septum) — stop, remove, reaccess.
Locking / de-access (after use)
  • Flush with NS 10–20 mL, then lock per policy: typically heparin 100 units/mL, 5 mL (saline-only per device IFU). Use positive-pressure technique (clamp while injecting last mL).
  • De-access: stabilize port, withdraw the Huber straight out, apply pressure + small dressing.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Port type (power-injectable?), site, Huber needle gauge/length, number of attempts.
  • Blood return (yes/no), flush volume & ease, dressing; any complication & action.
  • Meds/fluids given via port; lock solution & volume.
Overview — a central line under the skin

An implanted port (Port-a-Cath, MediPort, PowerPort) is a totally implanted central venous catheter: a reservoir with a self-sealing silicone septum sits in a subcutaneous pocket (usually upper chest), with the catheter tip in the SVC/cavoatrial junction.1 It is a true central line, so it offers reliable access for medications, fluids, and blood in patients (oncology, frequent infusion, poor peripheral access) — but it carries the same central-line infection and air-embolism risks and demands strict aseptic technique and correct equipment.1,3 For the transport crew the protocol answers two questions: when to use it and how to access it safely.1,2

When to use it (and when not)

If a patient with a port needs vascular access and accessing it is within the crew's scope and protocol, the port is an excellent route.1,2 In a true emergency with no other access, an already-accessed port can be used immediately; if it is not accessed and the provider is not trained/credentialed to access it, the right move is intraosseous access rather than delaying resuscitation to attempt a sterile port stick.2 Before accessing, the crew identifies the device — single vs double lumen and, importantly, whether it is a power-injectable port (required if a CT contrast power injection is anticipated); non-power ports must not be power-injected.1

The two equipment rules that prevent disasters

Two rules are absolute. First, only a non-coring (Huber) needle may pierce the septum — a standard hollow needle cores a plug of silicone out of the septum, destroying its self-sealing ability and shortening the port's life; the Huber's deflected tip parts the silicone without coring.1 The needle length is chosen so the hub sits flush without tenting the skin (deeper or obese patients need a longer needle). Second, use only 10 mL or larger syringes: a small-barrel syringe generates very high pressure (PSI rises as barrel diameter falls), which can rupture the catheter or dislodge a clot; the larger syringe limits the achievable pressure.1,2 The corollary — never force against resistance — is the most important safety habit in the whole procedure.

Aseptic access & confirming the line

Because the port is central, access uses aseptic non-touch technique: mask the patient and provider, chlorhexidine skin antisepsis with full dry time, and sterile gloves/field — central-line bloodstream infections are serious and largely preventable by technique.3 The crew stabilizes (triangulates) the port and inserts the primed Huber perpendicular through the septum until it bottoms out on the back plate, then aspirates for blood return to confirm the needle is in the reservoir and the line is patent, and flushes briskly with normal saline watching for resistance, swelling, or pain.1,2 A line that draws blood and flushes freely without pain is confirmed; the needle is then secured and covered with a transparent occlusive dressing, and clamped when not infusing to prevent air entrainment.

Troubleshooting, locking & complications

A withdrawal occlusion (flushes but won't draw blood) is common and usually a fibrin sheath or tip position; repositioning the patient (raising the arm, turning the head, coughing, sitting up) often restores return.1 If it still won't draw, vesicants/irritants are withheld and another access is considered. Resistance to flushing means stop — forcing risks catheter rupture and embolism. Pain or swelling over the pocket signals the needle is not in the septum (infiltration) — remove and reaccess. After use the line is flushed and locked per policy (commonly heparin 100 units/mL, 5 mL, or saline for newer devices) using positive-pressure technique to prevent reflux clot.1,2 The crew remains alert for the central-line complications — infection, air embolism (clamp/position precautions), and catheter/port damage — and documents port type, needle, blood return, flush, and any issue for the receiving team.1,3
