Analgesia & Anxiolysis

Multimodal · IV Tylenol first · opioid-sparing
TS-AS-002 · Sec 11 Analgesia & Sedation
Assess & goal
  • Assess pain frequently with an age-appropriate scale (0–10 / CPOT / BPS — see TS-AS-001).
  • Treat to the lowest reasonable pain level balanced with cardiovascular stability & the patient's goal.
  • Monitor SpO₂, EtCO₂ (capnography), RR, BP when giving opioids/sedatives.
① Non-opioid FIRST — multimodal TS addition
IV Acetaminophen (Tylenol / Ofirmev) — give before narcotics
Adult ≥ 50 kg 1000 mg IV over 15 min q6h · < 50 kg / peds 15 mg/kg IV over 15 min (max 1000 mg) q6h. Max 4 g/24 h (3 g/24 h if hepatic risk/elderly/malnourished).
Ketorolac (NSAID adjunct, if not contraindicated)
Adult 15 mg IV over 30–60 sec · Peds 0.5 mg/kg (max 15 mg). Avoid in bleeding/renal injury/known fracture-nonunion concern/pregnancy.
Why
Give the non-opioid first/alongside to reduce opioid requirement (opioid-sparing multimodal analgesia). Acetaminophen does not cause respiratory/hemodynamic depression — valuable in flight. Trauma Star / Medical-Director addition to the PHI ladder — formulary-addition memo / sign-off ›
② Opioids — titrate to pain goal
Fentanyl (preferred if unstable — hemodynamically neutral)
0.5–2 mcg/kg IBW IV over 1 min, IM, NAS (peds same); repeat q10 min PRN.
Morphine
Adult 2–10 mg IV over 1–2 min, IM q20 min · Peds 0.05–0.2 mg/kg (max 10 mg) q10 min.
Hydromorphone
Adult 0.2–1 mg IV over 2 min, IM q15 min (max total 4 mg) · Peds 0.01–0.02 mg/kg (max single 1 mg, total 4 mg).
③ Adjunct — sub-dissociative ketamine
Ketamine (analgesic dose)
0.1–0.3 mg/kg IBW IV over 1–2 min, IM, NAS (max single 30 mg); repeat q10–20 min PRN. Give slowly (fast push → partial dissociation/agitation).
Use for
Opioid-refractory pain; patients on buprenorphine or naltrexone (opioids won't work). Avoid in active psychosis.
Anxiolysis — only AFTER pain controlled
  • Treat pain first; add a benzo for true anxiety. Benzo + opioid = extreme caution (synergistic respiratory depression) — monitor capnography.
  • Midazolam: adult 0.5–5 mg q5 min · peds 0.05–0.1 mg/kg (max 5). Lorazepam: adult 0.5–2 mg · peds 0.05–0.1 mg/kg (max 2).
Therapeutic opioid reversal (over-sedation)
  • Naloxone 0.001–0.01 mg/kg IV/IM/IN, repeat q2–10 min, titrate to restore breathing (not full arousal — preserves analgesia). Airway support may be preferable to reversal.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Pain score before/after; goal set; SpO₂/EtCO₂/RR with opioids.
  • IV acetaminophen (dose/time) and other non-opioids given before/with opioids.
  • Each opioid/ketamine/benzo (dose/route/time/response); reversal if used.
Overview — treat pain, and treat it smartly

Pain is both a humane priority and a physiologic one: untreated pain drives a catecholamine surge that raises heart rate, blood pressure, myocardial oxygen demand, and intracranial pressure, and worsens shock.1 The protocol's goal is the lowest reasonable pain level balanced against cardiovascular stability — fully obtunding a hypotensive trauma patient with opioids can be as harmful as leaving them in agony. The modern approach to achieve good analgesia while minimizing the dangers of opioids is multimodal analgesia: combining agents with different mechanisms so each can be used at a lower, safer dose.2,3

Why IV acetaminophen goes first (Trauma Star addition)

This protocol adds IV acetaminophen as the first rung of the ladder, given before (or together with) opioids — a Trauma Star / Medical-Director enhancement to the PHI analgesia guideline. The rationale is opioid-sparing: IV acetaminophen provides reliable baseline analgesia with a rapid onset, and trials show it reduces opioid requirements and opioid-related side effects.3,4 Critically for the aircraft, acetaminophen has no respiratory depression, no sedation, and no hemodynamic effect — so it lowers the total opioid the patient needs without adding the very risks (apnea, hypotension) that make opioid titration in a noisy cabin hazardous.3,4 Dosing is weight-based (1000 mg ≥ 50 kg; 15 mg/kg if smaller) with a hard 4 g/24 h ceiling (3 g in the elderly, malnourished, or hepatically impaired), and it is paired where appropriate with the NSAID ketorolac as a second non-opioid — both reducing how much narcotic is required to reach the pain goal.3

Opioids — choosing and titrating

When opioids are needed, they are titrated in small, repeated doses to the pain goal rather than given as a single large dose, checking vitals between doses.1 Fentanyl is often preferred in the unstable patient because it is the most hemodynamically neutral opioid (little histamine release) and is fast and short-acting; morphine causes more histamine-mediated vasodilation (avoid in hypotension/shock); hydromorphone is a potent alternative with a defined ceiling. All are dosed by ideal body weight and demand continuous monitoring of respiratory rate and capnography, since the dominant risk is respiratory depression — now reduced by the non-opioid first step.1,2

Sub-dissociative ketamine — the opioid-sparing adjunct

Sub-dissociative (analgesic-dose) ketamine provides potent analgesia through a different (NMDA) mechanism, so it is additive to opioids and useful for opioid-refractory pain and for opioid-sparing.2,3 It is specifically valuable in patients on buprenorphine or naltrexone, whose opioid receptors are blocked so conventional opioids fail. The key technical caveat is that it must be given slowly (over 1–2 minutes or as a short infusion): a rapid push produces partial dissociation — a distressing, hallucinatory, agitated state — whereas slow administration gives clean analgesia without dissociation. It is avoided in active psychosis.1,2

Anxiolysis after analgesia, and reversing over-sedation

A frightened patient is often a patient in pain, so the protocol treats pain first and adds a benzodiazepine only for genuine anxiety once analgesia is adequate.1 The combination of a benzodiazepine plus an opioid is flagged for extreme caution because their respiratory-depressant effects are synergistic — the leading cause of iatrogenic apnea — mandating capnography and airway readiness. Finally, when therapeutic opioids cause excessive respiratory depression, naloxone is used in small titrated doses to restore breathing, not to fully reverse (which would dump the patient into pain and withdrawal); often, simply supporting the airway and ventilation while the opioid wears off is preferable to reversal, especially since naloxone's duration may be shorter than the opioid it is reversing.1
