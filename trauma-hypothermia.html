Hypothermia, Frostbite & Heat Illness

Core temp · staged rewarming
TS-TR-007 · Sec 9 Trauma
All hypothermia — do these
  • Measure core temperature continuously.
  • Remove wet clothing; warm environment; cover with warm dry blankets; warm all IV fluids.
  • Handle gently — rough movement can trigger VF in the cold heart.
Stage & treat
Mild — 32–35 °C
Awake, shivering, uncomfortable, tachycardia, mild amnesia. Warm fluids as tolerated; passive + active external rewarming.
Moderate — 28–32 °C
Altered LOC, minimal shivering, confusion, HR decreasing. Avoid stimulation; thermal blanket; warm oxygen.
Severe — < 28 °C
Obtunded/unconscious, no shivering, bradypnea, severe bradycardia. Active core rewarming (heated O₂, warm gastric lavage, ECMO at center).
Frostbite
  • Treat associated hypothermia first; move to heat, handle gently, warm blankets.
  • Do NOT rub frostbitten areas; bandage with loose sterile dressings.
  • Protect from trauma & partial thawing — if you can't fully thaw and keep thawed, withhold thawing until arrival.
Heat-related illness
  • Move to a cool environment; remove clothing.
  • Active cooling: cold packs / cool cloths to lateral chest walls, groin, axillae.
  • Support airway/perfusion; treat associated seizures/AMS.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Core temp + method, trend; stage; rewarming/cooling measures & response.
  • Cardiac rhythm; gentle-handling precautions; frostbite care (no rubbing / thaw decision).
  • Destination (ECMO/critical care for severe hypothermia).
Overview — temperature is a true vital sign

Accidental hypothermia (core temperature < 35 °C) and heat illness sit at opposite ends of a thermoregulatory spectrum, but both demand that core temperature be measured, not estimated, because skin feel is unreliable and management is staged by the actual core reading.1,3 In trauma, hypothermia is also one corner of the lethal triad (with acidosis and coagulopathy) — a cold patient cannot clot — so preventing and reversing hypothermia is part of hemorrhage control, and the same warming discipline applies whether cold is the primary problem or a complication of injury and resuscitation.2,3

Why "handle gently" is a hard rule

The cold myocardium is electrically irritable and prone to ventricular fibrillation, and below about 30 °C that VF is notoriously resistant to defibrillation and drugs until the patient is rewarmed.1,3 Rough handling, sudden movement, or unnecessary procedures can mechanically trigger VF in a moderately or severely hypothermic patient — which is why the protocol mandates gentle handling, minimal stimulation, and keeping the patient horizontal. It also reframes the cardiac arrest: in profound hypothermia the patient is not dead until "warm and dead," so resuscitation and rewarming continue to a normothermic temperature before outcome is judged, because full neurologic recovery has occurred from extreme hypothermic arrest.1,3

Staged rewarming — matching method to severity

The staging drives the rewarming strategy.1,3 Mild (32–35 °C) patients are still shivering — their own most effective heat generator — so they need insulation, a warm environment, and warm fluids, and they generally recover with passive and active external rewarming. Moderate (28–32 °C) patients have lost shivering and have a slowing heart; they need active external rewarming with care to avoid stimulation and to anticipate "afterdrop" — the transient core-temperature fall and hypotension that occur when cold, acidotic peripheral blood returns centrally during rewarming, which is why warmed core measures and IV fluids are favored.1 Severe (< 28 °C) patients require active core rewarming (warm humidified oxygen, warm IV fluids, warm lavage) and, for arrest or refractory instability, extracorporeal rewarming (ECMO/bypass) — which is a key destination driver, since only select centers offer it.1,3 Warm IV fluids are used throughout because room-temperature fluid actively cools the patient.

Frostbite — protect the tissue, don't make it worse

Frostbite management is dominated by avoiding refreezing and mechanical injury, which cause far more tissue loss than the original freeze.1,4 The frostbitten part is never rubbed (ice crystals shear the tissue) and is dressed loosely and protected from trauma. The pivotal field decision is about thawing: rapid rewarming is the definitive treatment, but a partial thaw followed by refreezing is devastating, so if the crew cannot fully thaw the part and reliably keep it thawed through transport, thawing is deliberately withheld until arrival.4 Underlying systemic hypothermia is always treated first, because a frostbitten extremity in a hypothermic patient is a sign the whole patient is cold.

Heat illness — cool first, fast

At the other extreme, heat stroke (hyperthermia with CNS dysfunction) is a true emergency in which the speed of cooling determines survival and neurologic outcome.5 Field treatment is immediate active cooling — moving to a cool environment, removing clothing, and applying cold packs and cool cloths to the groin, axillae, and lateral chest/neck where large vessels run close to the skin to maximize heat transfer (evaporative and, where feasible, cold-water immersion cooling are most effective).5 Supportive care addresses the airway, perfusion, and any seizures or altered mental status, and cooling continues during transport with monitoring to avoid overshoot into hypothermia.
