Preterm / Active Labor & PROM

Tocolysis · terbutaline · magnesium · ketorolac
TS-OB-004 · Sec 8 Obstetrics
Assess (ask specifically)
  • Home meds: nifedipine, terbutaline, progesterone (17P), ibuprofen, antibiotics.
  • Cervical cerclage (+ date); antenatal steroids (+ dates); history of rapid labor.
  • Labs: fetal fibronectin (fFN), GBS, cervical length; time of last exam.
  • PROM: time of ROM, fluid color/odor; ferning / AmniSure; AFI; GBS.
Sterile exam — only if indicated
  • Sterile speculum/vaginal exam for: urge to push, urge to BM, active labor, bedside-to-bedside time > 30 min.
  • Hips elevated if cervical dilation > 3–4 cm.
Hydration
  • Empiric fluid bolus 250–500 mL for suspected dehydration/infection.
Tocolysis — target < 1 contraction / 10 min
Terbutaline (fast-acting)
0.25 mg SQ, repeat q20–30 min PRN. Use for contractions more frequent than 1 every 6 min (bridge while mag is prepared). Hold if maternal HR > 120.
Magnesium sulfate
6 g IV over 20–30 min loading bolus, then 3–4 g/hr infusion (titrate q45–60 min, up to 4 g/hr). Top up to a full 6 g bolus if < 6 g given PTA. Contraindicated if nifedipine given within the last hour.
Ketorolac (if terbutaline contraindicated)
30 mg IV over 30–60 sec, may repeat ×1 after 60 min.
Analgesia
Fentanyl (labor pain unrelieved by tocolysis)
0.5–1 mcg/kg IBW IV over 1 min, IM, NAS q1h PRN. Not if delivery possible within 1 hour.
Magnesium toxicity → calcium
  • Loss of DTRs / weakness / respiratory depression → calcium chloride or gluconate 0.5–1 g IV over 3 min (repeat ×1 PRN); stop the mag.
Contact medical control (MFM/OB) if…
  • Advancing dilation / labor with possible delivery en route.
  • Strip worsens (Cat I → Cat II/III).
Documentation — medically necessary
  • GA, cerclage, steroid status; contraction frequency/duration + response to tocolytics.
  • PROM: time/color/odor, ferning/AmniSure, GBS; exam findings + indication.
  • All tocolytic/analgesic doses + times + maternal HR; FHR/NICHD trend; MC contacts.
Overview — buying time, not stopping birth forever

Preterm birth (< 37 weeks) is the leading cause of neonatal death and long-term disability, and the goal of preterm-labor management is not to abolish labor permanently but to buy a defined, high-value window of time.1,2 Tocolysis typically prolongs pregnancy only by about 48 hours, but those hours are precisely what allow two interventions that genuinely change neonatal outcome: a course of antenatal corticosteroids (which accelerate fetal lung maturity and reduce respiratory distress, intraventricular hemorrhage, and death) and in-utero transfer to a hospital with an appropriate NICU.1,3 For a Keys transport, that second point is the entire point — moving the fetus while still inside the mother is far safer than transporting a fragile premature neonate after delivery, so quieting the uterus enough to complete the flight has real value.

Why these specific tocolytics — and their trade-offs

No tocolytic is dramatically superior, so the choice is driven by speed and side-effect profile.1 Terbutaline, a beta-agonist, is fast-acting and useful as a bridge for frequent contractions, but its beta-1 activity causes maternal tachycardia, tremor, and (with prolonged use) the risk of pulmonary edema and arrhythmia — hence the hard stop at HR > 120 and its use only as a short bridge.1 Magnesium sulfate provides smoother, sustained tocolysis and carries an additional, important benefit: at preterm gestations it is fetal neuroprotective, reducing the incidence and severity of cerebral palsy (the BEAM trial).4 Ketorolac (an NSAID/prostaglandin inhibitor) is an effective alternative when terbutaline is contraindicated, but NSAIDs are avoided near term and with prolonged use because of fetal ductus arteriosus constriction and oligohydramnios.1

A critical drug interaction — magnesium + nifedipine
  • Do not start magnesium if nifedipine was given within the last hour. Both lower blood pressure and relax smooth muscle, and the calcium-channel blocker potentiates magnesium's neuromuscular blockade — the combination can cause profound hypotension and respiratory/neuromuscular compromise.1,5
  • This is why the home-medication history (specifically nifedipine, terbutaline, progesterone, indomethacin/ibuprofen) is gathered before initiating a tocolytic.
PROM — the bleeding-free emergency of infection and cord

Premature rupture of membranes (PROM/PPROM) shares this protocol because it frequently precedes preterm labor and changes the risk calculus. Once membranes rupture the principal threats are ascending infection (chorioamnionitis) and, especially with a high presenting part or low fluid, cord prolapse.1,6 This drives several protocol choices: minimizing digital exams (each exam introduces bacteria and shortens the latency period), confirming rupture by history plus ferning/AmniSure rather than repeated probing, noting GBS status and antenatal steroid/antibiotic administration, and elevating the hips when dilation exceeds 3–4 cm to reduce cord-compression risk during transport. Foul-smelling fluid, maternal fever, or fetal tachycardia suggests infection and must be communicated.6

When to stop fighting labor

Tocolysis is contraindicated or futile in several situations, and recognizing them prevents harm: advanced cervical dilation with imminent delivery, significant hemorrhage or suspected abruption, chorioamnionitis, and a non-reassuring fetal tracing all argue for preparing for delivery rather than suppressing it.1,5 This is why the protocol pairs tocolysis with explicit triggers to contact MFM/OB — advancing dilation with possible delivery en route, or a strip deteriorating from Category I to II/III — and why the crew documents contraction frequency, duration, and the response to each tocolytic. The judgment of whether to fly, when to fly, and whether to keep tocolyzing is shared with online medical control, because delivering a 28-week neonate in the aircraft is a fundamentally different mission than delivering one in a tertiary L&D.2,5
