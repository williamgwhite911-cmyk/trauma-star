Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)

Pediatric upper airway · steroid + neb epi

Overview — a small airway with a big swelling problem

Croup is a viral (usually parainfluenza) infection causing inflammation and edema of the subglottic airway — the narrowest part of a child's airway, encircled by rigid cricoid cartilage. A few millimeters of swelling there dramatically increases resistance (resistance rises with the fourth power of radius), producing the classic barky cough and inspiratory stridor.1,2 Most croup is mild and self-limited, but a transported child has been escalated for a reason, so the protocol focuses on reducing airway edema and, above all, not making things worse.

Why "keep the child calm" is a treatment

The single most important and most counter-intuitive principle is that agitation worsens obstruction: crying increases turbulent airflow and dynamic collapse of the inflamed airway, can precipitate complete obstruction, and raises oxygen demand.1,2 Therefore the crew minimizes upsetting interventions, leaves the child in a position of comfort with the caregiver, defers non-essential exam and IV placement, and delivers oxygen by blow-by if a mask is refused. In croup, a calm child is a safer airway — this is genuine therapy, not just bedside manner.2

Steroids and nebulized epinephrine — the two effective drugs

Corticosteroids (dexamethasone is the classic agent; methylprednisolone per this protocol) reduce airway inflammation and are the mainstay — they shorten symptoms, reduce return visits and intubation, and benefit even mild croup, so a steroid is given before/at transfer.2,3 Nebulized epinephrine (racemic or L-epinephrine — they are equivalent in effect) provides rapid relief of stridor at rest by α-mediated vasoconstriction that shrinks the swollen mucosa, buying time for the steroid to work.2,3 Its key transport caveat is the rebound phenomenon: the effect wears off in roughly two hours, and the child can return to (or exceed) baseline obstruction — so a child who received nebulized epinephrine must be observed/transported and never discharged from the crew's attention on the basis of transient improvement.2

Intubation, the smaller tube & the dangerous mimics

Intubation is avoided when the child can be managed medically, because instrumenting an already-swollen subglottis can worsen edema; when it is unavoidable, a tube 0.5–1 mm smaller than predicted is selected to pass the narrowed subglottic space.1,2 The crew also stays alert for croup mimics that change the plan: epiglottitis (toxic, drooling, tripoding, no barky cough — do not agitate, prepare for a difficult airway), bacterial tracheitis, and foreign body (sudden onset, choking history). Recognizing these prevents applying the croup pathway to a different, more dangerous airway emergency.2
