Overview — a small ingestion can be lethal

Tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline, nortriptyline, imipramine, doxepin, clomipramine) have a narrow therapeutic index, and overdose remains one of the deadliest common ingestions in toxicology.1 They produce four overlapping toxic effects: fast sodium-channel blockade (the cardiotoxic mechanism — QRS widening and ventricular dysrhythmia), anticholinergic effects (tachycardia, hyperthermia, dilated pupils, dry skin, ileus, agitation/delirium), CNS toxicity (rapid progression from agitation to coma and seizures), and refractory hypotension from α-blockade and direct myocardial depression.1,2 Deterioration is characteristically fast and can be precipitous — a patient who looks well can arrest within an hour — so any significant TCA ingestion is treated as a high-acuity transport with aggressive monitoring and a low threshold to intervene.1

The ECG is the vital sign — why QRS width predicts danger

Because the lethal mechanism is sodium-channel blockade in the His-Purkinje system and myocardium, the QRS duration on the ECG is the best bedside predictor of toxicity — better than the ingested dose or drug level. Classic data (Boehnert & Lovejoy) showed that a QRS > 100 ms predicts seizures and a QRS > 160 ms predicts ventricular dysrhythmias.2 A second clue is a terminal R wave in lead aVR (a tall R / prominent rightward terminal axis), reflecting the same sodium-channel effect.2 This is why continuous ECG monitoring with serial QRS measurement is central to the protocol: a widening QRS is the trigger to act, and a narrowing QRS is the marker that treatment is working. Acidosis and hypoxia worsen sodium-channel blockade, so maintaining oxygenation and ventilation is itself part of cardiac protection.1,2

Sodium bicarbonate — why it is first-line, and how it works

Sodium bicarbonate is the cornerstone antidote and works through two simultaneous mechanisms.1,3 First, the sodium load increases the electrochemical gradient across the blocked fast sodium channels, overcoming the blockade and narrowing the QRS. Second, alkalinization (raising serum pH toward 7.45–7.55) decreases the fraction of drug that is free and bound to the channel. The combined effect narrows the QRS, suppresses dysrhythmias, and supports blood pressure. Dose is adult 50–100 mEq IV over 5–10 min (max single 100 mEq), pediatric 1–2 mEq/kg IV over 5–10 min; one dose may be given on standing order with additional doses requiring a physician order.4 The endpoints are a narrowing QRS and improving hemodynamics, titrated against the risks of over-alkalinization and hypokalemia. The indications to give it — hypotension, wide QRS, ventricular dysrhythmia, or rapid CNS deterioration — all reflect the same underlying cardiotoxicity.1,3

Seizures, hypotension & what to avoid

Seizures are common, raise the danger sharply (they cause acidosis that worsens cardiotoxicity), and are treated with benzodiazepines — phenytoin is avoided as it is both ineffective here and shares sodium-channel effects.1 Hypotension is managed with bicarbonate and IV crystalloid first; for refractory hypotension a direct α-agonist (e.g., norepinephrine) is generally preferred over agents reliant on endogenous catecholamine release.1 Several drug classes must be avoided because they prolong the QRS or QT and can precipitate arrest: Class IA (procainamide, quinidine) and Class IC antiarrhythmics, and other sodium-channel-active agents.1,2 In refractory cardiovascular collapse, IV lipid emulsion and extracorporeal support are considerations at the receiving facility — another reason to expedite transport. Contact Poison Control (1-800-222-1222) early for management guidance.
