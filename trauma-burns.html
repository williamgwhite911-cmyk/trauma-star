Burns

Thermal · electrical · chemical · airway & fluids
TS-TR-006 · Sec 9 Trauma
First — stop the burn, then ABCDE
  • Responder safety; stop the burning process. ABCDE is unchanged from any trauma.
  • Airway is the priority — examine oropharynx for soot, edema, mucosal injury. Inhalation injury can appear hours later.
Early intubation indications (thermal)
  • Hoarseness, stridor, accessory-muscle use, sternal retraction.
  • TBSA burn > 40–50%; extensive/facial burns; burns inside the mouth; difficulty swallowing.
  • Secure the airway early — laryngeal edema makes later intubation impossible.
Fluid resuscitation — deep partial / full thickness > 20% TBSA (LR)
Fixed age-based rate (early phase)
≤ 5 yr 125 mL/hr · 6–13 yr 250 mL/hr · ≥ 14 yr 500 mL/hr LR.
Weight / TBSA (when practicable)
Adult 2 mL × kg × %TBSA · Peds 3 mL × kg × %TBSA LR. ½ over first 8 h, ½ over next 16 h. Peds: add D-containing maintenance fluids.
Titrate to urine output
Adult 30–50 mL/hr · Peds 1 mL/kg/hr. No catheter → use HR/SBP/cap refill/mentation. Avoid over-resuscitation.
Electrical burns
  • LR 4 mL/kg/%TBSA if TBSA > 20%; obtain EKG; urinary catheter if practicable.
  • Cutaneous burns under-represent deep injury — assess for compartment syndrome & contact points.
  • Myoglobinuria: adult 150–200 mL/hr to make urine 75–100 mL/hr; peds 1.5–2× MIVF to make 1.5–2 mL/kg/hr.
Chemical burns
  • PPE; remove clothing/jewelry/contacts that may hold the agent; brush off dry chemical first.
  • Irrigate ≥ 20 min (shower/hose; include eyes) — irrigation is the only thing that stops the burn. Do not soak.
  • Do not transport before thorough decontamination.
Burn transport care
  • Cover burns with clean DRY dressings; replace any wet dressings (they cause hypothermia). Keep patient warm; warm cabin.
  • Monitor distal neurovascular function with circumferential burns; remove jewelry early.
  • Pain control may need higher/more frequent analgesia; monitor urine output.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Time/mechanism, %TBSA & depth, airway findings + airway interventions.
  • Fluid (type/rate/formula) + urine output; EKG & myoglobinuria (electrical); decon details (chemical).
  • Analgesia; temperature management; burn-center destination.
Overview — a burn is still a trauma patient

The dramatic appearance of a burn can distract from the fact that the ABCDE primary survey is unchanged — burn patients die of the same airway, breathing, and circulation problems as other trauma patients, plus burn-specific threats.1,2 After ensuring responder safety and stopping the burning process, the two burn-specific priorities that change outcome in the field are protecting the airway and starting measured fluid resuscitation, while never forgetting concomitant mechanical trauma (especially after explosions, falls, or motor-vehicle fires).1,2

Why the airway is the time bomb

Inhalation of hot gases and combustion products causes progressive laryngeal and airway edema that may be subtle at first and then swell shut over hours. The danger is that intubation gets harder by the minute as edema advances, so the protocol intubates early on suspicion rather than waiting for failure.1,2 The listed indications — hoarseness or stridor, soot/edema/burns inside the mouth, facial burns, difficulty swallowing, accessory-muscle use, and very large (>40–50%) TBSA burns — are all markers that the airway is threatened or that massive fluid shifts will compromise it. A patient who looks comfortable now can have an unobtainable airway in an hour, and in a noisy transport cabin that deterioration is easy to miss — hence the bias toward securing the airway before flight.1,2 (For enclosed-space fires, also treat CO and cyanide — see TS-TOX-002.)

Fluid resuscitation — enough, but not too much

Deep burns cause a systemic capillary leak that drives massive plasma loss and burn shock; replacing it with warmed isotonic crystalloid (LR) maintains organ perfusion.2,3 Resuscitation is indicated for deep-partial/full-thickness burns over ~20% TBSA. Fixed age-based rates allow immediate, calculation-free starting in the field, transitioning to a weight- and TBSA-based estimate (the Parkland-type formula, 2 mL/kg/%TBSA for adults, 3 for children, half in the first 8 hours from the time of injury) when practicable.2,3 Crucially, these formulas are starting points titrated to urine output (adult 30–50 mL/hr; peds 1 mL/kg/hr), because the equally important error is over-resuscitation — "fluid creep" causes pulmonary edema, abdominal and extremity compartment syndromes, and conversion of partial- to full-thickness burns.3 Children additionally need dextrose-containing maintenance fluid because of limited glycogen stores.2

Electrical burns — the iceberg injury

Electrical injury is deceptive: the visible skin wounds at contact points vastly under-represent the deep destruction of muscle and nerve as current passes through the body.2 Two consequences drive the protocol. First, extensive deep-muscle necrosis releases myoglobin, so fluids are run higher (LR 4 mL/kg/%TBSA) and titrated to a brisk urine output (75–100 mL/hr in adults) when myoglobinuria is present to protect the kidneys.2 Second, current depolarizes the heart and can cause life-threatening dysrhythmias, so an EKG and cardiac monitoring are obtained, and the swollen deep compartments must be assessed for compartment syndrome that the cutaneous exam will not reveal.2

Chemical burns & temperature — two field-defining rules

For chemical burns, decontamination by copious irrigation is the only thing that stops the injury, and it must be thorough (≥ 20 minutes, including the eyes) with dry chemicals brushed off first; a patient is not transported until decontaminated, both to halt their injury and to protect the crew and aircraft.1 Finally, the burn patient is exquisitely vulnerable to hypothermia — the loss of skin destroys thermoregulation, and a cold patient becomes coagulopathic and acidotic. This is why burns are covered with clean, dry dressings (any wet dressings applied earlier are removed because they wick away heat), jewelry is removed before swelling, and the cabin is kept warm.1,2 Adequate, often high-dose analgesia is humane and physiologically beneficial, and major burns are routed to a burn center.
