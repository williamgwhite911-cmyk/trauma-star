Stroke — Hemorrhagic (ICH)

Suspected intracerebral hemorrhage
TS-NEU-002 · Sec 6 Neurology
Ischemic Hemorrhagic Undetermined
Recognize — suspect ICH
  • Severe sudden headache ("worst of life"), vomiting, rapid ↓LOC.
  • Very high BP, seizure, or anticoagulated patient.
  • Herniation signs — unequal/blown pupil, posturing, Cushing reflex (HTN + bradycardia + irregular resp).
  • BE-FAST positive; Glucose (rule out mimic); LKW; med/anticoagulant list.
Blood pressure — controlled lowering
ICH with SBP > 160 mmHg
Lower SBP to 130–160 mmHg. Smooth, sustained control; manage with treating provider / medical control where practicable.
Anticoagulant reversal
Assess home/recent anticoagulant use; reversal only under direct medical control. Relay agent + last dose to receiving.
Antihypertensives — choose one (PHI doses)
Nicardipine
5 mg/hr IV, ↑ 2.5 mg/hr q5–15 min PRN, titrate, max 15 mg/hr (may ↓ to 3 mg/hr after target).
Clevidipine
1–2 mg/hr IV; may double q90 s, slow as BP nears goal; max 32 mg/hr.
Labetalol
20 mg IV over 2 min; repeat 40 mg in 10–20 min, then 80 mg q10–20 min (×3) PRN; or 2 mg/min infusion; max 300 mg.
Esmolol
Load 500 mcg/kg IV over 1 min; titrate 50 mcg/kg/min q5 min to target (repeat load before each titration PRN); max 300 mcg/kg/min.
Other management
  • HOB 30°, midline head; avoid hypoxia (SpO₂ ≥ 94%) and hypotension.
  • Airway: high aspiration/↓LOC risk — suction, protect airway; anticipate intubation.
  • Anticipate ↑ICP / herniation; anticoagulation reversal will be needed at the receiving facility — relay agent/last dose.
  • Destination: neurosurgical-capable center; pre-notify.
Cautions & red flags
  • Do not drop SBP < 130 — can cause harm.
  • Avoid hypoxia/hypotension — worsen secondary brain injury.
  • Herniation → elevate HOB, optimize ventilation/oxygenation, notify early; treat per protocol.
  • Confirm it isn't hypoglycemia or seizure mimic.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • LKW / onset time; presenting & serial BP and GCS/pupils.
  • Stroke scale + score; signs suggesting ICH/herniation.
  • Anticoagulant/antiplatelet agent + last dose (critical for reversal).
  • BP intervention (agent/dose) + order source and response.
  • Destination rationale (neurosurgical capability) + pre-notification time.
  • Why rotor-wing / level of care (time-critical, distance to neuro center).
Overview & burden of disease

Spontaneous (non-traumatic) intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH) is bleeding directly into the brain parenchyma, most commonly from chronic hypertensive small-vessel disease or cerebral amyloid angiopathy. Although it represents only about 10–15% of all strokes, it is disproportionately lethal: 30-day mortality approaches 40%, and a large share of deaths occur in the first 24–48 hours.1 Unlike ischemic stroke, there is no reperfusion therapy to deliver — the prehospital and transport priorities are to limit ongoing bleeding (hematoma expansion), prevent secondary brain injury, and reach a neurosurgical-capable center quickly with the information the receiving team needs to act.1,2

Why early hematoma expansion drives outcome

The strongest modifiable predictor of death and disability in ICH is hematoma expansion, which occurs in roughly a third of patients within the first hours and is a direct mechanism of clinical deterioration. Elevated blood pressure promotes continued bleeding and expansion, which is the rationale for early, controlled BP lowering.1,3 The INTERACT2 trial showed that intensively lowering SBP toward < 140 mmHg was safe and associated with better functional outcomes,3 while ATACH-2 demonstrated that aggressive lowering into the 110–139 range provided no additional benefit and signaled renal and ischemic harm — establishing that there is a floor below which lowering becomes counter-productive.4 The practical synthesis: lower elevated BP early and smoothly, but do not over-shoot, and avoid the large swings (variability) that independently worsen outcome.1,3,4

Blood-pressure targets & why "smooth" matters

Per PHI guidance, for acute ICH presenting with SBP 150–220 mmHg, the target is roughly 130–150 mmHg, avoiding a drop below ~130; for SBP > 220, reduce by about 20% in the first hour with continuous, titratable control.5 Treatment is most beneficial when started within ~2 hours of onset and the target is reached within ~1 hour, because the bleeding window is early.1,3 Smoothness is itself therapeutic: abrupt drops can fall below the cerebral autoregulatory range and cause perilesional ischemia, while rebound surges can re-provoke bleeding. This is why titratable agents (nicardipine, clevidipine, labetalol, esmolol) delivered by infusion are preferred over bolus-only strategies, and why continuous arterial or frequent cycled BP monitoring is mandatory in transport.1,5

Anticoagulation reversal — why the medication history is urgent

A substantial proportion of ICH patients are on anticoagulants or antiplatelets, and these agents both increase initial bleed severity and drive expansion. Reversal is time-critical and definitive treatment (e.g., 4-factor PCC and vitamin K for warfarin, idarucizumab for dabigatran, andexanet or PCC for factor-Xa inhibitors) begins at the receiving facility — so the single most useful thing the transport crew can do is obtain and relay an accurate medication history, including last dose, so reversal can be drawn up and given on arrival rather than after a delay.1 Documenting the anticoagulant/antiplatelet status and pre-notifying the neurosurgical team converts ground we cannot make up later.1,2

Secondary injury, ICP & positioning

After the primary bleed, outcome is shaped by prevention of secondary insults. Maintain oxygenation (SpO₂ ≥ 94%) and normocapnia, avoid hypotension, treat hyperthermia, and correct hypoglycemia — each independently worsens injured brain.1,5 Elevate the head of bed to ~30° to promote venous drainage and lower intracranial pressure, and protect the airway early in any patient with a declining level of consciousness, since loss of airway reflexes plus hypercarbia rapidly raises ICP.1 Signs of expansion or rising ICP / herniation — declining GCS, new pupillary asymmetry, Cushing response (hypertension with bradycardia), or posturing — should trigger the herniation pathway and immediate communication with the receiving neurosurgeon.1

In-flight considerations & destination
  • Continuous BP and neuro monitoring; titrate to a smooth target rather than abrupt drops.
  • HOB ~30°, early airway protection in declining LOC; avoid hypoxia, hypotension, and hyperthermia.
  • Destination must have neurosurgery and ICH capability; pre-notify and relay anticoagulation status and last dose for reversal.
  • From the Keys, rotor-wing transport removes 3+ hours of ground time — decisive in a disease where deterioration is measured in hours.
