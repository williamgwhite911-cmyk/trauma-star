Stroke — Hemorrhagic (ICH)

Suspected intracerebral hemorrhage
TS-NEU-002 · Sec 6 Neurology
Ischemic Hemorrhagic Undetermined
Recognize — suspect ICH
  • Severe sudden headache ("worst of life"), vomiting, rapid ↓LOC.
  • Very high BP, seizure, or anticoagulated patient.
  • Herniation signs — unequal/blown pupil, posturing, Cushing reflex (HTN + bradycardia + irregular resp).
  • BE-FAST positive; Glucose (rule out mimic); LKW; med/anticoagulant list.
Blood pressure — controlled lowering
ICH with SBP > 160 mmHg
Lower SBP to 130–160 mmHg. Smooth, sustained control; manage with treating provider / medical control where practicable.
Anticoagulant reversal
Assess home/recent anticoagulant use; reversal only under direct medical control. Relay agent + last dose to receiving.
Antihypertensives — choose one (PHI doses)
Nicardipine
5 mg/hr IV, ↑ 2.5 mg/hr q5–15 min PRN, titrate, max 15 mg/hr (may ↓ to 3 mg/hr after target).
Clevidipine
1–2 mg/hr IV; may double q90 s, slow as BP nears goal; max 32 mg/hr.
Labetalol
20 mg IV over 2 min; repeat 40 mg in 10–20 min, then 80 mg q10–20 min (×3) PRN; or 2 mg/min infusion; max 300 mg.
Esmolol
Load 500 mcg/kg IV over 1 min; titrate 50 mcg/kg/min q5 min to target (repeat load before each titration PRN); max 300 mcg/kg/min.
Other management
  • HOB 30°, midline head; avoid hypoxia (SpO₂ ≥ 94%) and hypotension.
  • Airway: high aspiration/↓LOC risk — suction, protect airway; anticipate intubation.
  • Anticipate ↑ICP / herniation; anticoagulation reversal will be needed at the receiving facility — relay agent/last dose.
  • Destination: neurosurgical-capable center; pre-notify.
Cautions & red flags
  • Do not drop SBP < 130 — can cause harm.
  • Avoid hypoxia/hypotension — worsen secondary brain injury.
  • Herniation → elevate HOB, optimize ventilation/oxygenation, notify early; treat per protocol.
  • Confirm it isn't hypoglycemia or seizure mimic.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • LKW / onset time; presenting & serial BP and GCS/pupils.
  • Stroke scale + score; signs suggesting ICH/herniation.
  • Anticoagulant/antiplatelet agent + last dose (critical for reversal).
  • BP intervention (agent/dose) + order source and response.
  • Destination rationale (neurosurgical capability) + pre-notification time.
  • Why rotor-wing / level of care (time-critical, distance to neuro center).
Overview

Spontaneous intracerebral hemorrhage is bleeding into the brain parenchyma; it carries high early mortality and frequently expands in the first hours. Early, smooth blood-pressure control and rapid delivery to a neurosurgical-capable center are key.1

Pathophysiology / rationale

Higher blood pressure promotes hematoma expansion, so early controlled lowering reduces growth and improves outcomes; however, dropping SBP below ~130 mmHg can compromise perfusion and cause harm. Treatment is most beneficial when started within 2 hours and target reached within about 1 hour, with smooth control avoiding large BP variability.1

In-flight considerations
  • Continuous BP and neuro monitoring; titrate to a smooth target rather than abrupt drops.
  • HOB 30°, airway protection in ↓LOC; avoid hypoxia/hypotension.
  • Destination must have neurosurgery; pre-notify and relay anticoagulation status for reversal.
Management — detail

For acute ICH with presenting SBP 150–220 mmHg, target roughly 130–150 and avoid dropping below 130; for SBP > 220, reduce by about 20% in the first hour with smooth, sustained control.1 Protect the airway, elevate the head, and avoid secondary insults (hypoxia, hypotension, hyperthermia). Obtain online medical control for any BP agent, and convey the anticoagulant/antiplatelet history so reversal can begin promptly on arrival.1
