Vascular Emergencies

AAA · aortic dissection · endoleak
TS-MED-010 · Sec 10 Medical
General
  • Physician should have a confirmed diagnosis before crew implements these. Large-bore access for resuscitation/blood.
  • Monitor ECG, SpO₂, EtCO₂, NIBP; review 12-lead, labs, imaging.
Ruptured AAA — permissive hypotension
  • Cautious fluid/blood resuscitation — titrate to a palpable radial pulse / SBP 80–100 & normal mentation. Avoid over-resuscitation.
  • Manage hypothermia/acidosis/coagulopathy. Definitive Rx = emergent vascular surgery — fastest route to the OR.
Aortic dissection — control shear (impulse)
  • BP in both arms; treat the higher. Avoid IV in a pulse-deficit limb. Treat pain (opioids) — pain drives BP.
  • Goal: lowest HR & SBP that maintains perfusion. Beta-blocker FIRST → then vasodilator.
① Beta-blocker (target HR < 60) — choose one
Esmolol
500 mcg/kg load over 1 min, then 50 mcg/kg/min (↑q5 min, re-load PRN, max 300 mcg/kg/min).
Labetalol
20 mg IV → 40 mg → 80 mg q10–20 min (max 300) or 2 mg/min infusion.
Metoprolol
5 mg slow IV q5 min PRN (max 15 mg).
② Vasodilator if SBP > 120 after β-blockade
  • Only after beta-blockade (vasodilator alone → reflex tachycardia → ↑ shear → false-lumen propagation).
  • Nicardipine (preferred) 5 mg/hr, ↑2.5 q5–15, max 15; Clevidipine 1–2 mg/hr, max 32; Nitroprusside 0.3–10 mcg/kg/min (keep ≤ 2 to avoid toxicity). Target SBP 100–120.
Leaking endovascular (endoleak) / dissection w/ shock
  • Endoleak SBP > 120: short-acting agents (esmolol/nicardipine/clevidipine), target SBP 90–120, HR 60–80; consult surgeon.
  • Dissection + hypotension/shock → manage per CV-instability (impending rupture/tamponade) — be ready to stop antihypertensives & resuscitate.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Diagnosis/imaging; bilateral arm BP + pulse deficits; pain.
  • Drugs (β-blocker then vasodilator) w/ HR/BP response; resuscitation for ruptured AAA.
  • Vascular-surgery destination + pre-notification.
Overview — two opposite hemodynamic problems

This protocol covers acute aortic catastrophes that are highly time-dependent and surgical. They split into two hemodynamic strategies that are nearly opposite, so getting the diagnosis right matters: the ruptured AAA is a bleeding problem managed with permissive hypotension and rapid transport to the OR, while the intact aortic dissection is a shear-stress problem managed by aggressively lowering heart rate and blood pressure.1,2 Both require a physician-confirmed diagnosis before the crew applies these therapies, large-bore access, and the fastest feasible route to vascular surgery.

Ruptured AAA — don't pop the clot

A ruptured abdominal aortic aneurysm is exsanguinating into the retroperitoneum, and the only definitive treatment is emergent surgical/endovascular repair — so the transport clock is everything.1 Resuscitation is deliberately cautious (hypotensive/permissive): aiming for a palpable radial pulse / SBP ~80–100 and preserved mentation rather than a normal pressure, because aggressively normalizing the blood pressure raises hydrostatic pressure across the contained rupture and blows out the tamponading clot, converting a contained leak into free hemorrhage.1 The same lethal-triad discipline as trauma applies — manage hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy — and blood products are preferred over crystalloid. The destination is a facility that can take the patient straight to the OR.

Aortic dissection — impulse control (dP/dt)

In aortic dissection, blood tears into the aortic wall creating a false lumen that can propagate, occlude branch vessels, rupture, or cause tamponade.2 Propagation is driven by the force and rate of the pulse wave hitting the intimal flap — the rate of pressure rise (dP/dt) — not just the peak pressure. This is why the protocol's primary target is the heart rate (< 60) via a beta-blocker FIRST, which reduces both the force of ventricular contraction and the rate, lowering shear stress; then, only if SBP remains > 120, a vasodilator is added to reach an SBP of ~100–120.2 The order is non-negotiable: giving a vasodilator before beta-blockade causes reflex tachycardia and increased contractile force, paradoxically increasing aortic wall stress and extending the dissection.2 Pain is treated aggressively because pain itself drives catecholamines and blood pressure.

Two limbs, two pressures — and where not to put the IV

A dissection can compromise flow to one or more arteries, so the blood pressure may differ markedly between arms; the protocol measures both arms and titrates to the higher pressure, because that reflects true central perfusion (the low-reading arm may simply have a compromised subclavian).2 IV lines and pressure monitoring are avoided in a limb with a pulse deficit, since drug delivery and readings there are unreliable. The drug toolkit favors titratable, short-acting agents — esmolol (very short half-life, easy to back off), labetalol (combined α/β), and metoprolol for beta-blockade; nicardipine (preferred), clevidipine, or nitroprusside for vasodilation — so therapy can be dialed precisely to the "lowest HR and BP that maintains end-organ perfusion."2

When the dissection patient crashes & endoleaks

If a dissection patient becomes hypotensive, the situation has changed from "control the pressure" to "impending rupture, tamponade, or severe aortic regurgitation" — the crew must be ready to stop the antihypertensives and resuscitate per the cardiovascular-instability pathway and expedite to surgery.2 A related entity, the leaking endovascular repair (endoleak), is managed similarly with short-acting agents to a slightly higher target (SBP 90–120, HR 60–80) and close surgical consultation, always prepared to switch to volume/blood resuscitation if the patient suddenly deteriorates.1,2 In all of these, the transport mission is to buy time and protect the aorta until the surgeon can.
