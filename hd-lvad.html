LVAD Transport

Continuous-flow LVAD · MCS interfacility
TS-HD-003 · Sec 2 Hemodynamics & Shock
☎️
CALL the patient's VAD Coordinator / implanting center FIRST
They guide device troubleshooting & parameters — get the number from the patient/family/sending facility.
Before you move — bring ALL equipment
  • Controller (on patient) + BACKUP controller, ≥ 2 fully charged batteries + spares, power module/AC cables, and the patient's VAD travel bag. Never separate the patient from the controller.
  • Keep the pump powered & running at all times. Do not disconnect the driveline. Secure connections against vibration.
  • Bring the patient's device type, baseline parameters, and the coordinator number.
Assess — continuous flow = no normal pulse
  • Often NO palpable pulse; SpO₂ & NIBP unreliable. Confirm the pump is running by auscultating a continuous "hum" over the chest.
  • BP = MAP by Doppler (manual cuff + Doppler over brachial/radial — first sound = MAP). Target MAP 70–90 (avoid > 90).
  • Judge perfusion by mentation, skin, cap refill, EtCO₂, urine output — not pulse.
  • Read the controller: speed (RPM), flow (L/min), power (W), pulsatility index (PI), and any alarms.
Troubleshoot alarms (call coordinator)
Low flow / low PI · "suction" alarm
Usually hypovolemia (bleeding, dehydration) or RV failure → give IV fluid/blood; coordinator may lower pump speed. Check for tamponade.
High power / dark (tea-colored) urine
Suggests pump thrombosis / hemolysis — notify coordinator + receiving; expedite.
Power / controller alarm
Swap to charged battery / AC power; if controller fault → change to backup controller per the device's procedure (coordinator-guided).
Common problems & treatment
  • Bleeding — patients are anticoagulated (warfarin ± antiplatelet); GI/intracranial bleed common. Resuscitate with blood (TS-HD-002); relay to receiving.
  • Arrhythmias (VT/VF) may be surprisingly tolerated (LVAD maintains flow) — treat per ACLS; you MAY defibrillate (pads anterior-posterior, clear of the device). Treat reversible causes.
  • RV failure — supports filling: cautious fluids, pressor/inotrope per medical control; avoid hypoxia/acidosis.
CPR / arrest
  • First confirm the pump is running (hum) and powered, and fix any device cause — most "arrests" are device/power problems.
  • If no signs of life (unresponsive, no perfusion, EtCO₂ low/absent) AND the pump cannot be restarted → start chest compressions. Do NOT withhold CPR from a truly arrested, non-perfusing LVAD patient.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Device type, baseline + transport parameters (RPM/flow/power/PI), alarms & actions; coordinator contact.
  • Doppler MAP trend, perfusion signs; meds/fluids/blood; anticoagulation status.
  • Equipment carried (controller/backup/batteries); destination (implanting/VAD center).
Overview — a pump that changes the rules

A continuous-flow left ventricular assist device (LVAD — e.g., HeartMate 3, HeartMate II, HeartWare) is a surgically implanted pump that draws blood from the left ventricle and propels it into the aorta in a continuous, largely non-pulsatile stream.1,2 That single fact upends normal assessment: there is often no palpable pulse, and pulse oximetry and standard automated blood-pressure cuffs frequently fail. The transport mission is to keep the pump running and powered, assess perfusion by other means, troubleshoot device alarms with the patient's VAD center, and treat the patient's physiology — bleeding, arrhythmia, RV failure — while moving to definitive care.1,2

First call the VAD coordinator — and bring everything

The patient's VAD coordinator / implanting center knows the device, its baseline parameters, and its specific troubleshooting steps, so contacting them early is the highest-yield action and is built into the protocol.1 Equally important is equipment: the patient must never be separated from the controller, and the crew brings the backup controller, multiple fully charged batteries, power cables, and the patient's travel bag — a dead battery with no spare is a preventable arrest. All connections are secured against the vibration of flight, and the driveline is never disconnected. Keeping the pump running and powered is the non-negotiable foundation of every other step.1,2

Assessing the non-pulsatile patient

Because there is no reliable pulse, the crew confirms the pump is working by auscultating the continuous mechanical hum over the precordium, and measures blood pressure as a mean arterial pressure using a manual cuff and a Doppler (the pressure at which flow first returns over the brachial/radial artery approximates the MAP).1,2 The usual target is a MAP of about 70–90 mmHg; hypertension (MAP > 90) increases afterload and stroke risk and is avoided. Perfusion is judged clinically — mental status, skin, capillary refill, end-tidal CO₂, and urine output — and the controller display is read for speed, flow, power, and pulsatility index, the numbers that reveal what the pump is doing.1

Reading the alarms — what the numbers mean

Two patterns are worth knowing. A low-flow / low-PI or "suction" alarm usually means the pump is out-running its filling — most often from hypovolemia (bleeding, dehydration) or right-ventricular failure — so the response is volume (fluid or blood) and, on coordinator guidance, reducing pump speed, while excluding tamponade.1,2 High power, especially with dark, tea-colored urine (hemolysis), suggests pump thrombosis, an emergency that is relayed to the coordinator and receiving center. Power and controller alarms are addressed by switching to a charged battery/AC power or, for a controller fault, exchanging to the backup controller per the device's procedure. The unifying principle: most LVAD "arrests" are device or power problems, and fixing the device restores the patient.1

Bleeding, arrhythmia, RV failure & the CPR question

LVAD physiology shapes the medical care. Patients are anticoagulated, so GI and intracranial hemorrhage are common and are resuscitated with blood products.1 Ventricular arrhythmias may be remarkably well tolerated because the pump keeps flowing, but they still need treatment, and defibrillation is permitted (pads placed clear of the device).2 Right-heart failure is managed by supporting RV filling and contractility and avoiding hypoxia/acidosis. The historically fraught question of chest compressions has a current answer: first confirm the pump is running and powered and correct any device cause, but if the patient has no signs of life and the pump cannot be restarted, chest compressions are performed — withholding CPR from a truly arrested, non-perfusing patient over a theoretical risk of dislodging the device is not supported.2,3 Throughout, the patient is taken to a VAD-capable / implanting center.
