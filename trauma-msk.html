Musculoskeletal & Extremity Trauma

Fractures · pelvis · open fx antibiotics · amputation
TS-TR-004 · Sec 9 Trauma
Always — neurovascular checks (CMS)
  • Check circulation, motion, sensation distal to injury before AND after every manipulation/splint. (SpO₂ on the limb can gauge distal perfusion.)
  • Notify the receiving facility of any extremity injury with absent distal pulses.
Fractures & splinting
  • Realign deformed long-bone fractures with steady traction/rotation; splint joint above and below.
  • Severe angulation / presumed fx with absent pulse / dislocation with absent pulse: realign with traction & rotation — max 2 attempts.
  • Never apply traction/rotation/realignment to elbow injuries.
Femur fracture + traction splint
  • Steady traction + rotation to alignment; splint with the other leg or backboard.
  • Traction device (KTD / Slishman / Optimum) only for well-aligned mid-shaft femur fx.
  • Contraindicated: hip fx/injury, knee dislocation/injury, ankle injury.
Pelvic fracture / unstable pelvis
  • Stabilize with SAM pelvic sling, commercial device, or sheet wrap.
  • Do NOT place a urinary catheter with suspected pelvic fx or blood at the meatus/perineum — risk of urethral injury.
Open fracture — antibiotics early (choose ONE)
  • DO NOT give if allergic to cephalosporins — Ancef (cefazolin), Ceclor (cefaclor), cefdinir (Omnicef), Keflex (cephalexin) → use Clindamycin instead.
Cefazolin (Ancef) — first-line
Adult / ≥ 80 kg: 2 g in 50 mL NS over 10 min. Peds > 1 yr: 25 mg/kg IV (max 2 g) by actual weight — give 2 g if ≥ 80 kg regardless of age. (20 kg = 500 mg · 40 kg = 1 g · 60 kg = 1.5 g · ≥ 80 kg = 2 g)
Ceftriaxone (Rocephin) — alternative (per protocol)
Adult / ≥ 40 kg: 2 g in 50 mL NS over 10 min. Peds > 28 days: 50 mg/kg IV (max 2 g) by actual weight — give 2 g if ≥ 40 kg regardless of age. (10 kg = 500 mg · 20 kg = 1 g · 30 kg = 1.5 g · ≥ 40 kg = 2 g)
Clindamycin (cephalosporin allergy)
Adult 900 mg IV over 30 min · Peds 10 mg/kg IV over 10–30 min (max 900 mg) — rate ≤ 30 mg/min.
Cefazolin is first-line; reserve ceftriaxone per institutional protocol. Control bleeding, realign, splint; cover wound with saline-soaked gauze. Any skin opening near a fracture = open fracture.
Amputation · avulsion · impaled · eye
  • Amputated part: rinse → wrap in NS-dampened gauze → sealed dry bag → bag into ice + water (do NOT freeze / direct ice).
  • Avulsion: rinse debris, replace tissue in normal position, dress.
  • Impaled object: do NOT remove — stabilize in place with bulky dressing.
  • Eye: loose sterile dressing + shield; cover both eyes PRN to limit movement; remove contacts.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • CMS before & after each manipulation/splint; pulse status changes.
  • Open fx + antibiotic (drug/dose/time); realignment attempts; pelvic binder; tourniquet time.
  • Amputated-part care/handoff; analgesia; destination.
Overview — limbs are about perfusion and contamination

Most extremity injuries are not immediately life-threatening, so after catastrophic bleeding is controlled (see TS-TR-001) the orthopedic priorities shift to two goals that determine whether the limb survives and stays functional: preserving distal perfusion and preventing infection.1,5 The unifying habit that protects the limb is the neurovascular (CMS) check before and after every intervention — it both detects a perfusion problem and documents that a maneuver did not create one.1,5

Why realign — and the two-attempt / no-elbow rules

A grossly deformed fracture or dislocation can kink or compress the artery and nerve running past it, threatening the limb. Gentle realignment with steady traction and rotation restores both length and, often, the distal pulse, and it reduces pain and bleeding — so a deformed long bone, a fracture with an absent distal pulse, or a dislocation with an absent pulse is realigned.1,5 The protocol caps this at two attempts because repeated forceful manipulation causes more soft-tissue and neurovascular injury, and a limb that stays pulseless needs the vascular surgeon, not more attempts. The elbow is the explicit exception — its tightly bound brachial artery, median nerve, and complex joint make blind manipulation uniquely dangerous, so elbow injuries are splinted as found and reassessed, not reduced in the field.1

Femur fractures and traction splints

A femur fracture can sequester a liter or more of blood into the thigh and is intensely painful; traction splinting realigns the bone, reduces the thigh compartment volume to tamponade bleeding, relieves pain, and prevents the bone ends from causing further injury.1 But traction devices are appropriate only for isolated, well-aligned mid-shaft femur fractures — they are contraindicated with hip, knee, or ankle injury because applying longitudinal traction across an injured joint (or a hip fracture) pulls against the wrong structures and worsens the injury.1 When a traction device is contraindicated or unavailable, the limb is splinted to the other leg or a board in anatomic position.

Pelvic fractures — bind it, don't catheterize it

An unstable pelvic fracture can cause massive, often concealed retroperitoneal hemorrhage from the disrupted venous plexus and bone ends. A pelvic binder (SAM sling or sheet) closes the pelvic ring volume and stabilizes clot, and is applied for any likely or suspected pelvic fracture — it is a hemorrhage-control device, not just a splint.1,2 The protocol's firm rule against placing a urinary catheter when a pelvic fracture is suspected or there is blood at the urethral meatus/perineum exists because pelvic fractures frequently injure the urethra, and blind catheter passage can convert a partial urethral tear into a complete one — a serious, avoidable complication.1,2

Open fractures — why antibiotics are time-critical

Any skin breach near a fracture is treated as an open fracture, meaning the bone is contaminated and at high risk of osteomyelitis and deep infection. The strongest modifiable predictor of that infection is time to antibiotics: early administration markedly reduces infection rates, and the benefit is large enough that antibiotics are given in the field rather than deferred to the hospital.3,4 A first-generation cephalosporin (cefazolin) covers the relevant skin flora and is first-line, with weight-based dosing; clindamycin is the alternative for penicillin/cephalosporin allergy.3,4 The wound is otherwise handled to minimize further contamination and drying — bleeding controlled, gross alignment restored, and the wound covered with saline-soaked gauze — while definitive irrigation and debridement await the operating room.

Amputations, impaled objects & the eye

An amputated part is salvageable if preserved correctly: rinsed, wrapped in saline-dampened gauze, sealed in a dry bag, and placed on ice water — never in direct contact with ice or frozen, because freezing destroys the tissue and makes replantation impossible.1 An impaled object is left in place and stabilized, because it may be tamponading a vessel and removal can trigger uncontrolled hemorrhage. Ocular injuries are protected with a shield (not a pressure patch) to avoid pressure on a possibly ruptured globe, and covering both eyes limits the consensual movement of the injured eye.1 Throughout, adequate analgesia is humane and reduces the catecholamine surge that worsens bleeding, and any extremity with absent distal pulses is flagged to the receiving team so a vascular service can be ready.
