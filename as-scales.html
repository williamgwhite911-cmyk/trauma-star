Pain & Sedation Scales

CPOT · BPS · RASS · SAS
TS-AS-001 · Sec 11 Analgesia & Sedation
Which scale to use
  • Pain — can self-report: 0–10 numeric scale.
  • Pain — cannot self-report: CPOT (0–8) or BPS (3–12).
  • Sedation/agitation: RASS (−5 to +4) or SAS (1–7).
  • Assess & document before and after every analgesic/sedative; set a target (e.g., post-intubation RASS −1 to −5).
CPOT (0–8) — 4 categories, 0–2 each
Facial expression
0 relaxed · 1 tense (frown) · 2 grimacing (+ eyes shut).
Body movements
0 absence/relaxed · 1 protection (guarding/rubbing) · 2 restlessness (thrashing, striking).
Muscle tension
0 relaxed · 1 tense/rigid · 2 very rigid (can't complete passive movement).
Ventilator compliance (intubated) / Vocalization (not)
0 tolerating / normal tone · 1 coughs but tolerates / sighing-moaning · 2 fighting vent / crying out.
BPS (3–12) — 3 items, 1–4 each
  • Facial: 1 relaxed → 4 grimacing.
  • Upper limbs: 1 no movement → 4 permanently retracted.
  • Ventilator compliance: 1 tolerating → 4 unable to control.
RASS (−5 to +4)
  • +4 combative · +3 very agitated · +2 agitated · +1 restless · 0 alert & calm.
  • −1 drowsy · −2 light · −3 moderate · −4 deep · −5 unarousable.
SAS (1–7)
  • 7 dangerous agitation · 6 very agitated · 5 agitated · 4 calm/cooperative · 3 sedated · 2 very sedated · 1 unarousable.
Why scales — you can't titrate what you don't measure

Pain and sedation are subjective, and in the loud, vibrating, time-pressured transport cabin they are easy to under- or over-treat. Validated scales convert a vague impression into a reproducible number that any crew member or the receiving team interprets the same way, which is the basis for titrating drugs to a defined target and for detecting trends.1,2 The principle that anchors the analgesia/sedation protocols is simple: assess, set a goal, treat, reassess — and document the score before and after each medication.1

Matching the tool to the patient

The correct scale depends on what the patient can do. An awake patient self-reports on a 0–10 numeric scale — the gold standard, because pain is whatever the patient says it is.1 A patient who cannot self-report (intubated, sedated, delirious) is assessed with an observational behavioral pain scale — the CPOT or BPS — which score facial expression, movement/muscle tension, and ventilator interaction, all validated against the patient's true pain state.2 Separately, depth of sedation/agitation is measured with RASS or SAS, which run from dangerous agitation through calm to unarousable.3 Pain and sedation are distinct axes — a patient can be deeply sedated yet in pain — which is why both a pain scale and a sedation scale are used together in the intubated patient.1,2

The critical limitation — paralysis erases the score

Every behavioral scale measures behaviors a paralyzed patient cannot display. A patient who has received a neuromuscular blocker may be wide awake and in agony yet score a perfect "comfortable" on CPOT/BPS/RASS/SAS because they physically cannot frown, move, or fight the ventilator.1,2 This is one of the most important safety concepts in transport sedation: in the paralyzed or otherwise unassessable patient, scales are invalid, and the crew must instead provide empiric, targeted-dose analgesia and sedation rather than waiting for signs that can never appear (see TS-AS-004). Recognizing when a score is meaningless is as important as knowing how to calculate it.1

Targets & trends

Scales also define the goal of therapy. For post-intubation care the protocol targets a RASS of −1 to −5 (calm-to-deep depending on the clinical need), titrating drugs up or down to hold that target rather than chasing a moving picture.1,3 Trends matter more than single values — a rising RASS or CPOT predicts impending agitation and prompts pre-emptive treatment before a patient becomes dangerous in flight, while an over-deep score prompts lightening sedation to avoid hypotension and prolonged recovery. Used this way, the scales are not paperwork; they are the control loop for safe, effective analgesia and sedation.1
