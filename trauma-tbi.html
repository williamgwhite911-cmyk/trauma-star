Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

Avoid hypoxia & hypotension · CPP
TS-TR-002 · Sec 9 Trauma
The two killers — avoid at all costs
  • Hypoxia and hypotension each raise TBI mortality up to 6×. A single episode of either worsens outcome.
  • Use high FiO₂ and aggressive resuscitation to prevent and treat both.
Targets
Oxygenation
SpO₂ > 90% (avoid any hypoxia; high FiO₂).
Blood pressure
SBP 100–110 mmHg for TBI with altered mental status (higher than the general trauma SBP ~90 target — protects cerebral perfusion).
Ventilation (intubated)
EtCO₂ 35–45 mmHg (normocapnia). 30–35 only if herniation signs.
Position
Head midline, HOB up 15–30°; avoid tight C-collar / jugular obstruction.
Monitoring
  • Baseline + continuous GCS, pupils (size/reactivity), respiratory rate & pattern.
  • Continuous SpO₂, EtCO₂ (if intubated), BP. Check glucose; treat hypoglycemia.
  • Obtain GCS/pupils/motor before any paralytic.
Herniation syndrome → escalate
  • Signs: declining LOC, unilateral blown pupil, posturing (decorticate/decerebrate), Cushing reflex (HTN + bradycardia + irregular resp), abnormal breathing.
  • Mild controlled hyperventilation to EtCO₂ 30–35 as a temporizing rescue (never routine).
  • Hyperosmolar therapy per the Brain Herniation protocol (TS-NEU-005): 3% NaCl first-line.
Note — TBI vs hemorrhagic stroke BP
  • TBI: keep SBP up (100–110) for perfusion. Non-traumatic ICH: controlled lowering (different mechanism) — see TS-NEU-002. Don't confuse the two.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Mechanism, initial & serial GCS, pupils, motor exam (and pre-paralytic baseline).
  • Every SpO₂/SBP value + any hypoxia/hypotension episode and its correction; EtCO₂ targets.
  • Herniation signs + interventions; glucose; neurosurgical-center destination + pre-notification.
Overview — preventing the second injury

In traumatic brain injury the primary injury (the impact itself) is already done by the time the crew arrives and cannot be undone. Everything prehospital and transport care can change is the secondary injury — the cascade of ischemia, edema, and metabolic failure that follows, and that is driven largely by two insults the crew controls: hypoxia and hypotension.1,3 This reframes the mission: TBI care is not about a drug, it is about relentlessly maintaining oxygenation, blood pressure, ventilation, and cerebral venous drainage so the injured-but-salvageable brain survives to definitive neurosurgical care.1,3

Why hypoxia and hypotension are catastrophic

The landmark prehospital data are stark: in severe TBI, a single episode of hypotension or hypoxia is associated with a mortality increase of up to roughly six-fold, and the two together are worse still.3 The mechanism is cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP = MAP − ICP): after injury, ICP rises and cerebral autoregulation is impaired, so perfusion becomes dangerously dependent on systemic blood pressure. A drop in MAP collapses CPP directly, and hypoxia/hypercarbia cause cerebral vasodilation that raises ICP and further lowers CPP.1,4 This is why the protocol uses high FiO₂ and aggressive resuscitation in TBI — the usual caution about hyperoxia is outweighed by the catastrophic cost of even brief hypoxia in the injured brain.3,5

Why the TBI blood-pressure target is higher

General trauma uses permissive hypotension (SBP ~90) to avoid disrupting clot, but TBI flips this: an SBP that is "fine" for a bleeding limb may be inadequate for a swollen brain. The protocol therefore raises the target to SBP 100–110 mmHg in TBI with altered mental status to preserve CPP.1,5 When a patient has both hemorrhagic shock and TBI, the brain target generally wins — perfusing the brain takes precedence, which usually means earlier and more aggressive use of blood products. A critical teaching point is that this is the opposite of non-traumatic intracerebral hemorrhage, where blood pressure is deliberately lowered to limit hematoma expansion (TS-NEU-002); the mechanisms differ, and confusing the two pathways harms patients.5

Ventilation — the normocapnia rule

CO₂ is a potent cerebral vasodilator, so ventilation directly controls cerebral blood volume and ICP. The target is normocapnia, EtCO₂ 35–45 mmHg — both extremes are harmful.1,4 Hypoventilation (high CO₂) raises ICP; aggressive hyperventilation (low CO₂) constricts cerebral vessels and causes ischemia, and routine prophylactic hyperventilation worsens outcome.1 Brief, controlled mild hyperventilation to EtCO₂ 30–35 is reserved strictly as a temporizing rescue for active herniation while definitive measures are mobilized, never as standard practice. Because manual or unmonitored ventilation easily drifts into harmful hypocapnia, continuous EtCO₂ monitoring is mandatory in the intubated TBI patient.1,4

Positioning, monitoring & recognizing herniation

Simple mechanical measures lower ICP at no cost: keep the head midline (a turned head or tight cervical collar kinks the jugular veins and impedes venous drainage) and elevate the head of bed 15–30°.1,4 Serial neurologic assessment is the early-warning system — a baseline GCS, pupils, and motor exam are obtained before any paralytic, because sedation/paralysis erase the exam, and the trend (a falling GCS, a newly fixed pupil) is what signals deterioration.1 Signs of herniation — declining consciousness, a unilateral dilated pupil, posturing, the Cushing reflex, or an abnormal respiratory pattern — trigger escalation: controlled mild hyperventilation as a bridge and hyperosmolar therapy (3% saline first-line) per the Brain Herniation protocol (TS-NEU-005), plus expedited transport to a neurosurgical-capable center.1,4 From the Keys, rotor-wing delivery to neurosurgery is frequently the deciding factor in survival.
