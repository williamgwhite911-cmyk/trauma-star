Sec 1 — Airway & Ventilation

TS-AW-001
Ketamine-Forward Trauma Airway (RSI)
Indications: RSI for trauma / critical-care transport; choose pathway by hemodynamics (normotensive: MAP ≥ 65 & SBP ≥ 100; hypotensive: MAP < 65 or SBP < 100).
Normotensive: Ketamine 1–2 mg/kg IV; Rocuronium 1–1.2 mg/kg IV. Post-intubation ketamine drip start 1 mg/kg/hr (titrate 1–4); midazolam add-on 1–2 mg then 1–10 mg/hr.
Hypotensive: push-dose epi bridge (see TS-HD-001) / norepinephrine; Ketamine 0.5–1 mg/kg IV; Rocuronium 1–1.2 mg/kg; resuscitate to MAP ≥ 65 / SBP ≥ 100; same post-intubation sedation.
Key points: confirm post-intubation sedation before paralysis; rocuronium outlasts ketamine — never leave a paralyzed patient unsedated.

Clinical rationale & evidence

The peri-intubation period is one of the most dangerous moments in critical-care transport. Post-intubation hypotension and peri-intubation cardiac arrest are common and independently associated with death, so the protocol is built to protect the blood pressure through induction rather than treat the airway in isolation.1 Induction agent choice matters: ketamine preserves sympathetic tone and is the preferred sedative in the hemodynamically unstable patient, whereas etomidate can cause adrenal suppression and propofol/benzodiazepines blunt vascular tone.2,3 A reduced ketamine dose (0.5–1 mg/kg) is used in shock because a catecholamine-depleted patient can still become hypotensive at full dose.2

The protocol resuscitates before intubation in the hypotensive pathway — a push-dose or infusion vasopressor and/or blood to a MAP ≥ 65 / SBP ≥ 100 — because positive-pressure ventilation reduces preload and can precipitate arrest in an under-resuscitated patient.1 Rocuronium provides reliable, fast intubating conditions, but its duration (~45 min) far outlasts a single dose of ketamine; the most dangerous and preventable error is an awake, paralyzed patient. Confirming that a post-intubation sedation (and analgesia) plan is ready before the paralytic is given is therefore a hard rule, not a preference.1,4

Sec 2 — Hemodynamics & Shock

TS-HD-001
Push-Dose Pressors
Indications: transient hypotension / peri-intubation bridge to a continuous infusion.
Epinephrine: mix 1 mg in 100 mL NS/D5W = 10 mcg/mL; give 0.5–2 mL (5–20 mcg) IV q2–5 min.
Phenylephrine: mix 10 mg (1 mL of 10 mg/mL) in 100 cc NS/D5W = 100 mcg/mL; give 0.5–2 mL (50–200 mcg) IV q2–5 min.
Which one: HR low/normal or pump failing (cardiogenic, post-ROSC, bradycardia) → epinephrine (α + β). HR high / avoid ↑ rate & O₂ demand (tachydysrhythmia, ischemia, aortic stenosis, neurogenic/sedation-induced vasodilation) → phenylephrine (pure α). Phenylephrine alone is a poor choice in cardiogenic shock/bradycardia.
Norepinephrine (Levophed) infusion — transition: mix 4 mg in 250 cc NS/D5W = 16 mcg/mL (8 mg/250 mL = 32 mcg/mL if fluid-restricted); start 5 mcg/min (≈ 0.05 mcg/kg/min), titrate q2–5 min to MAP ≥ 65 (range 5–70 mcg/min). Central line preferred; on a pump; watch extravasation. (Sepsis ladder: TS-MED-009.)
Key points: label syringe + concentration; bridge only — transition to infusion early.

Clinical rationale & evidence

Push-dose (bolus-dose) vasopressors give the crew a way to treat brief, immediately dangerous hypotension — most often peri-intubation or during a transient drop — while a continuous infusion is prepared.1,3 The agent choice is mechanistic: epinephrine (α + β) raises blood pressure and supports cardiac output and heart rate, making it the better choice in bradycardic or low-output states; phenylephrine (pure α) raises afterload without inotropy or chronotropy and is useful when the heart rate is adequate or fast (e.g., tachydysrhythmia, or to avoid worsening myocardial oxygen demand).3

The dominant safety issue is the dosing error. Push-dose pressors are prepared at the bedside from concentrated stock, and tenfold concentration or dilution errors have produced severe hypertensive and dysrhythmic events.2 For this reason the protocol fixes the dilutions (epinephrine 10 mcg/mL; phenylephrine 100 mcg/mL), requires the syringe to be labeled with drug and concentration, and frames push-dose pressors explicitly as a bridge — the patient who needs more than a few boluses needs a titrated infusion and a search for the underlying cause (hemorrhage, tension physiology, sedation effect), not repeated boluses.1,2

TS-HD-002
Whole Blood — Field Administration
Indications: hemorrhagic shock — SBP < 90, or HR ≥ 110 with SBP ≤ 90, or class III–IV shock from hemorrhage.
Product: LTOWB adult 1 unit (~500 mL) IV/IO / peds 10–20 mL/kg, titrate to radial pulse / SBP ~90 (TBI: SBP ≥ 110).
Calcium (with 1st unit): CaCl₂ adult 1 g / peds 20 mg/kg (max 1 g), OR Ca gluconate adult 3 g / peds 60 mg/kg (max 3 g) IV/IO.
TXA (< 3 h): adult 2 g IV over 10 min (initial) / peds 15 mg/kg over 10 min (max 2 g) — don't delay blood.
Key points: warm to ~38 °C (≤ 42); NaCl-only line (no LR/Ca); two-person unit verification; treat hypothermia/hypocalcemia.

Clinical rationale & evidence

Hemorrhage is the leading cause of preventable death in trauma, and patients in hemorrhagic shock are dying from loss of whole blood — red cells, plasma, platelets, and clotting factors together. Resuscitating with crystalloid dilutes the remaining clotting factors, worsens acidosis, and aggravates the "lethal diamond" of coagulopathy, acidosis, hypothermia, and hypocalcemia.1,2 Low-titer O whole blood (LTOWB) replaces what is being lost in physiologic proportion and, in the PROPPR trial and subsequent military and civilian experience, balanced resuscitation improves hemostasis and early survival compared with red-cell-predominant strategies.1,3

Permissive hypotension (titrating to a radial pulse / SBP ~90, but SBP ≥ 110 with concurrent TBI to protect cerebral perfusion) avoids "popping the clot" and dilutional coagulopathy before surgical control.2 Calcium is given because citrate in stored blood chelates ionized calcium, and calcium is essential to the clotting cascade and to myocardial contractility — hypocalcemia independently predicts mortality in massive transfusion.4 Warming the product counters hypothermic coagulopathy, and TXA within 3 hours reduces mortality by limiting fibrinolysis (CRASH-2); given after 3 hours it may increase harm.5 The NaCl-only line (no LR or calcium-containing fluid in the same line) prevents clotting in the tubing, and two-person verification mitigates the catastrophic consequence of a transfusion error.2,6

Sec 3 — Pediatrics

TS-PED-001
Pediatric Resuscitation (Broselow / Handtevy)
Indications: pediatric resuscitation; weight from Broselow tape / Handtevy (age) / scale.
Core doses: Epi (arrest) 0.01 mg/kg IV/IO; fluids 20 mL/kg (10 in trauma); defib 2→4 J/kg; D10 5 mL/kg; naloxone 0.1 mg/kg; amiodarone 5 mg/kg; midazolam 0.1 mg/kg; ketamine 1–2 mg/kg; rocuronium 1 mg/kg.
Key points: interactive weight-based calculator; ketamine concentration default 500 mg/5 mL; color zones with weight + age ranges.

Clinical rationale & evidence

Pediatric resuscitation is weight-based, and weight estimation under stress is the single largest source of dosing error in children. Because clinicians see critically ill children infrequently, calculate doses by hand under pressure, and face a wide range of patient sizes, prehospital pediatric medication errors are common and can be an order of magnitude off.3 The length-based (Broselow) tape was developed precisely to remove this hazard by mapping a measured length to a reliable weight estimate and a set of pre-computed doses and equipment sizes, which reduces error compared with age- or eyeball-based estimates.2

The doses in this protocol follow current PALS standards (e.g., epinephrine 0.01 mg/kg for arrest, defibrillation 2→4 J/kg, 20 mL/kg isotonic boluses — reduced to 10 mL/kg in trauma and in suspected cardiac/DKA physiology).1 The app pairs an interactive weight-based calculator with the familiar Broselow/Handtevy color zones as a visual aid, and standardizes the ketamine concentration (default 500 mg/5 mL) so that a calculated milligram dose maps cleanly to a volume — closing the gap between "knowing the mg/kg" and "drawing up the right mL." The color zones are used as a visual reference only and do not replace an actual measured or known weight when available.2,3

Sec 4 — Operations & Transport

TS-OPS-001
IFT Transport Times — Ground vs AW139
Reference: Keys → Miami corridor (single highway, US-1). Ground > 4 h (~160 mi from Lower Keys) vs AW139 ~45–50 min — ~3+ hours saved.
Use: supports the air-vs-ground medical-necessity rationale; confirm actual mileage/ETA per transport (GPS / Google Maps).

Clinical rationale & evidence

The Florida Keys are served by a single highway (US-1) with no alternate route. A patient in the Lower Keys is roughly 160 road miles from Miami's tertiary trauma, neuro, cardiac, and ECMO resources — a ground transport that routinely exceeds 4 hours, and one that lengthens unpredictably with traffic, weather, and any bridge or road incident on the only artery out. Rotor-wing transport in the AW139 covers the same corridor in roughly 45–50 minutes, saving 3+ hours and providing continuous critical-care monitoring en route.

This time differential is the basis of the medical-necessity rationale for air transport in the Keys, and it is clinically meaningful because the conditions Trauma Star moves — hemorrhage, stroke, STEMI, sepsis, and unstable obstetric and pediatric patients — are all time-dependent, with outcomes that degrade hour by hour. For several of these (e.g., stroke reperfusion windows, hemorrhage control), the hours saved are frequently the difference between eligibility and ineligibility for definitive therapy. Helicopter transport of major trauma patients has been associated with improved survival in large registry analyses, consistent with both reduced time-to-definitive-care and a higher level of en-route care.1 The tabulated times are planning estimates; actual mileage and ETA should be confirmed per transport (GPS / Google Maps), as ground times vary with traffic and weather and air times with winds, routing, and ATC.2

Sec 5 — Environmental & Diving

TS-ENV-001
Decompression Sickness (Bends)
Indications: dive injury with joint/limb pain, skin, neuro, or cardiopulmonary symptoms after a dive.
Treatment: assume DCI; notify pilot for lowest feasible cabin altitude; 100% O₂ 15 L/min continuous; supine; IV fluids LR or NS 150 mL/hr; DAN +1-919-684-9111 + nearest chamber.
Flight (rotor-wing): fly < 1000 ft / near sea-level; continuous O₂. No nitrous oxide.

Clinical rationale & evidence

Decompression illness (DCI) — decompression sickness and arterial gas embolism — is caused by inert-gas bubbles forming in blood and tissue when ambient pressure falls faster than dissolved nitrogen can be eliminated. Two gas laws drive management: by Henry's law tissue gas load is proportional to pressure at depth, and by Boyle's law bubbles enlarge as ambient pressure drops.1,3 High-flow 100% oxygen is the field treatment because it replaces alveolar nitrogen and creates a steep washout gradient that shrinks bubbles and oxygenates ischemic tissue; it is continued even if symptoms resolve, since improvement on oxygen alone is often temporary without recompression.2,3

The defining transport hazard is altitude: any gain in cabin altitude above the dive site lowers ambient pressure and expands bubbles (Boyle's law), which can convert a mild case into a severe one in flight. Trauma Star transports rotor-wing only and flies as low as safely possible (below ~1000 ft, near sea level) with continuous oxygen.4,5 The patient is kept horizontal — the historical head-down/left-lateral position is no longer recommended and may cause harm — and isotonic IV fluid (LR or NS) supports perfusion and inert-gas elimination.3,6 Definitive treatment is hyperbaric recompression, so the operational priorities are early oxygen, the lowest safe altitude, early DAN consultation, and delivery to the nearest staffed chamber; nitrous oxide is avoided because it expands gas spaces.1,2

Sec 6 — Neurology

TS-NEU-001
Stroke — Ischemic
Assess: BE-FAST + LVO screen; Last Known Well; glucose; anticoagulant use.
BP: no tPA/intervention — treat only SBP > 220 or DBP > 120 (limit reduction 15% baseline). To qualify for / post-tPA: ≤ 180/105. Post-thrombectomy: per interventionalist.
Alteplase (online order): 0.9 mg/kg TBW (max 90 mg) — 10% over 1 min, 90% over 60 min; < 4.5 h, age ≥ 18.
Antihypertensives: Nicardipine 5 mg/hr, ↑2.5 q5–15, max 15; Clevidipine 1–2 mg/hr, max 32; Labetalol 20 mg, repeat 40/80, max 300.

Clinical rationale & evidence

Ischemic stroke (~87% of strokes) is an occlusion with a salvageable penumbra that converts to infarct over time — an estimated 1.9 million neurons are lost per minute of delay, the quantitative basis for "time is brain" and for fixing an accurate Last-Known-Well, which governs eligibility.1,2 Field care identifies stroke and screens for large-vessel occlusion (LVO), because LVO patients benefit from triage to an endovascular-capable center even if it means bypassing a closer hospital.1,4 Reperfusion windows reflect the trial evidence: IV thrombolysis to 3 h (NINDS) and 4.5 h (ECASS III), thrombectomy to 6 h (HERMES) and, with imaging selection, to 24 h (DAWN/DEFUSE-3) — so a patient who looks "too late" by the clock may still be a thrombectomy candidate.5,6,7

Blood pressure is generally not lowered because penumbral perfusion is pressure-passive; antihypertensives are reserved for SBP > 220 / DBP > 120 (limit reduction to ~15% of baseline), or to reach ≤ 185/110 before and ≤ 180/105 after thrombolysis to limit hemorrhagic transformation.1,8 Titratable agents (nicardipine, clevidipine, labetalol) allow controlled reduction. Glucose is checked first because hypoglycemia is a stroke mimic, oxygen is given only to keep SpO₂ ≥ 94%, and advance notification measurably shortens treatment times — all reasons the Keys air-vs-ground differential is frequently decisive for staying in window.1,3

TS-NEU-002
Stroke — Hemorrhagic (ICH)
BP: ICH with SBP > 160 → lower to 130–160 mmHg, smooth control.
Antihypertensives: Nicardipine / Clevidipine / Labetalol (as above); Esmolol 500 mcg/kg load, 50 mcg/kg/min titration, max 300 mcg/kg/min.
Key points: anticoagulant reversal under direct medical control; HOB 30°; neurosurgical-capable destination.

Clinical rationale & evidence

Spontaneous intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH) is ~10–15% of strokes but disproportionately lethal (30-day mortality ~40%), and the strongest modifiable predictor of death is early hematoma expansion, which occurs in about a third of patients in the first hours.1 Elevated BP drives expansion, so early controlled lowering reduces growth (INTERACT2), but aggressive over-lowering provides no added benefit and signals harm (ATACH-2) — establishing both a target and a floor.3,4 The protocol therefore lowers an elevated SBP smoothly into target with titratable infusions (nicardipine, clevidipine, labetalol, esmolol), avoiding the swings and abrupt drops that can cause perilesional ischemia.1,5

Because many ICH patients take anticoagulants or antiplatelets that worsen the bleed and accelerate expansion, the highest-yield transport action is to obtain and relay an accurate medication history (including last dose) so reversal agents can be drawn up and given immediately on arrival.1 Secondary-injury prevention — SpO₂ ≥ 94%, normocapnia, avoidance of hypotension and hyperthermia, HOB ~30°, and early airway protection in declining consciousness — limits further damage, and any sign of expansion or herniation (declining GCS, new pupillary asymmetry, Cushing response) triggers the herniation pathway and immediate neurosurgical-team notification at a neurosurgery-capable destination.1,5

TS-NEU-003
Stroke — Undetermined
BP: positive stroke assessment + glucose ≥ 60 → SBP goal < 185; antihypertensives as above (Nicardipine / Clevidipine / Labetalol).
Key points: hypoglycemia mimics stroke — check/treat first; rapid transport to CT to define type.

Clinical rationale & evidence

Without CT the field cannot reliably separate ischemic from hemorrhagic stroke — bedside judgment and classic features misclassify a large share of patients — so the undifferentiated stroke is the normal starting state for every stroke transport.1,2 The two subtypes call for opposite BP strategies (permissive hypertension for ischemia vs controlled lowering for hemorrhage), and the asymmetry of harm guides the default: with ~7 of 8 strokes ischemic, inappropriately dropping BP risks extending an infarct, so the field default is to not lower BP unless it is extreme or herniation is evident, and to let CT decide.1,3,4

What is safe for everyone defines the bundle: SpO₂ ≥ 94%, airway protection in declining consciousness, treat hypoglycemia (a mimic and a secondary insult), avoid hypotension and hyperthermia, fix an accurate LKW, gather the anticoagulation history, and move fast to a CT/stroke-capable center (ideally one with both thrombectomy and neurosurgery) with pre-notification.1,4 Two situations justify acting before imaging: extreme hypertension (cautious titratable reduction under medical control) and clinical herniation (the herniation pathway). From the Keys, rapid rotor-wing delivery to imaging is the intervention that most reliably helps every subtype.1,3

A convulsion lasting ≥ 5 minutes is status epilepticus and is treated as such — the 5-minute definition replaced older 30-minute thresholds because most seizures self-terminate in 2–3 minutes, and prolonged activity becomes progressively pharmacoresistant (GABA-receptor internalization) and neurotoxic.1,2 Early, adequately-dosed treatment is the main determinant of outcome. TS-NEU-005
Brain Herniation Syndrome
Recognize: abnormal breathing, pupillary changes, posturing, Cushing reflex (bradycardia + HTN + resp depression).
Treatment: Sodium Chloride 3% 250 mL IV over 5–15 min (peds 3–5 mL/kg IBW). Alternate Mannitol 0.5–1 g/kg over 30–60 min (online order). HOB 30°, optimize ventilation/oxygenation.

Clinical rationale & evidence

Herniation is the final common pathway by which expanding intracranial mass lesions kill. The Monro-Kellie doctrine explains its speed: once the small CSF and venous-blood buffers are exhausted, any further volume produces a steep, nonlinear rise in ICP and fall in cerebral perfusion (CPP = MAP − ICP).1,2 The Cushing reflex (hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respirations) is a late brainstem attempt to defend perfusion. This relationship makes two field errors catastrophic — allowing hypotension (collapses CPP) or hypoxia/hypercarbia (CO₂ vasodilates and raises ICP) — so oxygenation, ventilation, and blood pressure are as important as any drug.2,3

Hyperosmolar therapy draws water out of brain tissue across an intact blood-brain barrier. 3% saline (adult 250 mL; peds 3–5 mL/kg IBW over 5–15 min) is first-line and also supports BP/volume — valuable in a patient who must not become hypotensive — while mannitol (0.5–1 g/kg, online order) is effective but can cause diuresis-related hypovolemia.4 These are paired with the ICP bundle: HOB ~30°, midline head (avoid jugular obstruction), normocapnia (EtCO₂ ~35–40; brief mild hyperventilation only as a temporizing rescue for active herniation), and rapid transport to a neurosurgical-capable center for definitive decompression.2,3

Clinical rationale & evidence

Sec 7 — Toxicology

TS-TOX-001
Opioid Overdose
Treatment: ventilate first; Naloxone — adult 1–2 mg IV/IM/IN, peds 0.01 mg/kg (max 1 mg), newborn 0.1 mg/kg. Infusion: ⅔ of the effective dose each hour.
Key points: titrate to respiration, not full arousal; watch for re-sedation.

Clinical rationale & evidence

Opioids kill by suppressing brainstem respiratory drive, so the lethal element is hypoventilation, not the pupils or the level of consciousness. The first and most important intervention is oxygenation and ventilation (BVM); naloxone is an adjunct that buys time, not a substitute for airway management — a patient being ventilated effectively is, for the moment, safe.1,3 Naloxone is titrated to restore adequate spontaneous breathing, not full arousal, because over-reversal precipitates withdrawal, vomiting/aspiration, and agitation that is a genuine safety hazard in a moving aircraft.2,3

The central pitfall is the duration mismatch: naloxone lasts ~30–90 min, shorter than methadone, extended-release products, and high-potency fentanyl analogs, so a patient can re-sedate after a single dose. Severe or recurrent toxicity is therefore managed with an infusion (≈ ⅔ of the effective bolus per hour) with continuous monitoring of respiratory rate, SpO₂, and ideally EtCO₂.1,2 An incomplete response despite adequate naloxone should prompt consideration of co-ingestants, hypoglycemia, hypoxic injury, trauma, or chest-wall rigidity from high-potency agents. Contact Poison Control (1-800-222-1222) for atypical presentations.2

TS-TOX-002
Smoke Inhalation & Cyanide
Suspect: enclosed-space fire + AMS, seizures, SBP < 90/shock, arrest, lactate ≥ 8, or pH < 7.25 — treat empirically.

Clinical rationale & evidence

Enclosed-space fires produce two synergistic asphyxiants — carbon monoxide and hydrogen cyanide — that poison oxygen delivery and oxygen use respectively. Cyanide cannot be confirmed quickly (levels take hours), so it is treated empirically when smoke exposure coincides with shock, altered mental status, seizures, arrest, or a high lactate.1,2 Cyanide binds cytochrome c oxidase and halts aerobic ATP production despite available oxygen, producing a profound lactic acidosis; a lactate ≥ 8–10 in a fire victim is a strong surrogate marker.2,3 Oxygen alone does not fix it — an antidote is required.

Hydroxocobalamin (Cyanokit) binds cyanide to form renally-excreted cyanocobalamin (5 g over 15 min; peds 70 mg/kg, max 5 g; repeat once to 10 g).4 It is preferred over nitrite kits in fire victims because nitrites induce methemoglobinemia, which cannot carry oxygen — dangerous when CO has already crippled oxygen carrying; hydroxocobalamin does not impair oxygen transport.1,3 CO is treated with sustained 100% oxygen (hyperbaric for severe features), remembering that standard pulse oximetry reads falsely normal with carboxyhemoglobin. Secure the airway early for facial/airway burns, soot, or stridor before edema makes it impossible, and treat bronchospasm, seizures, and hypotension.2,4

TS-TOX-003
TCA Overdose
Treat if: CV instability/hypotension, seizures/coma, wide QRS, prolonged QT, ventricular dysrhythmia.
Sodium Bicarbonate: adult 50–100 mEq IV over 5–10 min (peds 1–2 mEq/kg); 1 dose standing, repeats need physician order.

Clinical rationale & evidence

TCAs have a narrow therapeutic index and overdose causes fast sodium-channel blockade (wide QRS, ventricular dysrhythmia), anticholinergic effects, CNS toxicity (seizures, coma), and refractory hypotension — with characteristically rapid deterioration.1 The ECG is the vital sign: QRS duration predicts toxicity better than dose or level, with QRS > 100 ms predicting seizures and > 160 ms predicting ventricular dysrhythmias, and a terminal R wave in aVR as a supporting clue.2 Acidosis and hypoxia worsen channel blockade, so oxygenation and ventilation are themselves cardioprotective.1,2

Sodium bicarbonate is the cornerstone antidote, working by a sodium load that overcomes channel blockade and alkalinization that reduces free drug — together narrowing the QRS, suppressing dysrhythmias, and supporting BP (adult 50–100 mEq over 5–10 min; peds 1–2 mEq/kg; one dose standing, repeats by physician order).3,4 Seizures (which cause acidosis and worsen cardiotoxicity) are treated with benzodiazepines — phenytoin is avoided — and Class IA/IC and other QRS-prolonging antiarrhythmics are avoided entirely. Refractory collapse may need lipid emulsion or ECMO at the receiving facility, another reason to expedite transport; contact Poison Control early.1

Clinical rationale & evidence

Every OB transport is the care of two patients, and because the placenta has no autoregulation, fetal oxygen delivery tracks maternal cardiac output, oxygen saturation, and uterine blood flow.1 The supportive bundle is therefore active fetal resuscitation: lateral positioning relieves aortocaval compression (the gravid uterus compresses the IVC/aorta when supine, dropping maternal output and uteroplacental flow), and fluids are kept modest (~125–150 mL/hr) because OB patients are prone to pulmonary edema.1,2 Oxygen is titrated to the fetal tracing rather than given by reflex, using the standardized NICHD categories so the report means the same thing to the receiving MFM.3,4

Analgesia, antiemesis, and anxiolysis reduce maternal catecholamines that constrict uterine flow, but each agent is chosen for fetal safety — fentanyl is withheld if delivery is possible within an hour to avoid neonatal respiratory depression.5 Because magnesium is used across OB, the crew must recognize hypermagnesemia early: deep-tendon reflexes disappear before respiration fails, and IV calcium is the immediate antidote.6

Clinical rationale & evidence

Preeclampsia is a multisystem disease of abnormal placentation causing systemic endothelial injury — hypertension, seizures, hepatic injury, and HELLP.1,2 The central concept: magnesium and BP control are two separate jobs. Magnesium prevents/treats eclamptic seizures (Magpie trial halves eclampsia; the Collaborative Eclampsia Trial shows superiority over diazepam/phenytoin) but does not lower BP.4,5 The leading killer is hemorrhagic stroke from sustained severe-range systolic pressure, so ACOG and this protocol treat SBP ≥ 160 / DBP ≥ 110 as an emergency requiring treatment within 10–20 minutes to a controlled (not normalized) target.3,6 Fluids are restricted (~125 mL/hr) because leaky endothelium predisposes to pulmonary edema, and reducing cabin stimuli is genuine seizure prophylaxis.3

Clinical rationale & evidence

Trauma kills in a predictable order, so ABCDE prioritizes the fastest killers, with the modern modification that catastrophic external hemorrhage is controlled first (the military MARCH sequence) — a limb can exsanguinate in minutes.1,2 High-flow oxygen maximizes delivery on reduced hemoglobin while permissive hypotension protects fresh clot — except in TBI, where perfusion needs a higher SBP because hypotension/hypoxia each raise TBI mortality up to six-fold.3,5 The hemorrhage-control ladder and early tourniquet use are validated by military/Stop-the-Bleed data (early tourniquets save lives with rare limb loss); pelvic binders tamponade venous bleeding; and hypothermia is actively prevented because it is one corner of the lethal triad — a cold patient cannot clot.2,6

Avoid the two killers: hypoxia & hypotension (each ↑ mortality up to 6×). Use high FiO₂ + aggressive resuscitation.

Clinical rationale & evidence

TS-MED-006
Glycemic Emergencies (Hypoglycemia / DKA / HHS)
Hypoglycemia (BG < 60 / < 50 neonate): Dextrose adult 10–25 g IV / peds 0.5–1 g/kg (max 25 g) — D50 adult/≥8 yr, D25 ≥1 yr, D10 all; or Glucagon 1 mg IM (peds ≥20 kg 1 mg / <20 kg 0.5 mg). Recheck q15 ×4.
DKA/HHS = physician-directed: NS for resuscitation; insulin only after K⁺ ≥ 3.3; BG q15 on drip (stop if falling > 100/hr); hourly neuro. Match high minute ventilation if intubating the acidotic patient.

Clinical rationale & evidence

Hypoglycemia is the fastest reversible cause of AMS; dextrose concentration is matched to age (D50 vein/tissue-toxic in young children → D25/D10), and glucagon is a bridge that fails in glycogen-depleted patients.1 Long-acting insulin/sulfonylurea hypoglycemia recurs, so glucose is rechecked to 4 normals.1 DKA/HHS are complex fluid/electrolyte/acid-base derangements managed under physician control: insulin is withheld until K⁺ ≥ 3.3 (insulin drives K⁺ into cells → fatal arrhythmia if low), and glucose is corrected gradually (too-fast → cerebral edema, esp. children).2 The intubation trap: acidotic DKA patients survive on high minute ventilation — under-ventilating after RSI crashes the pH, so ventilation is matched pre/peri/post.2

TS-MED-007
Hyperkalemia
Treat if K⁺ > 5.5 with ECG changes (peaked T, wide QRS, loss of P, sine wave) or > 6.0. ① Stabilize: Calcium chloride 1 g IV (peds 20 mg/kg) or gluconate 3 g IV (peds 60 mg/kg), repeat q10 ×3; flush line.
② Shift: Albuterol 5 mg neb; Sodium bicarbonate 50 mEq (peds 1 mEq/kg) if acidosis/arrest; Insulin + glucose per physician.

Clinical rationale & evidence

Hyperkalemia kills by arrhythmia and is recognized in the field by context (renal/missed dialysis, crush, DKA) + ECG (peaked T → wide QRS → sine wave).1,2 Management is stabilize → shift → eliminate; only the first two are field-achievable. Calcium raises the myocyte threshold and protects within minutes but does not lower K⁺ and is temporary (and precipitates with bicarbonate — separate lines).2 Albuterol, insulin/glucose, and bicarbonate (the latter for acidosis/hyperkalemic arrest) shift K⁺ intracellularly; definitive removal needs dialysis — the destination driver. This mirrors the trauma crush pathway (TS-TR-005).1,2

TS-MED-008
Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)
Keep child calm (agitation worsens obstruction); humidified 100% O₂. Methylprednisolone 2 mg/kg (max 125) before transfer.
Stridor at rest: racemic epi 0.25–0.5 mL 2.25% in 3 mL NS (peds <15 kg 0.25 mL) or L-epi 0.5 mL/kg (max 5 mL) SVN — watch rebound (~2 h). Intubate only PRN, ETT 0.5–1 mm smaller.

Clinical rationale & evidence

Croup is viral subglottic edema at a child's narrowest airway; small swelling hugely raises resistance (r⁴).1,2 Because agitation worsens obstruction, keeping the child calm with the caregiver is genuine therapy.2 Steroids are the mainstay (shorten illness, reduce intubation) and benefit even mild croup; nebulized epinephrine rapidly shrinks mucosal edema for stridor at rest but rebounds in ~2 h, so these children are observed/transported.2,3 Avoid intubation when possible; use a smaller ETT. Stay alert for mimics (epiglottitis, tracheitis, foreign body).1,2

TS-MED-009
Sepsis & Septic Shock
Identify: infection + organ dysfunction via qSOFA + EtCO₂ (RR > 22, new AMS, SBP < 100, EtCO₂ < 25; ≥ 2 = positive). NS 30 mL/kg rapidly, complete regardless; +20 mL/kg if still SBP < 90/MAP < 65.
Pressors to MAP ≥ 65: Norepinephrine 5–70 mcg/min → add Vasopressin 0.04 u/min (fixed) at norepi > 10–20 → add Epinephrine 2–10 mcg/min at norepi > 30. Wean reverse order.
Continue PTA antibiotics; resuscitate before intubation; catecholamine-resistant → Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV (physician). Peds under medical control.

Clinical rationale & evidence

Sepsis is dysregulated infection response causing vasodilation, leak, and oxygen-delivery failure; each hour's delay in perfusion restoration and antibiotics raises mortality.1,2,3 qSOFA + EtCO₂ screens without labs — low EtCO₂ reflects compensatory tachypnea and hypoperfusion (correlates with lactate/mortality).4 The 30 mL/kg bolus is completed because filling the tank and squeezing the vessels are complementary; norepinephrine is first-line, with fixed-dose vasopressin (different receptor, catecholamine-sparing) and then epinephrine added in sequence and weaned in reverse.2 Antibiotics are continued, steroids added for refractory shock, and patients resuscitated before intubation to avoid induction arrest.2

TS-MED-010
Vascular Emergencies (AAA / Aortic Dissection)
Ruptured AAA: permissive hypotension — titrate to radial pulse / SBP 80–100 + mentation; manage lethal triad; emergent vascular surgery.
Dissection: BP both arms, treat higher; avoid IV in pulse-deficit limb; opioids for pain. β-blocker FIRST (Esmolol load 500 mcg/kg → 50 mcg/kg/min; or Labetalol/Metoprolol) to HR < 60; then vasodilator if SBP > 120 (Nicardipine preferred / Clevidipine / Nitroprusside) to SBP 100–120.

Clinical rationale & evidence

These are time-critical surgical aortic catastrophes with opposite hemodynamic strategies.1,2 Ruptured AAA is bleeding — permissive hypotension avoids blowing out the tamponading clot, and the priority is the OR.1 Dissection propagation is driven by pulse force/rate (dP/dt), so the primary target is HR < 60 with a beta-blocker FIRST, adding a vasodilator only after — a vasodilator alone causes reflex tachycardia that increases shear and extends the tear.2 BP is measured in both arms (branch involvement), and a dissection patient who becomes hypotensive signals rupture/tamponade — stop antihypertensives and resuscitate.2

TS-MED-011
Nausea / Vomiting & EPS
First-line: Ondansetron 4 mg IV (peds 0.1 mg/kg, max 4), repeat ×1 in 30 min. Second-line: Prochlorperazine 5–10 mg (not < 2 yr/10 kg) or Promethazine 12.5–25 mg in 10 mL IV (boxed warning < 2 yr; tissue injury).
EPS / dystonia from dopamine-blockers → Diphenhydramine 25–50 mg IV/IM (peds 1–2 mg/kg, max 50).

Clinical rationale & evidence

Controlling vomiting prevents aspiration and is safer in a moving aircraft, but vomiting is a symptom — keep dangerous mimics (MI, ↑ICP, obstruction, DKA, pregnancy) in mind.1 Ondansetron is first-line for efficacy without sedation/hypotension/EPS.1,2 The dopamine-antagonists (prochlorperazine, promethazine) are effective but sedating and can cause EPS; promethazine carries a boxed warning under age 2 and causes severe tissue injury on extravasation (dilute, patent large vein).1 Their dopamine blockade can trigger acute dystonia/akathisia, rapidly reversed by diphenhydramine — so the antidote is paired with the drug.1

TS-AS-001
Pain & Sedation Scales
Pain: 0–10 (self-report) · CPOT 0–8 or BPS 3–12 (cannot self-report). Sedation/agitation: RASS −5 to +4 · SAS 1–7. Assess/document before & after every analgesic/sedative; target post-intubation RASS −1 to −5.

Clinical rationale & evidence

Validated scales turn subjective pain/sedation into a reproducible number, enabling titration to a defined target and detection of trends.1,2 The tool is matched to the patient (self-report 0–10; CPOT/BPS when unable; RASS/SAS for sedation depth), and pain and sedation are tracked as separate axes.2 The critical limitation: behavioral scales measure behaviors a paralyzed patient cannot display — under NMB they are invalid and empiric targeted dosing is required (TS-AS-004).1

TS-AS-002
Analgesia & Anxiolysis (IV acetaminophen-first — TS/MD addition)
① Non-opioid FIRST (multimodal): IV Acetaminophen ≥ 50 kg 1000 mg IV over 15 min / < 50 kg 15 mg/kg (max 1000), q6h, max 4 g/24 h (3 g if hepatic risk); ± Ketorolac 15 mg IV (peds 0.5 mg/kg, max 15). Give before/with narcotics to reduce opioid requirement.
② Opioids (titrate): Fentanyl 0.5–2 mcg/kg IBW (q10); Morphine 2–10 mg (peds 0.05–0.2 mg/kg); Hydromorphone 0.2–1 mg (peds 0.01–0.02; max total 4 mg).
③ Adjunct: sub-dissociative Ketamine 0.1–0.3 mg/kg (max 30 mg) slow — opioid-refractory or buprenorphine/naltrexone; avoid psychosis.
Anxiolysis (after pain): Midazolam 0.5–5 mg / Lorazepam 0.5–2 mg — benzo+opioid = extreme caution (capnography). Reversal: Naloxone 0.001–0.01 mg/kg titrated to breathing.

Clinical rationale & evidence

Untreated pain drives catecholamines (↑HR/BP, ↑myocardial & intracranial demand), so analgesia is treated to the lowest reasonable level balanced with hemodynamics.1 This protocol adds IV acetaminophen as the first rung, before opioids — a Medical-Director enhancement to the PHI ladder — because it is opioid-sparing and, unlike opioids, causes no respiratory depression, sedation, or hypotension, lowering total narcotic need without adding the risks that make opioid titration hazardous in flight.3,4 Opioids are then titrated in small repeat doses (fentanyl preferred if unstable — hemodynamically neutral; morphine releases histamine; avoid in shock).1 Sub-dissociative ketamine adds NMDA-mechanism analgesia (opioid-sparing; works when receptors are blocked by buprenorphine/naltrexone) and must be pushed slowly to avoid distressing partial dissociation.2 Benzodiazepine + opioid is synergistically respiratory-depressant; naloxone is titrated to restore breathing, not full reversal.1

TS-AS-003
Procedural Sedation
Before: SpO₂, ECG, NIBP, continuous capnography, RR; airway + CV rescue ready. Indications: cardioversion, thoracostomy, fracture reduction, extrication.
Ketamine (preferred): 1–2 mg/kg IBW IV or 4 mg/kg IM (lower dose if unstable). Etomidate (brief): 0.1 mg/kg TBW IV, repeat 0.05 mg/kg (max 30 adult / 20 peds); no analgesia, expect myoclonus, not for prolonged/post-intubation.

Clinical rationale & evidence

Procedural sedation depresses consciousness for a discrete painful task; the safety lies in preparation and monitoring, with capnography detecting hypoventilation before desaturation.1,2 Ketamine is preferred because it provides dissociation + analgesia while preserving airway reflexes, respiratory drive, and blood pressure (reduced dose if unstable).1 Etomidate suits very brief procedures (hemodynamically stable) but gives no analgesia, causes myoclonus, and transiently suppresses cortisol — so it is not used for prolonged/post-intubation sedation.1

TS-AS-004
Post-Intubation Analgesia & Sedation
Never leave an intubated/paralyzed patient unsedated. Goal RASS −1 to −5; analgesia first; optimize ONE agent before adding. Stratify: assessable → titrate; NMB/unassessable → empiric; unstable → ketamine, reduce other sedatives ½–¼.
First-line: Fentanyl 0.5–2 mcg/kg IBW or 1–2 mcg/kg/hr; Ketamine bolus 1–2 mg/kg then 1–4 mg/kg/hr (bolus before infusion). Add one sedative: Propofol 5–100 mcg/kg/min (no analgesia; hypotension), Midazolam 0.05–0.2 mg/kg/hr (preferred if seizure masked by NMB), or Dexmedetomidine 0.2–1.5 mcg/kg/hr.
Controlled substances: do NOT continue another facility's infusion — prepare a new one with program drugs; waste & document residual.

Clinical rationale & evidence

The ETT is noxious and awareness under paralysis is a devastating, preventable harm — so sedation is never omitted.1,2 The structure is analgesia-first at the lightest effective depth (PADIS: light sedation/analgosedation improves outcomes), choosing and optimizing one agent rather than stacking.2 The unstable patient gets ketamine (hemodynamically supportive) with all other sedatives reduced ½–¼; ketamine needs a dissociative bolus before the infusion or it under-sedates.1 Sedative choice is situational: propofol = titratable but no analgesia and hypotensive; midazolam preferred when a paralytic could mask seizure; dexmedetomidine for cooperative light sedation. Controlled-substance chain-of-custody requires preparing a fresh program infusion and wasting residual.1
