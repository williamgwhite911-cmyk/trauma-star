Procedural Sedation

Ketamine · etomidate · monitoring
TS-AS-003 · Sec 11 Analgesia & Sedation
Before you start — mandatory
  • Establish SpO₂, cardiac rhythm, NIBP, continuous waveform capnography, RR first.
  • Have airway/ventilation + CV-instability equipment & drugs ready. Be prepared to manage apnea (esp. dissociative ketamine).
  • Provide analgesia (TS-AS-002) for painful procedures — sedation ≠ analgesia.
Indications (short, unpleasant procedures)
  • Cardioversion · tube/simple thoracostomy · fracture realignment & splinting · extrication/disentanglement.
  • (CPR-induced consciousness is also an indication for sedation.)
Ketamine — preferred
Adult & Pediatric
1–2 mg/kg IBW IV over 30–60 sec or 4 mg/kg IBW IM. Use lower dose if hemodynamically unstable.
Etomidate — alternate (very brief procedures)
Adult & Pediatric
0.1 mg/kg TBW IV over 30–60 sec, repeat 0.05 mg/kg q3–5 min PRN (max single adult 30 mg / peds 20 mg). Pretreat ondansetron PRN.
Cautions
Caution in adrenal insufficiency; not for prolonged or post-intubation sedation; expect myoclonus (involuntary jerks).
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Indication/procedure; monitoring established (incl. capnography); pre-procedure airway plan.
  • Agent/dose/route/time; depth achieved; analgesia given; complications (apnea, myoclonus, laryngospasm) & management.
Overview — controlled depression of consciousness for a discrete task

Procedural sedation deliberately depresses consciousness so a patient can tolerate a short, painful, or distressing procedure — cardioversion, chest-tube/thoracostomy, fracture/dislocation reduction, or extrication — and then recovers.1,2 Because the same drugs that blunt awareness also blunt airway reflexes and respiratory drive, the defining feature of safe procedural sedation is not the drug but the preparation and monitoring around it: the crew must be ready to rescue an airway at any moment.

Monitoring is the protocol

Full monitoring — SpO₂, ECG, NIBP, respiratory rate, and continuous waveform capnography — is established before any drug is given, and oxygenation/ventilation and cardiovascular-rescue equipment and medications are laid out ready.1 Capnography is the key early-warning monitor: it detects hypoventilation and apnea seconds-to-minutes before pulse oximetry falls (especially in a pre-oxygenated patient), giving the crew time to stimulate, reposition, or assist ventilation before desaturation. In the dissociated patient the crew must specifically anticipate apnea, laryngospasm, and airway compromise and be prepared to manage them.1

Why ketamine is preferred

Ketamine is the preferred procedural-sedation agent for transport because it provides dissociative sedation plus profound analgesia while largely preserving airway reflexes, respiratory drive, and blood pressure — a uniquely favorable combination when an airway emergency mid-procedure would be catastrophic.1,2 It even supports hemodynamics through sympathetic stimulation, so it is favored in the unstable patient (at a reduced dose, since a catecholamine-depleted patient can still drop pressure). Its trade-offs — transient laryngospasm, hypersalivation, emergence phenomena, and dissociation — are anticipated and managed, and analgesia is still addressed separately for painful procedures because sedation depth and pain control are distinct goals.1

Etomidate's niche and its caveats

Etomidate is the alternative for very brief procedures (classically cardioversion) because it has a rapid onset, short duration, and excellent hemodynamic stability.1 But it carries specific limits: it provides no analgesia (pair with an opioid for painful procedures), it causes myoclonus (involuntary jerking that can be mistaken for seizure or distress, and that can interfere with a delicate procedure), and a single dose transiently suppresses cortisol — hence the cautions in adrenal insufficiency and the explicit prohibition on using it for prolonged or post-intubation sedation, where repeated/continuous exposure could cause clinically significant adrenal suppression.1 Dosing is by total body weight, and ondansetron pre-treatment reduces associated nausea.1
