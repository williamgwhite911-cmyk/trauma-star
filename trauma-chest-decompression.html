Pneumothorax & Chest Decompression

Needle · finger thoracostomy · chest tube
TS-TR-013 · Sec 9 Trauma
Decompress NOW for tension — any of these
  • Compatible MOI (chest trauma, rib fx, blast, barotrauma, positive-pressure ventilation) PLUS
  • Respiratory distress / difficulty ventilating (rising peak pressures on the vent), and/or
  • Falling SpO₂ (hypoxia), and/or hypotension / shock (obstructive), with
  • ↓ or absent breath sounds on the affected side ± hyperresonance, subcutaneous emphysema.
  • Late / unreliable signs (do NOT wait for them): tracheal deviation, distended neck veins (JVD), muffled heart tones, cyanosis.
  • Ventilated trauma patient who suddenly desats then drops BP = tension until proven otherwise — decompress.
① Needle decompression (fastest)
  • Site: 5th intercostal space, anterior-to-mid axillary line (nipple level) — preferred; OR 2nd ICS mid-clavicular line. Use a long catheter (≥ 8 cm / 14 G).
  • Over the TOP of the rib (avoid the neurovascular bundle under each rib). Listen/feel for the rush of air.
  • Remove the NEEDLE, LEAVE THE CATHETER in place, open to air; secure it. Never reinsert the needle into the catheter.
  • Reassess — needle decompression frequently fails (kinks, clots, too short, wrong space). If no improvement or it recurs → finger thoracostomy.
② Finger thoracostomy (definitive field decompression)
  1. Site: 5th ICS, anterior/mid-axillary line (the "triangle of safety"), affected side. Prep skin; analgesia/sedation if responsive.
  2. 2–3 cm incision over the top of the 6th rib (above the rib below).
  3. Blunt-dissect with a clamp through muscle/pleura; pop through the parietal pleura — gush of air/blood confirms entry.
  4. Insert a gloved finger, sweep to confirm pleural space & clear adhesions/lung. Leave open (or to a chest seal/tube).
  5. Reassess ventilation/SpO₂/BP; cover the wound; prepare for tube thoracostomy / monitor for re-accumulation.
③ Tube thoracostomy (chest tube)
  • Through the finger-thoracostomy tract (5th ICS, mid-axillary). Adult 28–32 Fr (hemothorax) or smaller for simple PTX.
  • Direct the tube posteriorly & superiorly; advance until all drainage holes are inside the pleura.
  • Connect to a one-way (Heimlich) valve or chest-drain unit; secure with suture + occlusive dressing.
  • Massive hemothorax (immediate ≥ 1–1.5 L or ongoing ≥ 200 mL/hr) → resuscitate with blood (TS-HD-002), expedite to OR.
Open ("sucking") chest wound
  • Apply a vented chest seal (or 3-sided dressing). After sealing, watch for tension → burp/lift the seal or decompress.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Indication (MOI + distress/hypoxia/hypotension + exam), side, time.
  • Procedure(s) done, site, catheter/tube size, air/blood return, response; catheter left in after needle.
  • Re-accumulation/reassessment; blood given for hemothorax; trauma-center destination.
Overview — recognize the pressure, relieve it

A pneumothorax is air in the pleural space; a tension pneumothorax is a one-way leak that progressively raises intrapleural pressure, collapses the lung, shifts the mediastinum, and obstructs venous return — causing obstructive shock and arrest if not relieved.1,2 It is a clinical diagnosis treated immediately; there is no time or imaging in the field to confirm it. This protocol defines clear indications to decompress and a procedural ladder — needle → finger thoracostomy → chest tube — chosen by speed, reliability, and the patient's situation.1,2

The indications — don't wait for the classic signs

The decision to decompress rests on mechanism + physiology + exam, not on the textbook late findings. A compatible mechanism (chest trauma, rib fractures, blast/barotrauma, and especially a patient on positive-pressure ventilation) plus respiratory distress, hypoxia (falling SpO₂), or hypotension, with decreased breath sounds on one side, is enough to act.1,2 The "classic" triad — tracheal deviation, distended neck veins, and muffled heart tones (the latter two overlapping with tamponade) plus hyperresonance — are late, insensitive, and often absent before arrest, so the protocol explicitly lists them but instructs the crew not to wait for them.2 The single most useful field rule is the ventilated-patient axiom: sudden desaturation followed by hypotension and rising airway pressures in a ventilated trauma patient is tension until proven otherwise, because positive pressure both causes and accelerates it.2

Needle decompression — and why the catheter stays

Needle decompression is the fastest first step. Evidence favors the 5th intercostal space at the anterior/mid-axillary line (and a longer, ≥ 8 cm catheter) over the traditional 2nd-ICS mid-clavicular site, because the lateral chest wall is thinner and the older site frequently fails to reach the pleura in adults.1,3 The needle is passed over the top of the rib to avoid the neurovascular bundle that runs along each rib's lower margin. Crucially, after the air rushes out the needle is withdrawn and the plastic catheter is left in place open to air: the rigid needle, if left in, will lacerate lung or vessels and will kink/clot — and it must never be reinserted through the catheter (shears off and embolizes plastic).1 Needle decompression often fails or is temporary (kinks, clots, displacement), so the crew reassesses and escalates to finger thoracostomy if there is no improvement or the tension recurs.2,3

Finger thoracostomy — the reliable field procedure

Finger (simple) thoracostomy is more reliable than a needle, especially in the ventilated patient, and is increasingly the procedure of choice in HEMS/critical-care transport.2,3 Through the "triangle of safety" (5th ICS, anterior to mid-axillary line — bordered by the pectoralis, latissimus, and a line at the nipple) the operator makes a 2–3 cm incision over the top of the rib, blunt-dissects through the intercostal muscles, and deliberately punctures the parietal pleura (confirmed by a gush of air or blood), then sweeps a finger to confirm the pleural space and clear adhesions.2 It immediately and durably relieves a tension and, unlike a needle, will also begin to drain a hemothorax. In the awake patient, analgesia/sedation is provided. It is left open to air (or connected to a seal/tube) and is the direct precursor to placing a chest tube.2,3

Tube thoracostomy, hemothorax & the open wound

A chest tube placed through the thoracostomy tract provides definitive, continuous drainage for transport: an adult hemothorax takes a larger tube (28–32 Fr), directed posteriorly and superiorly, with all drainage holes inside the pleura, connected to a Heimlich valve or drain unit and secured.1 A massive hemothorax — roughly ≥ 1–1.5 L of immediate output or ongoing ≥ 200 mL/hr — signals major intrathoracic bleeding requiring blood-based resuscitation (TS-HD-002) and rapid surgical care, and over-rapid drainage of a large hemothorax can worsen hemorrhage by releasing tamponade.1 An open ("sucking") chest wound is sealed with a vented chest seal, then watched for conversion to a tension (burp or decompress).1,2 Throughout, the procedures are documented with side, site, return, and response, and the patient goes to a trauma center.
