CNS Infection

Suspected meningitis / encephalitis
TS-NEU-006 · Sec 6 Neurology
Recognize — suspect CNS infection
  • Fever with headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, or altered mental status.
  • Seizure, focal deficit, or a petechial/purpuric rash (meningococcemia).
  • Infants: fever or hypothermia, poor feeding, irritability, bulging fontanelle.
Antibiotics — give ASAP
Cefotaxime — give to all
50 mg/kg IV over 3–5 min (adult & pediatric).
PLUS one, by age →
Ceftriaxone — age ≥ 3 months
50 mg/kg IV over 3–5 min (max 2 g).
Ampicillin — age < 3 months
100 mg/kg IV over 3–5 min
Supportive care
  • Do not delay antibiotics — give as early as possible; don't wait for imaging/LP.
  • Treat sepsis/shock per protocol (fluids, perfusion); manage seizures and fever.
  • Protect airway in ↓LOC; droplet precautions for the crew.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Findings supporting CNS infection (fever, neck stiffness, AMS, rash, age).
  • Antibiotic(s) — drug, dose, route, time given (door-to-antibiotic matters).
  • Glucose, vitals, neuro exam; sepsis/shock interventions.
  • Allergies; isolation/precautions; destination.
  • Why rotor-wing / level of care.
Overview

Suspected CNS infection (bacterial meningitis/encephalitis) is time-critical — early empiric antibiotics improve outcomes. In transport, antibiotics should be administered as early as possible and should not be delayed for diagnostics.

Management — detail

Give cefotaxime 50 mg/kg IV over 3–5 minutes to all suspected cases, plus a second agent based on age — ceftriaxone 50 mg/kg IV (max 2 g) for patients ≥ 3 months, or ampicillin 100 mg/kg IV for infants < 3 months (to cover Listeria). Provide supportive care for sepsis/shock, manage seizures and fever, protect the airway, and observe droplet precautions. Record the antibiotic administration time.
