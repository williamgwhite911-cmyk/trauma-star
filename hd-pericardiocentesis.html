Pericardiocentesis

Cardiac tamponade · last-resort temporizing
TS-HD-006 · Sec 2 Hemodynamics & Shock
Indications — severe cases only (online medical control)
  • Cardiac tamponade with hemodynamic collapse / peri-arrest: unexplained/obstructive hypotension not responding to fluids.
  • PEA arrest with no other reversible cause identified (tamponade is one of the H's & T's) — esp. narrow-complex PEA.
  • Beck's triad: hypotension + JVD + muffled heart tones (often incomplete). Pulsus paradoxus, tachycardia.
  • Suspect after penetrating "cardiac box" trauma (TS-TR-009/008), post-MI rupture, aortic dissection, pericarditis/effusion, post-cardiac surgery.
  • Ultrasound-confirmed effusion with tamponade physiology, if available. This is a temporizing bridge to definitive surgical care.
Equipment
  • Long needle — 18 G spinal/over-needle catheter, 8–12 cm (or pericardiocentesis kit).
  • Large syringe 20–60 mL + 3-way stopcock; extension tubing.
  • Chlorhexidine, sterile gloves/drape; cardiac monitor; point-of-care ultrasound (preferred).
  • Catheter-over-needle (or Seldinger kit) to leave an indwelling drain for re-aspiration.
Subxiphoid approach — landmarks & procedure
  1. Patient supine, HOB up ~30–45° if tolerated. Monitor + (ideally) US. Prep the subxiphoid area.
  2. Entry point: 1–2 cm inferior & just left of the xiphoid process (angle between xiphoid & left costal margin).
  3. Insert at 30–45° to the skin, aim toward the LEFT shoulder (tip of left scapula), advance slowly with continuous aspiration.
  4. Non-clotting blood / fluid returns → you are in the pericardium. Aspirate; even 10–20 mL can restore perfusion.
  5. ECG watch: ST elevation / ectopy / PVCs = needle touching myocardium → withdraw slightly until they resolve.
  6. Thread the catheter, remove the needle, attach stopcock; secure for repeat aspiration if tamponade re-accumulates.
Cautions
  • Traumatic tamponade is often clotted — may not aspirate; do NOT delay transport to a surgeon (needs thoracotomy / pericardial window). Pericardiocentesis is temporizing.
  • Risks: coronary/myocardial/ventricular laceration, new hemopericardium, pneumothorax, dysrhythmia. Ultrasound guidance markedly reduces complications — use it if available.
  • Continue resuscitation (fluids/blood, pressors) — pericardiocentesis does not replace volume/airway.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Indication (tamponade signs / PEA cause exclusion), US findings, medical-control order.
  • Approach, needle, fluid volume/character aspirated, hemodynamic response, ECG changes.
  • Catheter left in place? complications; cardiac-surgical destination + pre-notification.
Overview — relieving the squeeze on the heart

Cardiac tamponade is accumulation of fluid (blood, effusion) in the pericardial sac under enough pressure to prevent the ventricles from filling, producing obstructive shock and, untreated, pulseless electrical activity and death.1,2 Pericardiocentesis — needle aspiration of that fluid — is a last-resort, life-saving temporizing procedure performed only in severe cases under online medical control, to buy time to a surgeon. Because even a small reduction in pericardial pressure can dramatically improve filling, aspirating as little as 10–20 mL can restore a pulse.1,2

When to do it — the severe-case indications

The protocol restricts this to genuine emergencies: tamponade with hemodynamic collapse — unexplained obstructive hypotension that doesn't respond to fluids — and PEA arrest in which no other reversible cause is found, since tamponade is one of the classic "H's and T's" of PEA and is especially suggested by a narrow-complex PEA.3 The exam clue is Beck's triad — hypotension, distended neck veins, and muffled heart tones — though all three are frequently not present, so the crew also weighs the context: penetrating "cardiac box" trauma (stab/GSW — TS-TR-009/008), recent MI (free-wall rupture), aortic dissection, pericarditis/malignant effusion, or post-cardiac-surgery bleeding.1,2 Where point-of-care ultrasound is available, confirming an effusion with tamponade physiology both supports the indication and guides the needle.

Equipment & the subxiphoid landmarks

The kit is simple: a long (8–12 cm) over-the-needle catheter or spinal needle, a large syringe with a 3-way stopcock, antiseptic and sterile gear, a cardiac monitor, and — ideally — ultrasound.1,2 The classic blind approach is subxiphoid: enter 1–2 cm inferior and just left of the xiphoid process, hold the needle at 30–45° to the skin, and aim toward the left shoulder while aspirating continuously. Return of non-clotting blood or fluid indicates the pericardial space (clotted blood usually means the ventricle). A catheter is then advanced and the needle removed so the line can be left for repeat aspiration if the effusion re-accumulates during transport.1

Avoiding the heart — and the ECG cue

The chief danger is advancing into the myocardium or a coronary vessel, causing laceration and worsening hemopericardium. A key safety cue in the blind technique is the ECG: ST-segment elevation, injury current, or new ventricular ectopy/PVCs mean the needle tip is touching the epicardium — the operator withdraws slightly until they resolve.1 Other risks include pneumothorax, dysrhythmia, and hepatic injury. Ultrasound guidance substantially lowers these complication rates and is used whenever available; it shows the largest, closest fluid pocket and the needle path in real time, which is why image-guided pericardiocentesis is now standard wherever feasible.2

A bridge, not a cure — and which protocols it serves

A crucial limitation: in traumatic tamponade the blood is often clotted and cannot be aspirated, so a "negative" pericardiocentesis does not exclude tamponade, and definitive treatment is surgical (emergency thoracotomy / pericardial window).1,2 The crew therefore does not delay transport to a cardiac-surgical center to attempt the procedure, and continues full resuscitation — fluids/blood and pressors — because pericardiocentesis relieves the obstruction but does not replace volume or airway support. This procedure is the shared rescue step for several pathways: PEA cardiac arrest (reversible-cause "T"), penetrating chest trauma with a cardiac-box wound (TS-TR-008/009), and medical tamponade (post-MI rupture, dissection, effusion) — all of which point here when tamponade is the suspected killer.1,3
