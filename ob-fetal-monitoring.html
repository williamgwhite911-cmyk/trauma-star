Electronic Fetal Monitoring (NICHD)

3-tier categories · terminology
TS-OB-007 · Sec 8 Obstetrics
NICHD 3-tier categories
CAT I Normal
All of: baseline 110–160, moderate variability, no late or variable decels. Early decels & accels may be present/absent. → Routine; predicts normal acid-base.
CAT II Indeterminate
Anything not Cat I or Cat III (e.g., tachycardia, minimal/marked variability, recurrent variables, prolonged decels). → Most tracings; requires surveillance + intrauterine resuscitation.
CAT III Abnormal
Absent variability with any of: recurrent late decels, recurrent variable decels, bradycardia — or a sinusoidal pattern. → Abnormal acid-base; act now, notify MFM/OB.
Variability (10-min window)
  • Absent — undetectable amplitude.
  • Minimal — 0–5 bpm.
  • Moderate — 6–25 bpm (reassuring).
  • Marked — > 25 bpm.
Baseline & accelerations
  • Baseline: mean to nearest 5 bpm over 10 min. Brady < 110 · Normal 110–160 · Tachy > 160.
  • Acceleration: abrupt rise. ≥ 32 wk: ≥ 15 bpm × ≥ 15 sec. < 32 wk: ≥ 10 bpm × ≥ 10 sec.
  • Prolonged accel: ≥ 2 min but < 10 min (≥ 10 min = baseline change).
Decelerations
Early
Gradual (onset→nadir ≥ 30 s); nadir coincides with contraction peak. Head compression — benign.
Late
Gradual (≥ 30 s); nadir after contraction peak. Uteroplacental insufficiency — concerning.
Variable
Abrupt (onset→nadir < 30 s); ≥ 15 bpm, ≥ 15 s, < 2 min. Cord compression.
Prolonged
≥ 15 bpm below baseline, ≥ 2 min but < 10 min (≥ 10 min = baseline change).
Intrauterine resuscitation (Cat II/III)
  • Reposition (left/right lateral); O₂ via NRB; IV fluid bolus.
  • Stop/reduce uterotonics; treat maternal hypotension; correct hypoxia.
  • Notify MFM/OB; document category change + time + interventions.
Why a standardized language exists

Before the NICHD standardized nomenclature, fetal heart-rate strips were described in inconsistent, subjective terms, which made communication between clinicians unreliable and contributed to disagreement and litigation.1,2 The NICHD workshops (1997, updated 2008) created precise definitions for baseline, variability, accelerations, and the four deceleration types, and the resulting 3-tier interpretation system gives every provider — the referring nurse, the transport crew, and the receiving MFM — a shared, reproducible way to say how the fetus is doing.1,2 For Trauma Star this matters operationally: categorizing the strip with NICHD terms is what lets a phone report to the receiving unit actually mean the same thing on both ends.

What the categories actually predict

The system is built around fetal acid-base status. Category I is strongly predictive of normal fetal acid-base balance at the time observed and needs only routine surveillance.1,3 Category III is associated with abnormal acid-base status (fetal acidemia/hypoxia) and demands prompt action — intrauterine resuscitation and rapid escalation, because it signals a fetus that is decompensating.1,3 The crucial honest point is Category II: it is everything in between, it covers the majority of tracings in labor, and it is indeterminate — it neither reassures nor condemns. Category II is therefore a call to watch closely and intervene, not a diagnosis, and a Category II strip that is trending worse (e.g., losing variability, developing recurrent late decelerations) is treated with rising urgency.3

Variability is the single most important feature

Of all the components, moderate baseline variability is the most reassuring single finding, because it reflects an intact fetal autonomic nervous system and is highly predictive of the absence of significant hypoxic acidemia.1,3 Conversely, absent variability combined with recurrent late or variable decelerations, or with bradycardia, is the core of Category III precisely because it implies the fetus can no longer compensate. This is why the protocol asks the crew to assess variability explicitly and why decelerations are interpreted in the context of variability rather than in isolation. The deceleration types carry mechanistic meaning the crew can act on: early decels (head compression) are benign, variable decels suggest cord compression (often responsive to maternal repositioning), and late decels suggest uteroplacental insufficiency (responsive to oxygen, fluids, and reducing uterine activity).1,3

From interpretation to action — intrauterine resuscitation

A category is only useful if it changes what the crew does. A non-reassuring (Category II with concerning features, or Category III) tracing triggers intrauterine resuscitation, which directly targets the likely mechanisms: maternal lateral repositioning (relieves aortocaval and cord compression), high-flow oxygen (raises the maternal-fetal oxygen gradient), an IV fluid bolus (improves uteroplacental perfusion and treats maternal hypotension), and reducing uterine activity by stopping or decreasing uterotonics.3 These are the same supportive measures embedded in the OB transport protocol (TS-OB-001), now applied with urgency and documented as a category change with the time and the interventions. The receiving MFM/OB is notified early, because a Category III tracing en route may be the deciding factor in how and where the patient is delivered.3

Limits, gestational age, and honest use

EFM has well-documented limitations — high inter-observer variability for Category II and a high false-positive rate for predicting actual fetal compromise — which is why it is a screening and surveillance tool, not a stand-alone decision-maker, and why clinical context and trend matter more than any single feature.2,3 Gestational age also changes interpretation: the threshold for an acceleration is lower before 32 weeks, and very preterm fetuses normally show less variability and fewer accelerations, so a strip is read against gestational-age expectations.1 The PHI transport protocol applies EFM at ≥ 23 weeks (or by specific fetal indication), uses it to monitor rate at 18–22 weeks, and falls back to Doppler tones and maternal report of fetal movement when continuous monitoring is not possible — pragmatic rules that match the tool's strengths to the transport environment.4
