Overview — a rare, high-mortality endocrine emergency

Thyroid storm is the extreme, decompensated end of thyrotoxicosis — an exaggerated systemic response to thyroid hormone that produces hyperthermia, severe tachycardia and arrhythmia, agitation or delirium, vomiting and diarrhea, and ultimately cardiovascular collapse.1,2 It is uncommon but carries mortality of roughly 10–30% even when treated, and it is a clinical diagnosis (often scored with the Burch-Wartofsky criteria) because confirmatory thyroid labs return far too slowly to wait for — treatment is started on suspicion.2 In pregnancy the stakes double: maternal thyrotoxicosis threatens both mother (high-output failure, arrhythmia) and fetus, and pregnancy itself (labor, cesarean, infection, preeclampsia) is a common trigger.1,3

Recognize the precipitant — storm is usually "lit" by something

Thyroid storm rarely arises from stable hyperthyroidism alone; it is typically precipitated by an acute stressor — infection, surgery, trauma, labor and delivery, DKA, preeclampsia, or abrupt withdrawal of antithyroid medication.2,3 This is why the protocol's history specifically seeks the precipitating event and the medication history: identifying and treating the trigger (e.g., antibiotics for sepsis) is part of definitive therapy, and a missed infection will keep the storm burning despite antithyroid drugs. The hallmark that separates storm from ordinary hyperthyroidism is fever plus disproportionate tachycardia and altered mental status — temperature is therefore a vital sign that must be measured and trended, not assumed.

Why PTU is the antithyroid drug chosen in pregnancy

Definitive therapy attacks the hormone at several points, but the field/transport piece is the thionamide. Propylthiouracil (PTU) both blocks new thyroid-hormone synthesis and, at high doses, inhibits peripheral conversion of T4 to the more active T3 — a useful dual action in crisis.2 It is the preferred agent in the first trimester because the alternative, methimazole, carries a higher risk of teratogenic embryopathy early in pregnancy; later in pregnancy methimazole is often preferred because PTU carries a rare risk of fulminant hepatotoxicity, which is exactly the kind of risk/benefit judgment that belongs to a physician.3 That is why this protocol treats PTU as an online-order medication — to be discussed with the sending or receiving physician rather than given on standing order.4

The supportive bundle and the order of drugs

Beyond the thionamide, definitive treatment is classically a sequence — a beta-blocker to control the adrenergic storm and arrhythmia, iodine (given after the thionamide to avoid fueling hormone synthesis), and corticosteroids to blunt T4→T3 conversion — but these are physician-directed and often initiated at the receiving facility.2 The transport role is the supportive core: continuous cardiac monitoring (because tachyarrhythmias and high-output failure are the proximate killers), aggressive cooling and antipyresis with acetaminophen, and fluid/electrolyte replacement for the losses from fever, vomiting, and diarrhea. A key teaching point is to avoid aspirin/salicylates for the fever, because they displace thyroid hormone from binding proteins and raise free hormone — hence acetaminophen is the antipyretic of choice.2,4

Caring for the fetus and the dual transport plan

Maternal hyperthermia and tachycardia stress the fetus, and fetal tachycardia or a deteriorating tracing is both a sign of maternal severity and an independent reason to escalate, so fetal monitoring continues per the standard OB transport protocol.4,5 Cooling the mother, controlling her heart rate (under medical direction), and restoring volume all improve uteroplacental conditions. Because thyroid storm needs ICU-level endocrine and cardiac management — and frequently maternal-fetal medicine input on whether and when to deliver — the destination must have those resources, and the crew pre-notifies. From the Keys, rotor-wing transport is what makes timely access to that level of care feasible for a disease measured in hours.4,5
