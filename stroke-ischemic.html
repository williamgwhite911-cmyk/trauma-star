  • Order of preference: titratable infusion first — Nicardipine or Clevidipine; then Labetalol (avoid if bradycardia, asthma/COPD, or heart block); Esmolol for rate control. Max one agent through its range before adding another.
Nicardipine

Labetalol
20 mg IV over 2 min; repeat 40 mg in 10–20 min, then 80 mg q10–20 min (×3) PRN; or 2 mg/min infusion; max total 300 mg.

Cautions & red flags
  • Avoid hypotension/hypoxia — they extend the infarct.
  • Check glucose first — hypoglycemia mimics stroke.
  • Time is brain — don't delay transport; LKW governs eligibility.

Overview & burden of disease

Acute ischemic stroke (AIS) accounts for roughly 87% of all strokes and is a leading cause of long-term adult disability.1 It results from acute occlusion of a cerebral artery — most often thromboembolic — producing a central core of irreversibly infarcted tissue surrounded by a region of hypoperfused but still-viable brain known as the ischemic penumbra. The penumbra is the therapeutic target: it can be salvaged if blood flow is restored quickly, but it progressively converts to infarct as time passes. The entire prehospital and transport mission is therefore organized around one principle — minimize the interval from symptom onset to reperfusion at a capable center.1,2

Why time is the dominant variable

In a typical large-vessel ischemic stroke, an estimated 1.9 million neurons, 14 billion synapses, and 12 km of myelinated fibers are lost per minute that reperfusion is delayed; each hour of untreated occlusion ages the brain roughly 3.6 years.2 This is the quantitative basis for the "time is brain" doctrine and for every system metric that follows — last-known-well (LKW) determination, scene time, pre-notification, and door-to-needle / door-to-puncture times. Because eligibility for reperfusion therapy is defined by elapsed time from LKW (not from symptom discovery), establishing an accurate LKW and documenting it is one of the single most consequential prehospital actions.1,3 Where LKW cannot be established by history, advanced imaging-based selection may still identify salvageable tissue, which is why rapid delivery to an imaging-capable comprehensive center remains valuable even in "wake-up" or unwitnessed strokes.6,7

Recognition & large-vessel-occlusion screening

Field recognition uses a validated instrument (e.g., Cincinnati Prehospital Stroke Scale / BE-FAST) to identify stroke, paired with a large-vessel-occlusion (LVO) screen (e.g., gaze deviation, severe arm weakness, aphasia/neglect, or a quantitative scale such as RACE/LAMS).1,4 The distinction matters operationally: a suspected LVO patient benefits from triage directly to an endovascular-capable (comprehensive) stroke center, even if that means bypassing a closer primary center, because mechanical thrombectomy is the definitive therapy for LVO and is highly time-dependent.1,4,5 Glucose must be checked early because hypoglycemia (and, less often, hyperglycemia) is a classic stroke mimic; correcting glucose may resolve the deficit and avoids transporting a non-stroke as a stroke.1

Reperfusion therapy — why the windows exist

Two complementary strategies restore flow. IV thrombolysis (alteplase, and increasingly tenecteplase) was established by the NINDS trial, which showed improved 90-day functional outcomes when given within 3 hours of onset;5 ECASS III later extended the eligible window to 4.5 hours in selected patients.6 Mechanical thrombectomy for proximal LVO was validated by MR CLEAN and the pooled HERMES analysis (treatment within ~6 hours),4 and the window was then extended to 24 hours in carefully imaging-selected patients by the DAWN and DEFUSE-3 trials.7 The practical message for transport: the patient who appears "too late" by the clock may still be a thrombectomy candidate by imaging, so the destination decision should favor a center that can both image and intervene. None of these therapies are delivered in our aircraft — our role is to preserve the penumbra and shorten the time to the door of the right hospital.1,4,7

Blood-pressure management — why we tolerate hypertension

In AIS, perfusion of the penumbra depends on collateral flow, and collateral flow is pressure-passive — lowering an elevated blood pressure can drop perfusion across the watershed and extend the infarct. For this reason, routine BP reduction is avoided and permissive hypertension is accepted.1,8 Per PHI guidance, antihypertensive treatment is reserved for extreme elevation — SBP > 220 or DBP > 120 — and even then the reduction is limited to roughly 15% of baseline rather than normalization.8 The threshold is different when thrombolysis is planned: BP must be brought to and held ≤ 185/110 before the drug and ≤ 180/105 for 24 hours afterward to limit hemorrhagic transformation risk.1,8 Titratable agents (nicardipine, clevidipine, labetalol, esmolol) are preferred so the reduction can be controlled and reversed; any BP intervention en route should be performed under online medical control and fully documented with the order source and the response.8

Supportive physiology — oxygen, glucose, temperature, position

Secondary injury is minimized by avoiding the insults that enlarge the infarct: maintain SpO₂ ≥ 94% (supplemental O₂ only if hypoxic — routine hyperoxia confers no benefit), avoid hypotension and hypovolemia, treat hypoglycemia, and avoid hyperthermia, all of which independently worsen ischemic outcome.1 Head-of-bed positioning is individualized: flat positioning may augment cerebral perfusion in a patient who tolerates it, while 30° elevation is preferred when there is depressed consciousness, vomiting, or aspiration risk.1 Continuous neurologic and hemodynamic monitoring allows early detection of deterioration in the closed, vibration- and noise-limited cabin environment.

Destination, pre-notification & the Keys time differential
  • Select destination by suspected LVO status and the thrombolysis/thrombectomy window; route LVO patients to an endovascular-capable comprehensive center.1,4
  • Advance hospital notification measurably shortens door-to-imaging and door-to-treatment times and improves outcomes — pre-notify early with LKW, scale score, and LVO screen.1,3
  • From the Florida Keys, rotor-wing transport removes 3+ hours of single-highway ground time; that differential is frequently what keeps a patient inside the thrombolysis or thrombectomy window.3
  • Maintain SpO₂ ≥ 94%, avoid hypotension, and provide continuous neuro/BP monitoring throughout.
Reassess for hemorrhagic conversion

A small fraction of ischemic strokes — and a higher fraction after reperfusion therapy — undergo hemorrhagic transformation. A sudden decline in level of consciousness, new severe headache, vomiting, or an acute rise in blood pressure should prompt immediate reassessment and a switch to the hemorrhagic-stroke pathway (reverse permissive hypertension, target controlled BP, manage as intracranial hemorrhage).1,8 This is why serial neuro exams and BP trends are documented rather than single values.
