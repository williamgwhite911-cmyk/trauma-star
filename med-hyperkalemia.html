Hyperkalemia

Stabilize → shift → (eliminate)
TS-MED-007 · Sec 10 Medical
Treat immediately if…
  • K⁺ > 5.5 with ECG changes (peaked T, ↓/loss of P, wide QRS, sine wave), OR
  • K⁺ > 6.0 with or without ECG changes. Also consider in crush, renal failure/missed dialysis, DKA.
① Stabilize the myocardium — Calcium
Calcium chloride
Adult 1 g IV over 2–10 min · Peds 20 mg/kg (max 1 g); repeat q10 min ×3 PRN. (Faster in arrest/instability.) Flush line.
Calcium gluconate
Adult 3 g IV over 2–10 min · Peds 60 mg/kg (max 3 g); repeat q10 min ×3 PRN. Flush line.
Cautions
Don't mix calcium with bicarb (precipitates) — separate lines/flush. Watch digoxin patients; rapid push → brady/hypotension.
② Shift K⁺ into cells
Albuterol
5 mg neb, repeat q20 min ×3 PRN.
Sodium bicarbonate (if acidosis / arrest)
Adult 50 mEq IV over 5–10 min · Peds 1 mEq/kg IBW (max 50). IV push in arrest. 1 dose; repeats need physician order. Not routine in arrest unless hyperK.
Insulin + glucose
Per attending physician — clarify glucose & insulin type/dose/route before transport.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • K⁺ value + source (cause: renal/dialysis, crush, DKA); ECG changes + trend.
  • Calcium (type/dose/time/repeats), albuterol, bicarb, insulin/glucose orders.
  • Rhythm response; dialysis-capable destination.
Overview — a silent, ECG-diagnosed killer

Hyperkalemia is one of the few electrolyte derangements that kills suddenly, by inducing ventricular dysrhythmia and asystole. In transport there is usually no lab to follow, so it is recognized and treated by the clinical context plus the ECG: the high-risk patient is the one with renal failure or a missed dialysis session, crush injury (see TS-TR-005), or DKA, and the trigger to treat is a K⁺ > 5.5 with ECG changes or > 6.0 regardless.1,2 Treatment follows a fixed three-step logic — stabilize the heart, shift the potassium into cells, then eliminate it — with only the first two achievable in the field.2

Read the ECG — it is the severity meter

Because the danger is cardiac membrane instability, the ECG is both the diagnostic and the severity tool. The classic progression is peaked T waves → PR prolongation and loss of P waves → QRS widening → a sine-wave pattern → VF/asystole.1,2 Any of these changes in a high-risk patient is treated as life-threatening hyperkalemia immediately, without waiting for a number. A widening QRS or sine wave is a peri-arrest finding demanding instant calcium.2

Step 1 — calcium stabilizes, it does not lower K⁺

Calcium (chloride or gluconate) is the first and most important drug: it raises the cardiac myocyte threshold potential, antagonizing potassium's destabilizing effect and protecting against arrhythmia within minutes — but it does not lower the serum potassium, and its effect is temporary, so the shifting agents must follow.2 Calcium chloride contains ~3× the elemental calcium of gluconate (hence the 1 g vs 3 g dosing) and is preferred in arrest/instability but is more sclerosing, ideally given through a secure/central line. Two safety points: calcium precipitates with bicarbonate in the same line (flush between), and it must be used cautiously in digoxin toxicity.1,2

Step 2 — shifting K⁺ into cells

The temporizing measures move potassium from blood into cells while definitive removal (dialysis) is arranged.2 Albuterol (β-2 agonist) drives potassium intracellularly and is easy to deliver by nebulizer. Insulin with glucose is the most reliable shifter (insulin pushes K⁺ into cells; glucose prevents hypoglycemia) — given here under physician direction because it requires glucose co-administration and monitoring. Sodium bicarbonate helps when there is a metabolic acidosis (it shifts K⁺ as pH rises) and is specifically indicated in hyperkalemic cardiac arrest; the protocol notes bicarbonate is otherwise not routine in arrest, reserving it for the hyperkalemic (and acidotic) patient.1,2 None of these remove potassium from the body — that requires dialysis or potassium-binders at the receiving facility, which is the destination driver.

The crush and renal connections

This protocol is the medical counterpart to the trauma crush syndrome pathway (TS-TR-005), where reperfusion dumps potassium into the circulation and the same calcium/shift therapy is anticipated at the moment of extrication.2 The most common transport scenario, though, is the dialysis-dependent renal patient who missed a session — recognizing this population and pre-emptively monitoring the ECG allows treatment before the arrhythmia, and routing to a dialysis-capable facility. Because the field can only stabilize and shift, the clock to definitive potassium removal is part of the urgency.1,2
