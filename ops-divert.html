Principle — default is CONTINUE
  • Trauma Star exists to bypass community EDs and deliver to definitive care. A stop at a community / critical-access ED is the exception, not the reflex.
  • Closest appropriate facility = nearest hospital that can provide immediate airway, circulation, and emergency interventions — even if not a trauma or pediatric center.
  • Unstable or arresting → closest appropriate facility for resuscitation, then secondary transfer. Maintained but sick → continue to the specialty center.
Divert activation — any of these
Patient: worsening shock despite max therapy · respiratory failure / impending arrest unmanageable in flight · cardiac arrest / peri-arrest · rapid neuro decline (GCS fall, status, herniation) · any imminent decompensation if flight continues.
System: original destination on trauma divert / internal disaster · loss of critical capability (CT, OR, ICU, blood, peds) · weather / airspace / mechanical making continued flight unsafe.
EMS may DECLINE a divert and deliver anyway when the patient has an uncontrollable life threat and diverting further would increase morbidity/mortality.
Stabilization stop vs. continue
Stop at closest facility only if ALL THREE
  • 1 · Immediate in-flight threat the crew cannot control in the aircraft (failed airway / can't oxygenate-ventilate; sustained arrest needing ongoing CPR — a moving cabin is a poor compression platform).
  • 2 · The closest facility can plausibly fix or temporize that specific problem (definitive/surgical airway, blood, decompression, code on a stable platform).
  • 3 · Incremental time to the specialty center is large enough that continuing would reasonably cause death/arrest en route.
Continue when the patient is being maintained, when the threat is surgical bleeding the closest ED can't operate on (stopping adds time without the fix), or when the time difference is small (≤ 10 min air).
Time thresholds (incremental AIR time, wheels-up → bedside)
  • Small ≤ 10 min — acceptable for a NOT-unstable patient; reaching definitive resources usually worth it.
  • Large > 10 min (≈20 min presumptively large) — favors the closest appropriate facility when trajectory is uncertain/worsening.
  • Guidelines, not hard limits — weigh against actual trajectory and the crew's ability to hold ABC for the longer leg. Document any departure.
Scene call vs. IFT
SCENE No sending facility — only forward options (closest appropriate vs. continue). Broad standing-order autonomy under field triage; often before medical control is established.
IFT Three options — return to sender (early decline, sender can fix it) · continue to accepting · closest appropriate. Stabilize at sender FIRST — don't fly the uncontrolled. Acceptance doesn't bind the crew.
IFT rule: compare wheels-up-to-bedside time to (a) sending, (b) accepting, (c) closest appropriate — pick whichever delivers temporizing/definitive care fastest for the actual problem. Notify both sending and accepting physicians on any divert.
Pediatric
  • Arrest / peri-arrest → nearest hospital capable of pediatric resuscitation, regardless of designation; secondary transfer after ROSC/stabilization.
  • Still-perfusing, peds expertise critical: small time difference & child tolerating → Nicklaus; large difference or worsening → closest appropriate, then transfer.
  • Community-ED delivery for a peds arrest is a resuscitation stop only — start transfer to the designated pediatric center (Nicklaus Children's) ASAP.
LUCAS — adults only, NOT for kids
  • Do NOT use LUCAS on infants/children. Fit limits (LUCAS 3): chest height 170–303 mm, width ≤ 449 mm. A too-small patient triggers three fast beeps and won't enter ACTIVE mode — do not defeat it.
  • Only a large adolescent with an adult-sized chest who physically fits may be run as an adult. Otherwise manual PALS compressions.
  • No mechanical-CPR option for a small child → effective compressions need a stable ground platform, which favors diverting a peds arrest to the nearest capable ED.
No medical control available
  • If medical control can't be reached and there's an imminent threat to life, the crew may divert to the closest appropriate facility under standing orders without prior physician approval.
  • Document every failed contact attempt (times/methods); state the divert was made under the "no medical control" provision; notify both facilities when comms return.
Documentation
  • Time & reason for divert · patient condition before/after · medical-control involvement (or documented failure) · destination rationale.
  • Both sending & receiving notified (IFT) · outcome at receiving facility. All diverts → QA/QI; "no medical control" cases flagged.
Overview — divert is the exception

An air medical program exists to reduce the time to definitive care by bypassing community emergency departments, so the operating default for a Trauma Star patient is to continue to the planned specialty destination.1,2 A divert — changing destination en route or just before departure — is reserved for the situations where continuing the original plan is reasonably expected to harm the patient: an uncontrollable in-flight emergency, or a destination that can no longer safely receive or definitively treat the patient.3,4 The governing idea throughout is the closest appropriate facility: the nearest hospital that can deliver immediate resuscitation and stabilization, which for an unstable or arresting patient outranks an ideal but more distant specialty center.5,6

Closest appropriate facility & the time thresholds

When divert criteria are met the crew weighs the patient's trajectory against the incremental transport time — the additional wheels-up-to-bedside time to reach the specialty center instead of the nearest capable ED.5 A small time difference (≤ 10 min by air) is generally acceptable for a patient who is not unstable, so reaching definitive resources is usually worth it; a large time difference (> 10 min) shifts the balance toward the closest facility when the trajectory is uncertain or worsening.4,5 These are decision aids, not hard limits: a deteriorating patient may warrant the closest ED even at a small difference, and a rock-stable patient may tolerate more — the crew documents the rationale whenever it departs from the threshold. The single most important judgment is captured by the stabilization-stop-vs-continue rule: stop only when an in-flight threat is genuinely uncontrollable in the cabin, the nearest facility can actually fix or temporize that problem, and the time saved is large enough to matter; otherwise continue, because stopping at a hospital that cannot provide the needed intervention (e.g., a surgeon for torso hemorrhage) only adds time.3,5

Scene calls vs. interfacility transfers

The decision structure differs by mission type. On a scene call there is no sending facility and no accepted plan: the crew works under standing orders and field trauma-triage criteria, frequently before online medical control is established, so divert authority is broad but the only choices are forward — closest appropriate vs. continue.2,7 On an interfacility transfer the patient is already stabilized at a sending hospital and accepted by a receiving physician under an EMTALA-compliant transfer, which adds a third option: returning to the sending facility when deterioration occurs early in transport and that facility can address the problem.4,8 The IFT decision compares time to the sending, accepting, and closest-appropriate facilities and selects whichever delivers temporizing or definitive care fastest. Two IFT principles matter most: stabilize at the sending facility before launch rather than flying an uncontrolled emergency, and recognize that a receiving physician's acceptance does not bind the crew to fly past a closer life-saving option when the patient has an uncontrollable threat.4,8 Any divert from an accepted plan is communicated to both the sending and accepting physicians and documented.

Pediatrics & the LUCAS limitation

Critically ill children magnify the closest-appropriate principle, because timely stabilization supersedes the ideal specialty destination when delay increases risk.9,10 A child in cardiac arrest or peri-arrest is taken to the nearest hospital capable of pediatric resuscitation regardless of trauma designation, with secondary transfer to the designated pediatric center (Nicklaus Children's) once return of spontaneous circulation or adequate stabilization is achieved; a still-perfusing child for whom pediatric expertise is critical may continue to the pediatric center when the time difference is small and the child tolerates it.9,11 A decisive operational constraint is that mechanical CPR (LUCAS) is an adult-only device governed by physical size limits, not age: the LUCAS 3 fits a chest height of 170–303 mm and self-rejects a patient who is too small, so it cannot be used on infants and small children, for whom manual PALS compressions remain standard.12 Because effective manual compressions are nearly impossible in a vibrating cabin and no mechanical option exists for a small child, a pediatric arrest favors diverting to the nearest capable ED for resuscitation on a stable platform — only a large adolescent who physically fits the device can be managed with mechanical CPR and a continued flight.12

Authority, no-medical-control, and review

Any crew member may initiate an urgent divert request; the senior medical crew member makes the clinical destination decision when medical control cannot be reached, and the pilot-in-command retains final authority over aviation-safety diversions.13,7 Air-medical medical-direction frameworks and regional destination protocols explicitly authorize crews to act under standing orders when real-time medical control is unavailable, provided they document the failed-contact attempts and rationale — a provision built for the long over-water and remote legs of Keys operations.13,8 Every divert is logged and reviewed by the program's QA/QI process, with all "no medical control" cases specifically flagged, so that destination decisions can be audited against the regional trauma system and crew education refined.14,4 Florida sets the regulatory framework (Ch. 64J-1 air-ambulance and 64J-2 trauma-system rules), while the operative divert procedures are implemented at the county/regional and hospital level — this protocol is the Trauma Star expression of that framework.15
