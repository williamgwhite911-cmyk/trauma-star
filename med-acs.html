Acute Coronary Syndrome / STEMI

12-lead · Sgarbossa · ASA / NTG / heparin
TS-MED-002 · Sec 10 Medical
Assess / ECG
  • Apply multifunction/AED pads empirically — anterior-posterior preferred (anticipate arrhythmia).
  • 12-lead: rate, rhythm, axis, ischemia/injury/infarct location. Inferior MI → right-sided V4R for RVMI.
  • Posterior leads (V7–V9) if inferior/lateral ischemia, anterior ST depression, or tall R in V2–V3.
STEMI criteria / Sgarbossa
  • STEMI: ST elevation ≥ 0.1 mV in ≥ 2 contiguous leads, or new LBBB. Treat timely per system.
  • Sgarbossa (LBBB/paced), ≥ 3 pts = likely MI: concordant STE ≥ 1 mm (5) · concordant ST depression V1–V3 (3) · discordant STE ≥ 5 mm (2).
Treatment
Aspirin
162–324 mg chewed (2–4 baby ASA).
Oxygen
Only if SpO₂ < 90% → titrate to 90–95%.
Nitroglycerin
SL 0.4 mg repeat PRN; or infusion 40–200 mcg/min (titrate 20–50 mcg/min q3–5 min).
RVMI / SBP < 110 (inferior MI)
NS bolus 100–500 mL, repeat to maintain vitals if lungs clear.
Heparin
60 u/kg bolus (max 4,000), then 12 u/kg/hr (max 1,000/hr).
Nitro — do NOT give if…
  • PDE5 inhibitor: avanafil < 12 h, sildenafil/vardenafil < 24 h, tadalafil < 48 h (severe hypotension).
  • Caution: inferior/RV MI (preload-dependent), nitrate-naïve, elderly, hypotension. Treat hypotension with supine + fluids.
Reperfusion / IFT
  • Fibrinolytics (alteplase/tenecteplase) + antiplatelets (clopidogrel/ticagrelor) may be given before/at referral — collaborative with sending/receiving.
  • Post-fibrinolytic: anticipate reperfusion arrhythmias & bleeding; serial neuro exam.
  • Manage reperfusion arrhythmias with standard measures; recurrent VT/VF may signal ongoing occlusion.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Pain onset/character; serial 12-leads (+ V4R/posterior); STEMI/Sgarbossa findings.
  • ASA/NTG/heparin (dose/time); PDE5i screen; fluids for RVMI; fibrinolytic status + neuro exam.
  • PCI-capable destination + pre-notification; arrhythmias/defib.
Overview — time is myocardium

Acute coronary syndrome spans unstable angina, NSTEMI, and STEMI — the last being an acute total coronary occlusion in which "time is myocardium." Outcome is driven by the interval to reperfusion, so the transport mission is to diagnose early, give the time-critical drugs, prevent/treat lethal arrhythmia, and deliver to a PCI-capable center with advance notification.1,5 From the Keys, the air-vs-ground differential is frequently what keeps a STEMI patient inside the window for primary PCI.

The ECG — finding the occlusion others miss

A single 12-lead is a snapshot, so the protocol uses serial and extended ECGs. Every inferior MI gets a right-sided V4R because up to ~40% involve the right ventricle, which changes management dramatically.2 Posterior leads (V7–V9) are added when the standard ECG hints at a posterior infarct (anterior ST depression, tall R in V2–V3) that would otherwise be missed.3 In LBBB or paced rhythms, ordinary ST criteria fail, so the Sgarbossa criteria (concordance is the key concept — ST shifts in the same direction as the QRS) identify MI that would otherwise be electrocardiographically silent.4 Multifunction pads are placed pre-emptively in anterior-posterior position because these patients can arrest from VF without warning.1

Aspirin and the oxygen reversal

Aspirin is the highest-yield drug in ACS — chewed for rapid antiplatelet effect, it reduces mortality and is given to essentially all suspected ACS.1,5 Oxygen, by contrast, is a deliberate reversal of old teaching: routine supplemental oxygen in the non-hypoxic ACS patient confers no benefit and may cause harm through coronary vasoconstriction and reperfusion injury (AVOID-trial era evidence), so it is given only for SpO₂ < 90% and titrated to 90–95%.5 This "treat the number, not the diagnosis" approach is now standard.

Nitroglycerin — and the two dangerous traps

Nitroglycerin relieves ischemic pain by venodilation (reducing preload and wall stress) and some coronary dilation, but it carries two field-defining contraindications.1 First, with recent PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil/vardenafil < 24 h, tadalafil < 48 h, avanafil < 12 h) the combination causes profound, refractory hypotension. Second, in right-ventricular / inferior MI the failing RV is preload-dependent, so nitrate-induced venodilation can drop cardiac output catastrophically — which is exactly why every inferior MI gets a V4R and why RVMI hypotension is treated with fluid boluses rather than nitrates.1,2 These are the reasons the protocol pairs nitro with an explicit screen and a fluid pathway.

Anticoagulation, reperfusion & the IFT context

Heparin (and, for many STEMIs being moved for PCI, antiplatelets and sometimes fibrinolytics already given at the referring hospital) prevents clot propagation while the patient travels to definitive care.1,5 Because many Trauma Star ACS runs are interfacility transfers, care is explicitly collaborative with the sending and receiving physicians, particularly around thrombolytic/anticoagulant timing. After fibrinolytics the crew anticipates reperfusion arrhythmias and bleeding and performs serial neuro exams to detect intracranial hemorrhage; recurrent VT/VF can signal ongoing occlusion (failed reperfusion) and is managed with standard ACLS while expediting to PCI.1,5
