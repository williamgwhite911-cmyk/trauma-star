Smoke Inhalation & Cyanide

Fire/smoke · CO + CN · Cyanokit
TS-TOX-002 · §7 Toxicology
☎️
Poison Control (24/7)
1-800-222-1222
When to suspect cyanide
Exposure: enclosed-space structure fire — especially plastics, synthetics, wool, silk.
Treat empirically if smoke inhalation + any of:
  • Altered mental status (GCS < 15)
  • Seizures
  • Hypotension (SBP < 90) or shock
  • Cardiac arrest
  • Lactate ≥ 8 mmol/L (if quickly available)
  • Persistent metabolic acidosis (pH < 7.25)
Do NOT wait for lab confirmation if the patient is unstable.
Immediate actions (scene & transport)
  • Remove from smoke — scene safety / decon as needed.
  • High-flow 100% O₂ via NRB or advanced airway (treats both CO and CN).
  • Secure airway early if GCS < 8, respiratory distress, or suspected airway edema.
  • IV/IO access; monitor cardiac, SpO₂, glucose, EtCO₂ (if intubated).
  • Obtain lactate if point-of-care available — but don't delay the antidote in a critical patient.
  • Notify the receiving facility of suspected cyanide toxicity + ETA.
Cyanokit (hydroxocobalamin)
Adult
5 g (1 vial) in 100 mL NS → infuse over 15 min IV/IO.
Pediatric
70 mg/kg (max 5 g) in NS to 100 mL → over 15 min
Repeat
May repeat once if response inadequate — max total 10 g. In cardiac arrest may push undiluted.
Prep
Reconstitute vial with one 100 mL NS bag via transfer spike; gently invert (don't shake); infuse over 15 min; use a dedicated line (incompatibilities).
Concurrent management
Carbon monoxide
100% O₂ continuously; consider hyperbaric if COHb > 25%, pregnant + COHb > 15%, seizures, LOC, or arrhythmia.
Bronchospasm
Albuterol / ipratropium nebulizers.
Airway edema
Early intubation for stridor, hoarseness, facial burns, or progressive swelling.
Seizures
Benzodiazepine (see Seizures, TS-NEU-004).
Hypotension
Fluids; vasopressors if refractory after cyanide treatment.
Precautions & side effects
  • No absolute contraindications. Caution: known B12 allergy (rare). Pregnancy — use if benefit > risk.
  • Expected: red/orange discoloration of skin, urine, IV fluid (harmless, days); transient hypertension.
  • May interfere with colorimetric lab tests — notify the lab/receiving.
  • Cyanokit is preferred over nitrite-based kits (safer; no effect on CO carrying).
Handoff & documentation — medically necessary
  • Fire exposure (time, enclosure, materials burned); symptom-onset time.
  • GCS, vitals, lactate, COHb (if available).
  • Cyanokit — dose, time, response; any repeat.
  • Airway interventions, O₂ delivery; other treatments (seizure control, bronchodilators, pressors).
  • Request hyperbaric evaluation if CO and/or CN toxicity suspected.
  • Why rotor-wing / level of care.
Overview

Enclosed-space fires produce both carbon monoxide and hydrogen cyanide. Cyanide blocks cellular oxygen use (cytochrome oxidase), causing rapid neurologic, cardiac, and respiratory collapse and a high lactate / metabolic acidosis. Because confirmatory testing is slow, hydroxocobalamin is given empirically when smoke exposure occurs with shock, altered mental status, seizures, arrest, or a high lactate.1,2

Management — detail

Remove from smoke, give high-flow 100% oxygen (treats CO and supports CN), and secure the airway early when GCS is low or airway burns/edema are suspected. Give hydroxocobalamin (Cyanokit) 5 g in 100 mL NS over 15 minutes (pediatric 70 mg/kg, max 5 g), repeatable once to a 10 g maximum, through a dedicated line. Manage CO with sustained oxygen and hyperbaric referral for severe features, treat bronchospasm, airway edema, seizures, and post-antidote hypotension, and arrange hyperbaric evaluation at the receiving facility. Hydroxocobalamin is preferred over nitrite-based antidotes because it does not impair oxygen carrying and is safer in mixed CO/CN exposure.1,3
