Crush Injury / Crush Syndrome

Pre-release fluids · hyperkalemia
TS-TR-005 · Sec 9 Trauma
Recognize
  • Torso or mid/proximal extremity crushed & entrapped > 1 hour → risk of crush syndrome.
  • On release: surge of K⁺, myoglobin, acid → hyperkalemia, arrhythmia/VF, AKI, death.
  • Start IV fluids BEFORE extrication / release when possible.
Fluids — Sodium Chloride 0.9%
Initial bolus
Adult/Peds 40 mL/kg IBW (max 2 L).
After initial bolus
20 mL/kg IBW over 1 hour (max 1 L).
Maintenance
1.5 × maintenance rate. May substitute LR.
Monitor / anticipate hyperkalemia
  • Cardiac monitor + 12-lead: peaked T waves, wide QRS, sine wave; treat arrhythmia.
  • Anticipate VF/arrest at the moment of release; have defib + hyperkalemia drugs ready (calcium, bicarbonate per medical control).
  • Trend urine output / color (myoglobinuria — tea-colored).
Supportive
  • Treat associated hemorrhage/fractures; keep warm; analgesia.
  • Avoid potassium-containing fluids if marked hyperkalemia (favor NS for the resuscitation phase).
  • Transport to a facility with critical-care/renal (dialysis) capability.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Entrapment time + body region; time of release; fluids started pre/post release.
  • Fluid type/volumes/rate; ECG changes; urine output/color; arrhythmia treatment.
  • Destination rationale (renal/critical care).
Overview — the danger is the release, not the crush

Crush injury is direct trauma from sustained compression; crush syndrome is the systemic, potentially fatal consequence that appears after the compressing force is removed.1,2 While a limb is compressed, injured muscle leaks its contents into the trapped tissue but they stay sequestered. The moment circulation is restored, a bolus of potassium, myoglobin, phosphate, and acid washes into the central circulation — which is why a patient can look stable under the rubble and then arrest seconds after extrication. The syndrome can develop after as little as one hour of entrapment of the torso or a proximal extremity, and the entire protocol is built around anticipating and blunting that reperfusion surge.1,2

Why fluids before release is the key move

The single most important intervention is aggressive isotonic fluid started before extrication whenever access allows.1,2,3 Pre-loading the circulation does three things: it expands intravascular volume to counter the profound third-spacing and hypotension that follow release, it dilutes the potassium surge to reduce the risk of lethal arrhythmia, and it drives a brisk urine output that flushes myoglobin through the kidney before it precipitates and causes acute kidney injury (rhabdomyolysis-induced AKI).2,3 The protocol uses normal saline for the resuscitation phase — large-volume 40 mL/kg IBW initial bolus, then 20 mL/kg over an hour, then 1.5× maintenance — deliberately avoiding potassium-containing fluids during the period of greatest hyperkalemia risk (LR may be substituted once that risk is lower).1,3

Hyperkalemia — the proximate killer

The cause of death in early crush syndrome is usually hyperkalemic cardiac arrest, so the crew treats the ECG, not just the potassium number it cannot measure.2 Continuous monitoring watches for the classic progression — peaked T waves → widening QRS → loss of P waves → sine wave → VF/asystole — and these changes, especially appearing around the time of release, are treated as hyperkalemia. Definitive field therapy (IV calcium to stabilize the myocardium, plus shifting agents such as bicarbonate, and inhaled beta-agonist) is given per medical control, and the team should literally have calcium and the defibrillator ready at the instant of extrication.2 Bicarbonate also helps by alkalinizing the urine, which keeps myoglobin soluble and protects the kidney.3

Tourniquets, the limb, and supportive care

There is genuine controversy about tourniquet use in crush: a tourniquet placed before release can prevent the reperfusion washout, but it also guarantees ischemic loss of the limb and merely delays the metabolic insult, so it is generally reserved for life-threatening hemorrhage or as a deliberate, physician-guided decision rather than a routine crush maneuver.2,3 The rest of care is supportive and shares the trauma principles: control associated hemorrhage and fractures, provide analgesia, and aggressively prevent hypothermia (the cold, acidotic, coagulopathic crush patient is fragile).1 Because the downstream course frequently includes dialysis for AKI and hyperkalemia, the destination must have critical-care and renal capability — from the Keys, this is a clear indication for rotor-wing transport to a tertiary center.2,3
