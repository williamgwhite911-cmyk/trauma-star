CHF / Acute Pulmonary Edema

NIV · high-dose nitroglycerin
TS-MED-003 · Sec 10 Medical
Recognize
  • Dyspnea, rales, S3/S4, tachypnea, tachycardia, oliguria; hypoxia.
  • Triggers: ischemia/MI, dysrhythmia, med/diet noncompliance, valvular, hypertensive crisis.
Ventilation & oxygen — first
  • Start NIV (CPAP/BiPAP) as soon as practicable; escalate to intubation PRN.
  • Oxygen 2–25 L/min titrated to SpO₂ 93–99%.
Nitroglycerin (afterload/preload reduction)
Infusion (preferred)
40–200 mcg/min IV; titrate 20–50 mcg/min q3–5 min to effect.
SL (if infusion not ready)
0.4 mg SL, repeat PRN.
Cautions
Hold for PDE5 inhibitors; caution if hypotensive or RV/inferior MI. SBP-dependent.
Hypotension / poor perfusion
  • Do NOT give nitro if hypotensive. Treat poor perfusion / cardiogenic shock per Shock & CV Instability (pressors, not large fluid boluses).
  • Distinguish from sepsis/hypovolemia — wet lungs + hypotension suggests cardiogenic shock.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Work of breathing, lung exam, SpO₂; NIV settings + response; 12-lead (ischemic trigger?).
  • NTG dose/route/titration; BP trend; pressors if cardiogenic shock.
  • Suspected precipitant; destination.
Overview — a pump-and-pressure problem, not a fluid problem

Acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema is failure of the left ventricle to move blood forward, causing pressure to back up into the lungs and flood the alveoli. The patient is drowning in their own plasma, but is usually total-body fluid overloaded or merely redistributed — not volume depleted — so the treatment is the opposite of most shock: reduce the load on the heart and open the alveoli, not give fluids.1,3 The two interventions that reverse the crisis fastest in the field are non-invasive ventilation and nitroglycerin.2,3

Why NIV is the single highest-yield move

CPAP/BiPAP works through several mechanisms at once: positive intrathoracic pressure pushes alveolar fluid back into the capillaries and recruits collapsed alveoli (improving oxygenation), and it reduces both preload and LV afterload, directly unloading the failing ventricle.2 Strong evidence shows NIV in acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema improves dyspnea and oxygenation and reduces the need for intubation (and likely mortality), which is why the protocol starts it "as soon as practicable" rather than watching the patient tire toward intubation.2 In the transport environment, avoiding intubation is especially valuable, and NIV buys time for the pharmacology to work. Intubation is reserved for NIV failure or the patient who can no longer protect the airway.

High-dose nitroglycerin and the oxygen target

Nitroglycerin is the pharmacologic workhorse: at low doses it venodilates (reducing preload), and at the higher infusion doses used in acute pulmonary edema it also reduces afterload, which is often the dominant problem in the hypertensive "flash" pulmonary edema patient.1,3 The protocol allows generous titration (40–200 mcg/min) because the well-perfused, hypertensive APE patient tolerates and benefits from aggressive afterload reduction. The same nitrate cautions apply as in ACS — recent PDE5 inhibitors and preload-dependent RV/inferior MI — and nitro is withheld if the patient is hypotensive.1 Oxygen is titrated to SpO₂ 93–99% rather than run wide open, consistent with avoiding hyperoxia.3

The critical fork — APE vs cardiogenic shock

The decisive bedside distinction is blood pressure. The hypertensive APE patient is treated with NIV and aggressive nitrates and improves rapidly. The hypotensive patient with pulmonary edema is in cardiogenic shock — the pump is failing — and giving nitrates or large fluid boluses there is dangerous; these patients need inotropic/vasopressor support per the Shock & Cardiovascular Instability pathway and rapid transport to a center capable of advanced cardiac care (cath lab, mechanical support).1,3 Recognizing which patient is in front of you — wet lungs with high vs low pressure — is the most important judgment in this protocol. A 12-lead is obtained because acute MI is a common precipitant that changes destination.1
