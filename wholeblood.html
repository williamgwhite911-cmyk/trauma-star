Whole Blood — Field Administration

Hemorrhagic shock · LTOWB
TS-HD-002 · Sec 2 Hemodynamics & Shock
Indications — hemorrhagic shock
  • Suspected/known traumatic hemorrhage with shock: SBP < 90 mmHg, or HR ≥ 110 with SBP ≤ 90 (shock index > 1), or SBP < 70.
  • Class III–IV shock / poor perfusion (weak-absent radial pulse, altered mentation, pallor) from hemorrhage.
  • Non-compressible torso, pelvic, junctional, or major external hemorrhage after bleeding control measures.
  • Consider in non-trauma hemorrhagic shock (GI bleed, ruptured AAA, OB hemorrhage) per medical direction.
Product & dosing
Low-Titer O Whole Blood (LTOWB)
Adult 1 unit (~500 mL) IV/IO · Peds 10–20 mL/kg IV/IO — titrate to endpoint; repeat per protocol / medical control.
Resuscitation target (permissive hypotension)
palpable radial pulse / SBP ~90  ·  TBI: SBP ≥ 110 / MAP ≥ 80  ·  Peds: age-appropriate SBP / strong central pulse. Stop bolusing once perfusing.
Calcium chloride (citrate-induced hypocalcemia)
Adult CaCl₂ 1 g IV/IO · Peds 20 mg/kg IV/IO (max 1 g) — with the 1st unit; repeat per protocol. Central/large vein preferred.
Calcium gluconate (alternative)
Adult Ca gluconate 3 g IV/IO · Peds 60 mg/kg IV/IO (max 3 g) — with the 1st unit; repeat per protocol.
TXA (adjunct, if < 3 h from injury)
≥ 12 yr / adult 2 g IV over 10 min (initial) · < 12 yr (children) 15 mg/kg IV over 10 min (max 1 g), then 2 mg/kg/hr infusion if ongoing hemorrhage (per medical control). Do not delay blood for TXA.9,10,11
Procedure — infuse via fluid warmer
  • Control bleeding first — tourniquet, wound packing/pressure, pelvic binder, splinting. Obtain order / contact medical control per protocol.
  • Verify the unit (two-person): LTOWB, group O low-titer, unit/segment number, ABO/Rh label, expiration, and integrity (no clots, discoloration, leaks). Record the unit number.
  • Access: two large-bore IVs (≥18 g) or IO. Use a blood administration set with in-line filter (170–260 µm).
  • Fluid/blood warmer: use a device rated for blood; power on and let it reach ~38 °C — never exceed 42 °C (hemolysis). Prime the warmer and tubing per the device IFU.
  • Connect & infuse: run through the warmer; rapid for shock (pressure bag / rapid infuser within the device's rated flow). Titrate to endpoint. Only 0.9% NaCl is line-compatible — never LR or calcium-containing fluid in the same line (clots citrated blood).
  • Give calcium with the first unit; consider TXA. Keep the patient warm (blankets, warm cabin).
  • Monitor vitals + temp q5 min; watch for transfusion reaction. Reassess endpoint; repeat units per protocol.
Cautions & red flags
  • Blood does not replace hemorrhage control — control the source.
  • Hypothermia worsens coagulopathy — always warm blood and patient; avoid over-cooling.
  • Hypocalcemia (citrate): give calcium with transfusion; watch for hypotension/arrhythmia.
  • Transfusion reaction — fever, hypotension, dyspnea, urticaria, hemoglobinuria: stop, maintain access with NS, support airway/BP, document.
  • Caution with TACO/volume overload in cardiac/renal disease — titrate to endpoint, don't over-transfuse.
  • Honor documented refusal (e.g., faith-based) per consent/protocol.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Indication: mechanism, signs of hemorrhagic shock, and vitals (SBP, HR, shock index, perfusion) with times.
  • Order obtained / medical control contacted (name, time).
  • Product: unit/segment number(s), LTOWB, ABO/Rh, volume infused, expiration; two-person verification.
  • Warmer used and set temperature; start/stop time of each unit.
  • Adjuncts: calcium (dose/time), TXA (dose/time).
  • Response: serial vitals/mentation, endpoint reached, any reaction and management.
  • Why rotor-wing / this level of care was required (time-critical hemorrhage, capability/time differential).
Overview

Hemorrhage is the leading cause of preventable death after injury, and most of those deaths occur early — within the first hour(s), often before hospital arrival. Survival is time-dependent and tracks how quickly balanced resuscitation and hemorrhage control are achieved. Low-titer group O whole blood (LTOWB) provides red cells, plasma, and platelets together in near-physiologic proportions in a single product — replacing what is lost rather than diluting it — and removes the time/logistics of thawing and mixing separate components. Moving transfusion to the point of injury shortens time-to-resuscitation; national/international consensus bodies (THOR-AABB, the civilian Prehospital Blood Transfusion Coalition, EAST, TCCC, JTS) recommend prehospital blood, and prospective prehospital LTOWB programs have proven feasible and safe without excess transfusion reactions.1,2,4,5,6

Pathophysiology & why each step

Trauma-induced coagulopathy begins within minutes of major injury — before much fluid is given — driven by shock, endothelial activation, and fibrinolysis, and is amplified by the self-reinforcing "lethal diamond" (hypothermia, acidosis, coagulopathy, hypocalcemia).3,7

Why whole blood, not crystalloid: crystalloid carries neither oxygen nor clotting factors; large volumes dilute remaining factors, dislodge forming clot, and worsen acidosis and mortality. Balanced component resuscitation in ~1:1:1 ratio improved hemostatic outcomes in the PROPPR trial, and whole blood delivers that balance in one bag.5,8

Why permissive hypotension: until surgical/IR control, target the lowest pressure that maintains perfusion (palpable radial pulse / SBP ~90) to avoid dislodging clot — with a higher target when TBI is present to protect cerebral perfusion.3

Why calcium & warming: stored blood is citrate-anticoagulated and cold — citrate chelates ionized calcium (hypocalcemia impairs coagulation and cardiac function) so calcium is repleted early, and cold product worsens coagulopathy so all product is warmed.2,3,7

Why early TXA: tranexamic acid reduces fibrinolysis and lowered death due to bleeding in CRASH-2, with benefit limited to administration within 3 hours of injury (and possible harm later) — hence the time window.3,9

TXA in children: pediatric trauma is increasingly treated with TXA by extrapolation from adult data plus growing pediatric evidence. The military PED-TRAX analysis found TXA in injured children was independently associated with improved survival and no increase in thromboembolic complications, and pediatric major-hemorrhage / trauma protocols recommend a weight-based load with an optional infusion.10,11 Trauma Star uses 15 mg/kg IV over 10 min (max 1 g) for children < 12 yr (≥ 12 yr use the adult 2 g dose), with an optional 2 mg/kg/hr infusion for ongoing hemorrhage under medical control — given within 3 hours of injury and never delaying blood. The 1 g bolus cap matches Royal College of Paediatrics / NICE major-trauma and most pediatric trauma-center protocols (a weight-scaled CRASH-2 regimen).10,11

In-flight considerations
  • Maintain and document the blood's cold-chain/temperature until administration; follow your unit's storage and monitoring procedure.
  • Pre-flight: confirm the warmer is charged/functional and an approved blood set + NaCl are stocked; plan IO backup.
  • Keep the cabin warm and the patient insulated; vibration/limited lighting make the two-person unit check and reaction monitoring harder — slow down for the safety check.
  • Anticipate calcium and TXA needs before they're urgent; pre-draw/label.
Management — detail

Give LTOWB for hemorrhagic shock using the indication thresholds above; published prehospital criteria use SBP ≤ 90 with HR ≥ 108, or SBP ≤ 70, in patients at risk of hemorrhage.1,6 Titrate to a palpable radial pulse / SBP ~90, raising the target for suspected TBI.4 Repeat units and activate massive-transfusion/receiving-facility notification per protocol. Replete calcium with transfusion — civilian prehospital guidance suggests calcium after the first 1–2 units (CaCl₂ 1 g or Ca gluconate 3 g), with military guidance giving 1 g with the first unit and after every 4 units.2,3 Add TXA within 3 hours of injury if not contraindicated, without delaying blood.4 Infuse all product through a warmer set to ~38 °C (never >42 °C) via a blood set with in-line filter, using only 0.9% NaCl as a companion fluid.3,7 Monitor continuously for transfusion reaction and stop the infusion if one is suspected.
