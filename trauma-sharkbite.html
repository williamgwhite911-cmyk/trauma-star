Shark Bite / Marine Bite

Hemorrhage control + marine-organism antibiotics
TS-TR-012 · Sec 9 Trauma
Priorities — hemorrhage kills first
  • Control catastrophic hemorrhage immediately — shark bites cause arterial/major soft-tissue loss; bleeding (often an extremity) is the leading cause of death.
  • Get the patient out of the water; blood loss is grossly underestimated in water. Beware concurrent drowning, immersion hypothermia, C-spine.
  • Then MARCH / ABCDE (TS-TR-001).
Hemorrhage control — in order
  • Tourniquet (CAT/SOF-TT) high & tight for any life-threatening extremity bleed — apply early, record the time. Second tourniquet above the first if not controlled.
  • Wound packing + hemostatic gauze for junctional/deep wounds; firm direct pressure.
  • Pelvic binder if pelvis involved; splint mangled/fractured limbs.
  • Amputation: control stump bleeding; wrap part in saline-damp gauze → sealed bag → on ice water (not frozen) — see TS-TR-004.
  • Embedded teeth = impaled object — stabilize, do not remove (may tamponade).
Resuscitation
  • Blood products preferred (TS-HD-002); permissive hypotension SBP ~90 (100–110 if TBI). Transfuse on shock index ≥ 0.8.
  • TXA < 3 h — ≥ 12 yr 2 g IV over 10 min; < 12 yr 15 mg/kg (max 1 g).
  • Two large-bore IV/IO; high-flow O₂; keep warm (prevent lethal triad).
Antibiotics — give early (marine wound)
Ceftriaxone (Rocephin) — broad cover
Adult/≥ 40 kg 2 g in 50 mL NS IV over 10 min · Peds > 28 d 50 mg/kg (max 2 g).
PLUS Doxycycline — covers Vibrio / Aeromonas
Adult 100 mg IV. Ordinary cephalosporins/cefazolin do NOT cover Vibrio vulnificus — add doxycycline (or a fluoroquinolone). Peds < 8 yr: doxycycline per medical control (benefit outweighs risk in serious Vibrio).
Cephalosporin allergy
Ciprofloxacin 400 mg IV (or levofloxacin) + Clindamycin 900 mg IV (peds 10 mg/kg, max 900).
Wound
Irrigate gross debris; cover with saline-moistened sterile dressing. Tetanus update at receiving. Relay to receiving for surgical washout.
Transport
  • Highest-level trauma center (vascular/ortho/plastics + washout); pre-notify, activate massive transfusion for shock.
  • Serial exam & tourniquet reassessment; from the Keys, rotor-wing removes 3+ h of ground time.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Mechanism, water environment (salt/brackish), time in water, estimated blood loss.
  • Tourniquet time(s), packing, blood/TXA, access; antibiotics (drug/dose/time); amputated-part care.
  • Serial vitals/shock index; trauma-center destination + pre-notification.
Overview — two problems in one wound

A shark bite is simultaneously a massive-hemorrhage / mangled-extremity injury and a heavily contaminated marine wound. The immediate killer is exsanguination — large, serrated teeth shred muscle and divide major arteries, and most shark-attack deaths are from hemorrhagic shock, frequently from a femoral or other extremity vessel.1,2 The delayed killer is infection with unusual marine organisms. The protocol therefore stacks two priorities onto the core trauma pathway (TS-TR-001, TS-HD-002): aggressive hemorrhage control and damage-control resuscitation first, then early broad antibiotics that specifically cover marine bacteria.1,3

Hemorrhage control is the priority

As with any major extremity hemorrhage, the evidence-based ladder applies and the tourniquet goes on early and high for life-threatening limb bleeding — battlefield and civilian data show early tourniquets save lives with rare limb loss, and a second tourniquet is added above the first if bleeding continues.2,4 Junctional and deep wounds are packed with hemostatic gauze; a pelvic binder is applied for pelvic involvement; and mangled limbs are splinted. Two water-specific traps matter: blood loss is grossly underestimated in water (it disperses and dilutes), so the crew resuscitates to the clinical picture and shock index rather than the visible loss; and the patient may have drowned, become hypothermic, or sustained C-spine injury from the attack or rescue, so a full survey and active warming are essential. Embedded shark teeth are treated as impaled objects — stabilized, not removed, since they may be tamponading a vessel.1

Damage-control resuscitation

Resuscitation follows the whole-blood pathway: blood products over crystalloid, permissive hypotension to a radial pulse / SBP ~90 (100–110 with concurrent TBI), TXA within 3 hours, calcium with transfusion, and aggressive warming to counter the lethal triad.2,4 Because beta-blocked patients and immersion-cooled patients may not mount a tachycardia, the crew uses the shock index (≥ 0.8) and perfusion rather than heart rate alone to trigger transfusion, and activates massive transfusion early. The single highest-yield disposition decision is rapid delivery to a trauma center with vascular, orthopedic, and plastic-surgery capability and an OR — from the Keys, the helicopter collapses the multi-hour ground option.1,2

Why marine wounds need special antibiotics

This is the teaching point that distinguishes a shark bite from a land injury: shark and other marine wounds are contaminated with marine bacteria — chiefly Vibrio species (notably Vibrio vulnificus) and Aeromonas, in addition to ordinary skin flora.3,5 Vibrio vulnificus in particular can cause fulminant necrotizing soft-tissue infection and septic shock with high mortality, especially in patients with liver disease, diabetes, or immunocompromise.5 Crucially, the first-generation cephalosporins used for routine open fractures (cefazolin/Ancef) do NOT reliably cover Vibrio. Marine-wound guidance therefore adds an agent active against Vibrio/Aeromonas — a third-generation cephalosporin (ceftriaxone) PLUS doxycycline (or a fluoroquinolone) — given early because time-to-antibiotic drives infection outcome.3,5 In children under 8, doxycycline is normally avoided, but for serious suspected Vibrio infection its benefit outweighs the dental-staining risk and it is used under medical control; a fluoroquinolone is an alternative.5

Wound care, tetanus & definitive treatment

In the field the wound is irrigated of gross debris and covered with a saline-moistened sterile dressing — definitive irrigation, debridement, and vascular/soft-tissue repair occur in the operating room, so the crew does not attempt deep cleaning or closure.1,3 Tetanus status is addressed at the receiving facility. Amputated parts are preserved per the standard method (saline-damp gauze, sealed bag, on ice water — never frozen; TS-TR-004) in case replantation is feasible. The combination of early hemorrhage control, blood-based resuscitation, marine-spectrum antibiotics, and rapid transport to definitive surgical care is what converts a historically lethal injury into a survivable one.1,2
