Seizures / Status Epilepticus

Neurocritical care
TS-NEU-004 · Sec 6 Neurology
Immediate
  • Check bedside glucose — treat hypoglycemia straightaway.
  • Protect airway, O₂, position; identify a likely cause (lesion, med non-adherence, OD, tox, metabolic).
  • Give a benzodiazepine every 2–3 min until seizures stop. IV preferred; lorazepam IV is preferred, midazolam IV is an acceptable alternative.
  • Eclampsia seizures → manage per the eclampsia guideline.
First-line — benzodiazepines (repeat q2–3 min)
Lorazepam preferred with IV/IO access
Adult 4 mg IV / IM / NAS · Peds 0.1 mg/kg IV/IM/NAS (max 4 mg)
Midazolam preferred for IM / NAS route
Adult 0.1 mg/kg IV (max 5 mg) or 0.2 mg/kg IM/NAS (max 10 mg) · Peds: refer to adult dosing
Refractory / ongoing control — choose one (in order)
Levetiracetam
Adult 4 g IV over 15 min · Peds 60 mg/kg IV over 15 min (max 4 g)
Fosphenytoin
15 mg PE/kg IV — adult ≤ 150 mg PE/min; peds ≤ 3 mg PE/kg/min (max 150). Via pump.
Phenytoin
15 mg/kg IV — adult ≤ 50 mg/min (max 1 g); peds ≤ 1 mg/kg/min (max 50). Via pump.
Propofol, phenobarbital, or benzodiazepine infusions for status epilepticus → direct medical control only.
Cautions & red flags
  • Paralytics (NMBAs) do NOT treat seizures — they only mask motor activity; ongoing status continues. Not a treatment for status epilepticus.
  • Always correct hypoglycemia first.
  • Treat the underlying cause (tox, metabolic, lesion) alongside benzodiazepines.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Seizure type, onset/duration, and suspected cause.
  • Glucose; benzodiazepine(s) given — drug, dose, route, times, response.
  • Any second-line agent (drug, dose, rate) + medical-control orders for infusions.
  • Airway interventions, post-ictal exam, recurrence.
  • Why rotor-wing / level of care.
Overview

Seizures and status epilepticus require prompt evaluation and treatment. Therapy is guided by the likely cause — brain lesion, medication non-adherence, overdose, toxic exposure, or metabolic disorder. Hypoglycemia must be checked and corrected immediately, and eclamptic seizures are managed on the eclampsia pathway.

Management — detail

Benzodiazepines are first-line, repeated every 2–3 minutes until the seizure is controlled; the IV route is preferred (lorazepam preferred, midazolam an acceptable IV alternative), with IM/NAS midazolam when IV access is unavailable. For refractory seizures, status epilepticus, or ongoing control, add one anticonvulsant in order of preference — levetiracetam, fosphenytoin, or phenytoin — administered via infusion pump at the rate limits above. Propofol, phenobarbital, or benzodiazepine infusions are used only under direct medical control. Neuromuscular blocking agents mask motor manifestations but do not treat the underlying seizure and must not be used to treat status epilepticus.
