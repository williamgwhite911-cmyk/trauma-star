Seizures / Status Epilepticus

Neurocritical care
TS-NEU-004 · Sec 6 Neurology
Immediate
  • Check bedside glucose — treat hypoglycemia straightaway.
  • Protect airway, O₂, position; identify a likely cause (lesion, med non-adherence, OD, tox, metabolic).
  • Give a benzodiazepine every 2–3 min until seizures stop. IV preferred; lorazepam IV is preferred, midazolam IV is an acceptable alternative.
  • Eclampsia seizures → manage per the eclampsia guideline.
First-line — benzodiazepines (repeat q2–3 min)

Adult 0.1 mg/kg IV (max 5 mg) or 0.2 mg/kg IM/NAS (max 10 mg) · Peds: refer to adult dosing

Levetiracetam
Adult 4 g IV over 15 min · Peds 60 mg/kg IV over 15 min (max 4 g)
Fosphenytoin

Phenytoin
15 mg/kg IV — adult ≤ 50 mg/min (max 1 g); peds ≤ 1 mg/kg/min (max 50). Via pump.
Propofol, phenobarbital, or benzodiazepine infusions for status epilepticus → direct medical control only.
Cautions & red flags
  • Paralytics (NMBAs) do NOT treat seizures — they only mask motor activity; ongoing status continues. Not a treatment for status epilepticus.
  • Always correct hypoglycemia first.
  • Treat the underlying cause (tox, metabolic, lesion) alongside benzodiazepines.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Seizure type, onset/duration, and suspected cause.
  • Glucose; benzodiazepine(s) given — drug, dose, route, times, response.
  • Any second-line agent (drug, dose, rate) + medical-control orders for infusions.
  • Airway interventions, post-ictal exam, recurrence.
  • Why rotor-wing / level of care.
Overview & definitions

A generalized convulsive seizure becomes status epilepticus when it lasts ≥ 5 minutes or when seizures recur without recovery of consciousness between them. The 5-minute operational definition deliberately replaced older 30-minute definitions because most self-limited seizures stop within 2–3 minutes, so a convulsion still going at 5 minutes is unlikely to stop on its own and should be treated as status.1,2 This matters in transport: the clock, not the appearance of the patient, drives escalation. Prolonged seizure activity progressively becomes harder to abort (pharmacoresistance develops as GABA receptors are internalized) and begins to cause neuronal injury independent of any systemic complications, so early and adequately-dosed treatment is the single most important determinant of outcome.1,2,3

Identify and treat reversible causes first

Seizure is a symptom, not a diagnosis, and several causes are immediately reversible in the field. Hypoglycemia must be checked and corrected at once — it is a common, rapidly fatal, and fully treatable cause that benzodiazepines alone will not fix.4 Consider and address other drivers: anticonvulsant non-adherence (the most common cause in known epileptics), toxic ingestions (including TCA and sympathomimetics — see Toxicology protocols), hyponatremia and other metabolic derangements, hypoxia, CNS infection, and structural lesions or hemorrhage. In a pregnant or recently postpartum patient, a seizure is eclampsia until proven otherwise and is managed on the eclampsia pathway with magnesium, not the general status pathway.4 Documenting the suspected cause guides both field therapy and receiving-facility workup.

Why benzodiazepines first — and why dose matters

Benzodiazepines are unambiguously first-line. The RAMPART trial showed that for prehospital status epilepticus, IM midazolam is at least as effective as IV lorazepam and reaches the patient faster when IV access is not yet established, because the time saved not starting a line outweighs the slower absorption.3 The dominant real-world error is under-dosing — giving a timid first dose, waiting too long, and allowing the seizure to become refractory. Give a full weight-appropriate dose, then repeat every 2–3 minutes until the seizure stops. IV is preferred when access exists (lorazepam preferred; IV midazolam acceptable), with IM or intranasal midazolam when it does not.1,3 Respiratory depression is the main risk, so airway equipment and monitoring must be ready before dosing — but fear of respiratory depression should not lead to inadequate dosing, since uncontrolled status is the greater threat.1

Second-line anticonvulsants — the evidence for parity

When benzodiazepines fail to terminate the seizure, a second-line anticonvulsant is added by infusion. The ESETT trial compared levetiracetam, fosphenytoin, and valproate in benzodiazepine-refractory status and found them essentially equivalent in efficacy and safety — roughly half of patients stopped seizing with any of the three.5 This is why protocol allows a choice in order of preference (levetiracetam, then fosphenytoin/phenytoin), administered via pump at the specified rate limits; the choice can be driven by availability, IV compatibility, and patient comorbidity rather than a presumed efficacy difference. Propofol, phenobarbital, or continuous benzodiazepine infusions are reserved for refractory status under direct medical control, generally alongside definitive airway management.1,5

A critical pitfall — paralysis is not treatment
  • Neuromuscular blockade stops the movement, not the seizure. If a patient is intubated using a paralytic, electrical seizure activity continues in the brain and continues to injure it while the motor signs are masked.1
  • Paralytics must never be used to "treat" status epilepticus; if RSI is required for airway protection, continue aggressive anticonvulsant therapy and assume ongoing seizure until proven otherwise.1
  • Anticipate post-ictal hypoventilation and airway compromise; maintain SpO₂ ≥ 94%, support ventilation, and reassess glucose and temperature.
