Stroke — Undetermined

Type unknown — no field CT
TS-NEU-003 · Sec 6 Neurology
Ischemic Hemorrhagic Undetermined
The usual field situation
  • Clear stroke signs (BE-FAST +) but type cannot be determined without CT.
  • Get Last Known Well, glucose, anticoagulant/med list, LVO screen.
  • Watch for ICH clues (severe HA, vomiting, rapid ↓LOC, very high BP, herniation) — if present, manage on the Hemorrhagic pathway.
Blood pressure management (PHI)
Criteria to treat
Positive stroke assessment and finger-stick glucose ≥ 60 mg/dL → SBP goal < 185 mmHg. Manage with online medical direction where practicable.
Glucose first
Hypoglycemia mimics stroke — check & correct low glucose before attributing deficits to stroke.
Let CT define the type
Rapid transport to a CT / stroke-capable center — imaging then directs the ischemic vs hemorrhagic BP strategy.
Antihypertensives — run ONE agent to max before switching
  • Order of preference: titratable infusion first — Nicardipine or Clevidipine; then Labetalol (avoid if bradycardia, asthma/COPD, or heart block); Esmolol for rate control. Max one agent through its range before adding another.
Nicardipine
5 mg/hr IV, ↑ 2.5 mg/hr q5–15 min PRN, titrate, max 15 mg/hr (may ↓ to 3 mg/hr).
Clevidipine
1–2 mg/hr IV; may double q90 s, slow near goal; max 32 mg/hr.
Labetalol
20 mg IV over 2 min; repeat 40 mg in 10–20 min, then 80 mg q10–20 min (×3) PRN; or 2 mg/min infusion; max 300 mg.
Supportive care (safe for both types)
  • O₂ to keep SpO₂ ≥ 94%; protect airway; suction PRN.
  • HOB ~30° if ↓LOC/vomiting/ICP concern; otherwise supine as tolerated.
  • IV access; treat hypoglycemia; avoid hypotension, hypoxia, hyperthermia.
  • Pre-notify and pick destination by suspected LVO + time window.
Cautions & red flags
  • When in doubt, don't lower BP — get the order; expedite imaging.
  • Check glucose first — hypoglycemia mimics stroke.
  • Time is brain — LKW governs eligibility; don't delay transport.
  • Reassess continually; new ICH clues → Hemorrhagic pathway.
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Last Known Well + how determined; stroke scale + score; LVO screen.
  • Glucose; serial BP, GCS/neuro exam, pupils.
  • Anticoagulant/antiplatelet + med list, PMH.
  • Medical-control contact for any BP decision; destination rationale + pre-notification.
  • Interventions (O₂, glucose) and response.
  • Why rotor-wing / level of care (time-critical, distance to CT-capable center).
Overview — the default prehospital reality

In the field there is no CT scanner, and clinical features alone cannot reliably separate ischemic stroke from intracerebral hemorrhage — sensitivity and specificity of bedside judgment are poor, and even classic teaching (severe headache, vomiting, very high BP for ICH) misclassifies a large share of patients.1,2 This means the undifferentiated stroke is not an unusual case; it is the normal starting state for every stroke transport until imaging is obtained. Because ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke call for opposite blood-pressure strategies, the safest framework when the type is unknown is aggressive supportive care plus the fastest possible delivery to imaging, rather than committing to a BP strategy that could be exactly wrong.1,2,3

The core conflict — why we default to "don't lower"

In ischemic stroke, elevated blood pressure sustains collateral perfusion of the salvageable penumbra, so routine lowering is avoided (permissive hypertension).1 In intracerebral hemorrhage, elevated blood pressure drives hematoma expansion, so early controlled lowering is beneficial.4 Applying the ischemic strategy to a bleed, or the hemorrhagic strategy to an infarct, can each worsen outcome. When the type is genuinely undetermined, the asymmetry of harm guides the default: roughly seven of eight strokes are ischemic, and inappropriately dropping the pressure in an ischemic patient can extend the infarct across a pressure-passive watershed.1,3 Therefore the field default is to not lower BP unless it is extreme or there is clear herniation, and to let CT decide the pathway.1,2,4

What is safe to do for everyone, regardless of type

A large set of interventions help — or at least do not harm — in both stroke subtypes, and these define the undifferentiated bundle: maintain SpO₂ ≥ 94% with supplemental oxygen only if hypoxic; protect the airway and avoid hypercarbia in declining consciousness; treat hypoglycemia (a stroke mimic and a secondary insult); avoid hypotension and hypovolemia; and avoid hyperthermia.1,4 Establish IV access, perform serial neuro exams with a validated scale, document an accurate last-known-well, and obtain a focused medication history (especially anticoagulants/antiplatelets, which matter immediately if the CT shows blood).1,4 None of these require knowing the subtype, and all of them improve the patient's position once the diagnosis is made.

When the field must act before imaging

Two situations justify intervention before CT. First, extreme hypertension (e.g., SBP > 220) carries risk in either subtype, so cautious, titratable reduction under online medical control is reasonable while transport continues.2,4 Second, clinical herniation — declining GCS with new pupillary asymmetry, Cushing response (hypertension + bradycardia + irregular respirations), or posturing — is a neurosurgical emergency that overrides the BP debate and triggers the herniation pathway (airway control, head elevation, osmotic therapy considerations, and immediate receiving-team notification).4 Outside these, the highest-yield action remains speed to imaging.

In-flight considerations & destination
  • Prioritize speed to a CT/stroke-capable center (ideally one with both thrombectomy and neurosurgery); pre-notify so imaging and both pathways are ready.1,3
  • Continuous neuro and BP monitoring; escalate to online medical control before any BP agent.
  • Maintain oxygenation and perfusion, treat glucose, avoid hyperthermia, and gather the anticoagulation history en route.
  • From the Keys, rotor-wing transport collapses 3+ hours of single-highway ground time to imaging — the intervention that most reliably helps every stroke subtype.
