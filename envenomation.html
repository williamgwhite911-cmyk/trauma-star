Envenomation

Snake bite (pit viper) · scorpion sting
TS-TOX-004 · Sec 7 Toxicology
☎️
Poison Control (24/7)
1-800-222-1222 — antivenom guidance & species ID
General — all envenomations
  • Fully expose & examine the bite/sting — note edema, redness, puncture sites.
  • Mark the border of swelling with ink + time; remeasure to track progression.
  • Immobilize the affected extremity, positioned below heart level if practicable.
  • Do not bring the animal on transport.
Snake bite — Pit Viper
CroFab (Crotalidae Polyvalent Immune Fab)
Measure & monitor
Measure the limb circumference just proximal to the site every 15 min to track progression.
Adult dose
4–6 reconstituted vials in 250 mL 0.9% NS IV. Start 25–50 mL/hr ×10 min watching for allergic reaction; if none, increase to 250 mL/hr. (No pediatric standing orders.)
Scorpion sting — neurotoxic
Recognize
Flushing, diaphoresis, agitation, salivation, roving eye movements (opsoclonus), myoclonic limb jerking (not seizures).
Secretions — Atropine (one time)
Suction first. Adult 1 mg IV over 1 min / IM · Peds 0.02 mg/kg IV/IM (max 0.5 mg). Early advanced airway if secretions can't be controlled.
Clonus / sedation — Fentanyl (preferred)
0.5–2 mcg/kg IBW IV over 1 min / IM / NAS, repeat q10 min PRN.
Alternative — Midazolam
0.1 mg/kg IV (max 5 mg) or 0.2 mg/kg IM/NAS (max 10 mg).
Cautions & red flags
  • Snake bite: watch for allergic reaction during CroFab — slow start, then titrate up.
  • Scorpion: avoid long-acting opioids/benzos (hydromorphone, morphine, lorazepam, diazepam) and anticholinergics (diphenhydramine).
  • No tourniquets / incision / suction on snake bites; remove constrictive items (rings).
Documentation — medically necessary
  • Bite/sting site, time, species if known; swelling border marks + serial circumference.
  • CroFab (vials, rate, reaction) or scorpion meds (atropine/fentanyl/midazolam — dose/time/response).
  • Airway/secretion management; Poison Control contact.
  • Why rotor-wing / level of care.
Overview — two very different syndromes

This protocol covers two unrelated envenomation syndromes that share a need for early recognition and supportive transport. Pit viper (crotalid) envenomation — rattlesnakes, cottonmouths, copperheads — produces a primarily local and hematologic illness: progressive tissue injury at the bite, and a venom-induced coagulopathy (thrombocytopenia, hypofibrinogenemia, elevated INR) that can cause systemic bleeding.1 Neurotoxic scorpion envenomation (e.g., Centruroides) instead causes a neuro-excitatory storm — a mix of cholinergic excess (drooling, secretions, vomiting) and sympathetic surge (agitation, tachycardia, hypertension) with the hallmark roving eye movements and myoclonus/opsoclonus, most dangerous in small children.1,3 Because the venom load and the patient's response evolve over hours, the field role is serial assessment, supportive care, antivenom where appropriate, and rapid transport.

General field principles & what NOT to do

For every envenomation: expose and examine the site, remove constricting items (rings, watches) before swelling, mark the leading edge of swelling/erythema with the time so progression can be tracked objectively, immobilize the limb at roughly heart level, and minimize exertion.1 Several traditional "first aid" measures are contraindicated for North American pit viper bites because they concentrate venom and worsen local tissue loss or cause harm: arterial tourniquets, tight constriction bands, incision and suction, ice, and electrical therapy.1,2 Do not attempt to capture the snake — a photograph from a safe distance is sufficient, and identification rarely changes management since the same Fab antivenom covers North American crotalids. Establish IV access in an unaffected limb and obtain baseline vitals.

Pit viper bites — why antivenom, and the role of serial exams

The damage from crotalid venom is progressive: enzymes and toxins spread through tissue and continue to consume clotting factors and platelets for hours, so a bite that looks mild early can become limb- or life-threatening. Antivenom works by binding and neutralizing circulating venom, halting progression — earlier administration limits tissue loss and reverses coagulopathy.1,2 Crotalidae Polyvalent Immune Fab (CroFab) is dosed 4–6 vials in 250 mL NS, deliberately started slowly (25–50 mL/hr for the first 10 minutes) to detect an early allergic/anaphylactoid reaction before the rate is increased to 250 mL/hr.4 The objective marker for progression is limb circumference measured proximal to the bite every 15 minutes: an advancing margin signals ongoing envenomation and the need for additional antivenom. Watch for systemic venom effects — hypotension, bleeding, and airway/angioedema from facial or oral bites — and be prepared to treat anaphylaxis to the antivenom itself.1,4

Neurotoxic scorpion stings — supportive control of the storm

Scorpion neurotoxicity is largely a supportive-care problem: the threats are excessive secretions compromising the airway and severe agitation/myoclonus, particularly in children.1,3 Manage secretions with suction and a single dose of atropine (adult 1 mg; pediatric 0.02 mg/kg, max 0.5 mg) to dry them, and prepare for early advanced airway management if secretions or sedation threaten ventilation. Treat the neuromuscular agitation/clonus and provide sedation with fentanyl (preferred) or midazolam, titrated carefully. The protocol deliberately favors short-acting agents and avoids long-acting opioids/benzodiazepines and additional anticholinergics, because the syndrome is dynamic and over-sedation with a long-acting drug can outlast the venom effect and itself cause respiratory depression.1,3 Where available, scorpion-specific antivenom rapidly resolves severe pediatric neurotoxicity and is a receiving-facility decision; contact Poison Control (1-800-222-1222) for guidance on both syndromes.

Documentation & handoff anchors
  • Time of bite/sting, species if known (photo), and the timed swelling-margin marks with serial limb circumferences.
  • Antivenom name, vials, start time, infusion rate, and any reaction; atropine/sedation doses and response.
  • Bleeding, neuro/airway status trend; Poison Control contact; rationale for level of care / rotor-wing.
