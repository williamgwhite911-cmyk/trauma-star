Overview — a treatable killer if epi is given early

Anaphylaxis is a rapid, IgE-mediated systemic release of histamine and other mediators causing vasodilation, capillary leak, bronchospasm, and airway edema — a combination that can kill within minutes from airway obstruction or distributive shock.1,2 It is defined clinically by acute onset with skin/mucosal involvement plus respiratory or cardiovascular compromise, or by involvement of two or more organ systems, or by hypotension after a known allergen.2 The single most important fact in the whole protocol is that delayed epinephrine is associated with death and with biphasic reactions — so epinephrine is given first and fast, before antihistamines and steroids.1,2

Why epinephrine is first-line and IM

Epinephrine reverses every dangerous feature of anaphylaxis simultaneously: α-1 vasoconstriction raises blood pressure and reduces mucosal/airway edema, β-1 supports cardiac output, and β-2 relaxes bronchial smooth muscle and dampens further mediator release.1,2 No other drug does this. It is given intramuscularly into the anterolateral thigh because IM injection there produces faster, higher, and more reliable peak levels than subcutaneous or deltoid injection, and it avoids the arrhythmia/hypertension risk of IV bolus epinephrine in a perfusing patient.2 Dosing is repeated every ~5–20 minutes for ongoing symptoms; a patient who needs repeated doses or remains hypotensive is a candidate for an epinephrine infusion under medical control. Antihistamines and steroids are explicitly second-line adjuncts — they treat skin symptoms and may reduce biphasic reactions but do nothing for airway or shock, and must never delay epinephrine.1,2

Fluids, airway, and the bronchospasm adjunct

The distributive shock of anaphylaxis comes from massive vasodilation and capillary leak, so aggressive IV crystalloid (20 mL/kg boluses) is given alongside epinephrine for hypotension.2 Because airway edema can progress rapidly to complete obstruction, the crew prepares for a difficult airway early — voice change, stridor, or tongue/lip swelling are warning signs to secure the airway before it closes, anticipating that swelling can make later intubation impossible.2 Inhaled albuterol is added for bronchospasm that persists after epinephrine, treating the lower-airway component the same way as asthma.2

The beta-blocker trap — glucagon

A patient taking a beta-blocker may be refractory to epinephrine, because the β-receptors epinephrine needs to act on are blocked — leaving unopposed α effects and persistent hypotension/bronchospasm.1,2 Glucagon bypasses the β-receptor entirely, activating cardiac adenylate cyclase through its own receptor to restore inotropy, chronotropy, and vascular tone independent of β-blockade. This is why the protocol carries a specific glucagon pathway for the beta-blocked, epinephrine-refractory patient (with anti-emetic readiness, since glucagon commonly causes vomiting).2

Biphasic reactions & severity-based treatment

Anaphylaxis is stratified by severity: skin-only reactions are treated as mild/moderate with antihistamine and steroid, while any airway, cardiovascular, or multi-system involvement is severe and gets epinephrine.1,2 A crucial transport caveat is the biphasic reaction — symptoms can recur hours after apparent resolution, even without re-exposure — so a patient who responds to treatment is still monitored continuously, kept on the monitor, and the crew remains ready to re-dose epinephrine. Steroids are given partly to blunt this late phase. Documentation of the trigger, the systems involved, and the timing of epinephrine matters both clinically and for the receiving team's observation period.2
