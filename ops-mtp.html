Massive Transfusion Role Card

Whole blood · 2-person choreography
TS-OPS-004 · Sec 4 Operations & Transport
Trigger / activate
  • Hemorrhagic shock: SBP < 90, HR ≥ 110, shock index ≥ 0.8, class III–IV, or major ongoing loss (TS-HD-002). Blocked HR (β/CCB) → trust SI/perfusion.
  • Call it out: "Massive transfusion — go." Assign roles; pre-notify receiving + activate their MTP.
Split the work
Simultaneous, not sequential
P1 · Stop the bleeding + airway Tourniquet / wound packing / pelvic binder / splint (TS-TR-001); airway & oxygenation; chest decompression if needed.
P2 · Give the blood Two large-bore IV/IO; verify, warm, spike, infuse blood; push calcium & TXA; monitor & time.
P2 — blood sequence
Run it right
P2 Two-person verify unit (LTOWB, group O, unit/segment #, ABO/Rh, expiry, integrity). Warm ≤ 42 °C. NaCl-only line — NEVER LR or calcium in the same line.
P2 Infuse via warmer ± pressure bag; titrate to radial pulse / SBP ~90 (100–110 if TBI). Adult 1 unit / peds 10–20 mL/kg; repeat.
The "with-blood" bundle — don't forget
Calcium with the 1st unit: CaCl₂ 1 g (peds 20 mg/kg) or Ca gluconate 3 g (peds 60 mg/kg) IV/IO.
TXA < 3 h: ≥ 12 yr 2 g over 10 min · < 12 yr 15 mg/kg (max 1 g) + 2 mg/kg/hr.
Warm everything (patient + product); permissive hypotension; pelvic binder; minimize crystalloid.
Reassess & handoff
  • Reassess perfusion (pulse, mentation, EtCO₂) after each unit; watch for transfusion reaction → stop if suspected.
  • Track units, calcium doses, TXA time; relay running totals to the receiving trauma team.
  • Load-and-go — the patient needs the OR; the blood is a bridge.
Documentation
  • Trigger (SI/vitals), hemorrhage-control devices + times, unit #s, two-person verify.
  • Calcium, TXA (time), warming, responses; MTP activation & destination.
Overview — speed + sequence win the bleeding patient

Massive hemorrhage is a race the crew wins by doing several things at once, which is exactly what a two-person team struggles to do without a script.1,2 This role card splits the work so that hemorrhage control and airway (P1) happen in parallel with getting blood into the patient (P2), rather than one provider trying to do both serially. The clinical content lives in the whole-blood protocol (TS-HD-002); this is the choreography that makes it fast and error-free.

Recognize early — including the blunted patient

Outcomes track how early balanced resuscitation starts, so the team activates on objective triggers — SBP < 90, HR ≥ 110, and especially shock index ≥ 0.8, which detects occult hemorrhage before the systolic pressure falls.1,3 A key trap the card flags is the patient on beta- or calcium-channel blockers (or hypothermic) who cannot mount a tachycardia: the heart rate and shock index are falsely reassuring, so the crew transfuses on perfusion and suspicion, not on a "normal" pulse.3 Calling "massive transfusion — go" out loud and activating the receiving MTP turns individual effort into a coordinated response.

The blood sequence & the safety steps that get skipped

Whole blood replaces what is lost in physiologic proportion, but the procedure has safety steps that are easy to drop under pressure and are therefore assigned explicitly to P2: a two-person unit verification (catastrophic if wrong), warming (cold blood worsens the coagulopathy it is meant to fix), and a normal-saline-only line — LR or calcium in the same line clots citrated blood.2 Resuscitation is titrated to permissive hypotension (radial pulse / SBP ~90, higher with TBI) to avoid popping the clot, and crystalloid is minimized.1,2

The "with-blood" bundle — calcium, TXA, warmth

Three adjuncts must travel with the blood and are the items most often forgotten by a busy two-person crew, so the card hard-codes them: calcium with the first unit (citrate chelates ionized calcium, which is essential to clotting and cardiac function), TXA within 3 hours (reduces death from bleeding; pediatric weight-based with a 1 g cap), and aggressive warming of both patient and product to defeat the hypothermia corner of the lethal triad.2,3 Treating the lethal diamond — coagulopathy, acidosis, hypothermia, hypocalcemia — is as important as the volume itself.

Reassess, account, and go

After each unit the team reassesses perfusion (pulse, mentation, end-tidal CO₂) and watches for a transfusion reaction, while keeping a running tally of units, calcium doses, and the TXA time to hand to the receiving trauma team — accurate accounting changes hospital decisions.2 Above all, the card reinforces that blood is a bridge, not a destination: the bleeding patient needs a surgeon, so the crew minimizes scene time and load-and-goes to the highest-level trauma center with the MTP activated. From the Keys, the rotor-wing is what makes that timeline survivable.1,2
